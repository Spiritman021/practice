Number Series Patterns

A complete, structured reference of every pattern type tested in Bank & Insurance competitive exams — Missing, Wrong, and Multiple series with real examples.

3 Major Types
30+ Unique Patterns

Type 1

🔍 Missing Number Series

One number is missing. Identify the pattern and calculate the missing term.

Type 2

❌ Wrong Number Series

One number breaks the pattern. Find the incorrect term and what it should be.

🔀 Multiple Pattern Series

Two or more series are given. Decode the first, apply to others to find P, Q, R.

Missing Number Series
Foundational sub-types — each with a distinct mathematical structure
1a
Single Stage Difference
Arithmetic / constant change
Each consecutive term differs by a value that itself follows a fixed AP pattern.
Example — Differences form an AP
10+515+1025+1540+2060+2585
→Differences: +5, +10, +15, +20, +25 (AP with d=5)
+d, +2d, +3d…Arithmetic Progression
1b
Difference of Squares (+n²)
Add 2², 3², 4², 5²…
Each step adds the square of consecutive natural/odd/even numbers. Variants include +(n²+n) or +(n²−n).
Variant A — Add n²
4+48+917+1633+2558+3694
Variant B — Add (n²+n)
50+2²−153+4²−267+6²−3100+8²−4160
Variant C — Squares of odd numbers only (+7², +9², +11²…)
36+7²+186+9²+1168+11²+1290
+n²+n²±nn=2,3,4…
💡Also watch for adding squares of consecutive ODD (7,9,11…) or EVEN numbers.
1c
Difference of Cubes (+n³)
Add 1³, 2³, 3³ or alternate ²/³
Steps are cubes of natural numbers — or alternating squares and cubes for mixed series.
Example — Alternating n² and n³
8+1²+110+2³+220+3²+130+4³+296+5²+1122+6³+2340
Example — nᵏ − n pattern

+n³+(nᵏ−n)Alternate ²/³

Odd and even positions form separate series

Identify the one term that breaks the pattern — and state what it should be

Exam Strategy & Tips
Proven approaches to crack number series under time pressure
🔎

Start Right-to-Left

To identify multiplication patterns, check the ratio from the last term backwards — easier to spot the multiplier going right-to-left first, then confirm left-to-right.

📉

Decreasing-then-Increasing

If terms first decrease then increase (or vice versa), immediately check for ×0.5 ± n or ×1 − n patterns. Very common in Mains alternating series.

🧩

Multiple Series: Jump to Required Term

Once Series I's pattern is cracked, don't compute every term of Series II. Jump directly to the requested position using the same logic.

✂️

Two Hidden Sub-series

When no single rule fits, immediately split into alternate terms: 1st, 3rd, 5th form one series; 2nd, 4th, 6th form another.

⚡

Check Differences First

Always compute all consecutive differences first. If those differences are squares, cubes, primes, or an AP — you've found the pattern in seconds.

🎯

Approximate for Large Multiplications

For doubling/tripling series with large values, use approximations first to identify the required term's position. Exact calculation only when needed.

🔢

Wrong Series: Test from Both Ends

Start from the first term moving right, and simultaneously verify from the last term moving left. The bad term is where both directions break — usually term 3 or 4.

📌

Memorize Prime Squares

Know squares of primes up to 37: 4, 9, 25, 49, 121, 169, 289, 361, 529, 625, 841, 961, 1369. These appear as differences in advanced series.

🔁

Fraction Multipliers

If a series has non-integer terms, try ×½+c, ×3/2+c patterns. Also check ×0.5±n. The addend often stays constant while the multiplier alternates.
