Basic Identity & OSI Layer

A repeater is one of the simplest networking devices — it is pure hardware with no software of any kind. It operates exclusively at the Physical Layer (Layer 1) of the OSI model.

PropertyValue

Number of ports2 — it is a two-port device

Exam importanceHigh for UGC NET & PSUs; low for GATE
🔑 Key Point: Because a repeater works only at Layer 1, it has zero awareness of MAC addresses (Layer 2) or IP addresses (Layer 3). It simply sees raw bits and electrical voltages — nothing more.
2

The Need for Repeaters — Overcoming Attenuation

Every cable has a maximum distance limit beyond which the signal becomes too weak (attenuated) to be reliably decoded. A repeater is placed at or before this limit to catch the dying signal and send it out fresh.

📈 Signal Attenuation & Repeater Regeneration — 10Base2 Example (200 m segments)
The repeater catches the signal at exactly the 200 m mark (before it becomes unusable), regenerates it to the original strength, and retransmits it — effectively doubling the total range to 400 m.
💡 Formula: Total range with a repeater = max cable distance × number of segments. With one repeater on a 10Base2 cable: 200 m × 2 = 400 m. With two repeaters: 200 × 3 = 600 m, and so on.
3

Repeater vs Amplifier — A Critical Distinction

A very common exam trap: repeaters and amplifiers both boost a signal, but they work in completely different ways and produce different results.

🔊 Amplifier

Takes a signal at strength x
Boosts it to 2x, 4x, or more of original strength
Also amplifies any noise on the signal — distortion grows
Does not check or clean the signal — just makes it louder
Used in analog audio/video systems
✕ Noise/corruption is amplified along with the signal

🔁 Repeater

Takes a weakened signal (less than x)
Reads each bit and restores it to exactly x (original voltage)
Noise is removed — a clean signal is retransmitted
Processes signal bit by bit — regenerative, not just amplifying
Used specifically in digital networks
✓ Only the original data strength is restored; noise is discarded
📊 Signal Strength Comparison — Amplifier vs Repeater
An amplifier overshoots the original strength and drags noise along. A repeater precisely restores the signal to its original level and discards noise.
4

Key Functionalities — Forwarding, Filtering & Collision Domain

📤
Forwarding
A repeater always forwards a signal. When a signal arrives on Port 1, the repeater regenerates it and immediately sends it out from Port 2, and vice versa. There is no intelligence involved — it is unconditional and automatic.
✓ YES — Repeater CAN forward
🚫
Filtering
A repeater cannot filter any data. Suppose Device A wants to send data to Device B, and both A and B are on the same side (same segment) of the repeater. The repeater will still forward the signal to the other side — wasting bandwidth. It has no software and no knowledge of where any device is.
✕ NO — Repeater CANNOT filter
💥
Collision Domain
All devices on both sides of a repeater belong to a single collision domain. The maximum number of collisions equals n (the total number of connected devices across both segments). This happens because the repeater has no buffer or memory — if multiple signals arrive simultaneously they simply collide.
⚠ Collision domain = n (all connected devices)

IDENTITY & BASICS
OSI Layer & Type
  • Layer: Physical Layer (L1) ONLY
  • Type: Pure Hardware — zero software
  • Ports: 2 (two-port device)
  • Cannot read MAC or IP addresses
  • Exam: Important for UGC NET & PSUs; not for GATE
Why Repeaters Are Used
  • Signal weakens over distance = Attenuation
  • Repeater placed at the cable's max distance limit
  • Regenerates signal back to original strength
  • Doubles (or multiplies) total range
  • e.g. 10Base2 (200 m) + 1 repeater = 400 m
REPEATER vs AMPLIFIER
🔊 Amplifier
  • Signal strength: x → 2x / 4x (overshoots)
  • Amplifies noise along with the signal
  • Used in analog systems (audio, video)
  • Distortion can accumulate over hops
🔁 Repeater
  • Signal strength: x/2 → x (exactly original)
  • Removes noise — clean retransmission
  • Used in digital networks
  • Reads bit by bit and regenerates precisely
Key Difference (1-liner)
  • Amplifier = boost indiscriminately (noise included)
  • Repeater = read, clean, regenerate to exact original
  • NOT interchangeable — very common exam trap
FORWARDING, FILTERING & COLLISIONS
📤 Forwarding
  • YES — always and unconditionally
  • Signal in on P1 → regenerate → out on P2
  • No checks, no decisions — pure pass-through
🚫 Filtering
  • NO — cannot filter any traffic
  • No software = cannot know device locations
  • If A→B and both on same side, repeater still forwards to other side (waste)
💥 Collision Domain
  • Creates ONE single collision domain
  • All devices on both sides = same domain
  • No buffer/memory → simultaneous signals collide
  • Max collisions = n (total devices connected)
