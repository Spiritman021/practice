TYPES OF NETWORKS

PAN, LAN, CAN, MAN, and WAN — classified by distance/range. Understand their technologies, speeds, ownership, and costs from a single room to the entire globe.

BASIS

Fundamental Basis of Classification

All five types of computer networks are primarily classified on one single criterion: Distance or Range — how far the network can physically extend. As the range increases, the complexity, cost, maintenance difficulty, error rate, and ownership model all change accordingly.

📐 Key Rule: The basis of comparison between PAN, LAN, CAN, MAN, and WAN is always distance/range. Everything else — speed, cost, ownership, error rate — follows from this.

PAN

Personal Area Network

A PAN is the smallest type of network, designed for use by a single individual within a very short range — typically inside a single room. The range extends from 1 metre up to a maximum of about 100 metres.

The technologies used are wireless (Bluetooth, Infrared) or wired (USB). Common examples include connecting a mobile phone to a Bluetooth headset, connecting a phone to a laptop via mobile hotspot, or syncing devices via USB cable.

LAN

Local Area Network

A LAN covers a slightly larger area — typically up to 1 to 2 kilometres — and is the most commonly encountered network type in everyday life. It is used in homes, schools, and small offices where multiple devices need to share resources like internet access, printers, or files.

The dominant technology today is Ethernet (wired) and Wi-Fi (wireless). Historically, technologies like Token Ring were used, but Ethernet has completely replaced them as the standard.

CAN

Campus Area Network

A CAN is slightly larger than a standard LAN and is designed to connect multiple buildings within a geographically bounded area such as a university campus (IITs, Stanford) or a large corporate tech park (like a Microsoft or Google campus). Range is typically 1 to 5 kilometres.

Think of a CAN as multiple LANs stitched together. Since multiple buildings are now involved, more networking infrastructure (routers, switches, cabling between buildings) is required, which raises the cost and maintenance effort above what a LAN needs.

MAN

Metropolitan Area Network

A MAN spans a range of 5 to 50 kilometres and is designed to connect multiple LANs and CANs across an entire metro city — such as Mumbai, Kolkata, or Chennai. Cable TV networks and city-wide broadband infrastructure are classic examples.

Due to the large distances involved, high-capacity fibre-optic technologies are used. Setting up a MAN requires installing devices and transmission equipment at regular intervals across the city, making maintenance complex and expensive.

WAN

Wide Area Network

A WAN is the largest type of network, spanning across countries and continents. The most well-known example is the Internet itself (also called the World Wide Web). WANs connect MANs, CANs, and LANs across the globe.

WAN infrastructure is provided by major telecommunication companies (ISPs like Airtel, Jio, AT&T) using leased lines and historically dial-up connections. Because of the massive scale — billions of users, countless devices, intercontinental cables — maintenance is extraordinarily complex and the error rate and cost are the highest of all network types.

ANALOGY

Historical Analogy — Telephone Networks

The scale and concept of modern computer networks were heavily inspired by old telephone networks of the 1980s and 1990s. The three telephone call types map perfectly to three network types — making this a memorable and exam-friendly analogy.

📞
PCO — Local Call
≈ LAN
Calls within the same city — short local range
📟
STD — Inter-city Call
≈ MAN
State-to-state calls — larger metro/regional range
🌐
ISD — International Call
≈ WAN
Cross-country international calls — global range
🧠 Memory Hook: PCO = LAN · STD = MAN · ISD = WAN. This analogy is often directly tested in UGC NET MCQs.

Per-Network Quick Facts
PAN — Personal Area
  • Range: 1 m – 100 m
  • Tech: Bluetooth · IR · USB
  • Ownership: Private (one person)
  • Speed / Error / Cost: Very High / Very Low / Very Low
  • Example: Phone ↔ BT headset, hotspot
LAN — Local Area
  • Range: Up to 1–2 km
  • Tech: Ethernet (802.3) · Wi-Fi (802.11)
  • Ownership: Private
  • Speed / Error / Cost: High / Low / Low
  • Historical tech: Token Ring (now obsolete)
CAN — Campus Area
  • Range: 1 – 5 km
  • Multiple buildings on same campus
  • Examples: IIT, Stanford, Microsoft campus
  • Ownership: Private (university / company)
  • Everything moderate: cost, error, maintenance
MAN — Metropolitan Area
  • Range: 5 – 50 km
  • Tech: FDDI · ATM
  • Ownership: Private or Public
  • Speed / Error / Cost: Moderate / High / High
  • Analogy: STD call (inter-city phone)
WAN — Wide Area
  • Range: Countries / Continents
  • Tech: Leased Lines · Dial-up
  • Ownership: Mostly Public
  • Speed / Error / Cost: Low / Very High / Very High
  • Analogy: ISD call · Example: Internet
Telephone Analogy
  • PCO (local call) = LAN
  • STD (inter-city call) = MAN
  • ISD (international call) = WAN
  • Inspired by 1980s–90s phone networks

⚡ Last-Minute Must-Know Points

🔥 High-Yield Facts
  • Basis of classification = Distance / Range
  • Internet = biggest example of WAN
  • LAN tech = Ethernet (802.3) + Wi-Fi (802.11)
  • MAN tech = FDDI + ATM (memorise full forms)
  • PAN = no extra devices, just inbuilt hardware
📌 Ownership Pattern
  • PAN, LAN, CAN = always Private
  • MAN = Private OR Public
  • WAN = Mostly Public (Internet)
  • As range ↑, ownership shifts toward public
💡 Trend Rule
  • Range ↑ → Speed ↓
  • Range ↑ → Error Rate ↑
  • Range ↑ → Cost ↑
  • Range ↑ → Maintenance Difficulty ↑
  • PAN→LAN→CAN→MAN→WAN follows this strictly
