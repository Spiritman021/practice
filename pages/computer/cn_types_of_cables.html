TYPES OF CABLES IN COMPUTER NETWORKS

UTP, Coaxial & Fibre Optic cables — naming conventions (10BaseT, 100BaseFX), signal types, distance limits, attenuation, baseband vs broadband, and every exam angle for GATE / UGC NET / PSUs.

GATE UGC NET PSUs B.Tech / BCA
1

Role of Cables & OSI Layer

Cables are the physical medium that connect networking devices and carry data in the form of signals. They are the most fundamental component of any wired network. Cables are primarily used in:

PropertyDetails
OSI LayerLayer 1 — Physical Layer only
TypePure Hardware (zero software)
IntelligenceNone — cannot check IP, MAC, or filter data
Sole purposeProvide a physical connection and carry signals between devices
Used inEthernet LANs (< 10 km), Token Ring, Token Bus networks
🔑 Key Exam Point: Cables are pure hardware — they have absolutely no software. They cannot check IP addresses, MAC addresses, or filter signals. They simply provide a physical path. All decisions are made by intelligent devices (switches, routers) connected to the cables.
2

Decoding Cable Naming Conventions

Cable names like 10BaseT, 10Base2, 100BaseFX follow a strict 3-part format. This is a very common question in UGC NET and PSU exams. Understanding each part decodes the cable's complete specification.

🔎 Cable Name Decoder — Reading “100 Base FX”
Part 1
100
Bandwidth / Speed
Transmission rate in Mbps. This cable can carry 100 megabits of data per second.
Part 2
Base
Transmission Type
Base = Baseband (one signal at a time). Broad = Broadband (multiple parallel signals).
Part 3
FX
Distance / Medium
T = 100 m (Twisted Pair) • 2 = 200 m • 5 = 500 m • FX = Fibre Channel (~2 km)
The three parts always follow the same order: [Speed in Mbps] — [Base or Broad] — [Distance/Medium code]

The number after “Base” is a code, not always exact: T = 100 m, 2 ≈ 200 m (actually 185 m), 5 = 500 m, FX = Fibre Channel.
3

Baseband vs Broadband Transmission

▶ Baseband (Base)

📌 Only one signal can travel through the cable at a time
📌 If the sender is transmitting, the receiver cannot send simultaneously
📌 If multiple devices send at the exact same time → collision occurs
📌 Uses the entire bandwidth of the cable for a single signal
📌 All three cable types (UTP, Coaxial, Fibre) discussed here are Baseband
📌 Example: 10BaseT, 100BaseFX

🔁 Broadband (Broad)

📌 Multiple signals can travel simultaneously in parallel
📌 Each signal uses a different frequency channel — no interference
📌 Much higher effective throughput
📌 Used in cable TV (CATV) and modern internet connections
📌 Signals are modulated (carrier wave) — requires more complex hardware
📌 Example: Cable TV coaxial infrastructure
⚠️ Collision Formula (Exam): If n devices are all connected to the same baseband cable and all transmit simultaneously, there can be a maximum of n collisions. This happens because the cable is pure hardware — it cannot detect, stop, or filter any incoming signal.

Three Types of Cables — In Detail

All three cables carry data as signals but differ in signal type, maximum distance, speed, and use case.

🔗
Unshielded Twisted Pair
UTP
SIGNAL Electrical signals
EXAMPLES 10BaseT, 100BaseT
DISTANCE 100 metres — the “T” code
STRUCTURE Pairs of copper wires twisted together to reduce EMI (electromagnetic interference)
USE CASE Most common office LAN cable (Cat 5e, Cat 6); plugs into RJ-45 connectors
LIMIT Signal degrades (attenuates) beyond 100 m; needs a repeater to extend
🖄
Coaxial Cable
COAX
SIGNAL Electrical signals
EXAMPLES 10Base2, 10Base5
DISTANCE 200 m (Base2) or 500 m (Base5)
STRUCTURE Central copper conductor → insulation → metal mesh shield → outer jacket. Shield reduces interference.
VARIANTS 10Base2 = Thin Ethernet (Thinnet) • 10Base5 = Thick Ethernet (Thicknet)
USE CASE Older Ethernet networks; still used in cable TV (CATV) and antenna connections
💡
Fibre Optic Cable
FIBRE
SIGNAL Light signals (NOT electrical)
EXAMPLES 100BaseFX, 1000BaseFX
DISTANCE ~2 km (1.9 km) before degradation
SPEED Travels at the speed of light (3 × 108 m/s) inside the glass core
STRUCTURE Glass or plastic core → cladding (reflects light inward) → protective jacket. Uses total internal reflection.
ADVANTAGES Immune to EMI; highest bandwidth; very low attenuation; secure (no EM radiation)

Attenuation — Why Signals Weaken Over Distance

Attenuation is the gradual loss of signal strength as it travels through a medium. Every cable has a maximum distance beyond which the signal becomes too weak to be reliably read by a receiving device. Beyond this point, a Repeater must be used to regenerate the signal.

💡 Why does fibre travel much farther? Light signals (fibre optic) suffer almost no energy loss due to heat/resistance like electrical signals do. Light bounces inside the glass core via total internal reflection with minimal loss, allowing it to travel ~2 km vs 100–500 m for copper cables.

CABLE BASICS
Role & OSI Layer
  • OSI Layer: Physical Layer (L1) only
  • Type: Pure Hardware — zero software
  • Cannot check IP, MAC, or filter data
  • Used in Ethernet LAN (< 10 km), Token Ring, Token Bus
Name Decoder: [Speed][Base/Broad][Code]
  • Part 1: Speed in Mbps (e.g., 10, 100, 1000)
  • Part 2: Base = one signal; Broad = multiple signals
  • Part 3 codes: T=100m • 2=200m • 5=500m • FX=~2km
  • Example: 100BaseFX = 100 Mbps, Baseband, Fibre ~2km
Baseband vs Broadband
  • Baseband: 1 signal at a time; collision if many send together
  • Broadband: multiple signals parallel; no collision
  • All 3 cable types (UTP, Coax, Fibre) = Baseband
  • Collision max = n if n devices send simultaneously
THREE CABLE TYPES
🔗 UTP (Twisted Pair)
  • Signal: Electrical
  • Examples: 10BaseT, 100BaseT
  • Max distance: 100 metres (code: T)
  • Connector: RJ-45
  • Cheapest; most common in offices (Cat 5e, Cat 6)
🖄 Coaxial Cable
  • Signal: Electrical
  • 10Base2 → max 200 m (Thin Ethernet)
  • 10Base5 → max 500 m (Thick Ethernet)
  • Connector: BNC
  • Better EMI shielding than UTP; used in CATV
💡 Fibre Optic Cable
  • Signal: Light (NOT electrical)
  • Examples: 100BaseFX, 1000BaseFX
  • Speed: 3 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light)
  • Max distance: ~2 km (1.9 km)
  • Immune to EMI; lowest attenuation; most expensive
KEY CONCEPTS
Attenuation
  • Signal loses strength as it travels through a medium
  • UTP: attenuates after 100 m
  • Coaxial: attenuates after 200 / 500 m
  • Fibre: attenuates after ~2 km
  • Fix: use a Repeater to regenerate the signal
Collisions on Cables
  • Cables are pure hardware — cannot stop signals
  • On Baseband: only 1 signal allowed at a time
  • If n devices send simultaneously → n collisions
  • Managed by CSMA/CD protocol (not the cable itself)
Why Fibre Goes Farther 💡
  • Electrical signals lose energy as heat in copper wires
  • Light bounces inside glass core via total internal reflection
  • Almost no energy loss → travels ~2 km vs 100–500 m
  • Also immune to EMI since no electric current flows
