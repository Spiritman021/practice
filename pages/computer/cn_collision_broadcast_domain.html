COLLISION & BROADCAST DOMAINS

What are Collision Domain & Broadcast Domain?

Before studying how each device affects these domains, you must clearly understand what domain means in networking — the territory or range over which a particular event (collision or broadcast) can affect devices.

💥 Collision Domain

📌 Definition: The set of all devices that can simultaneously transmit data and cause their signals to collide on the shared medium.
📌 A collision happens when two or more devices send data at the exact same time on the same shared medium.
📌 In the worst case, if there are N devices on the shared medium and all N transmit simultaneously, all messages collide — maximum collision domain = N.
📌 Devices in separate collision domains cannot collide with each other.
📌 Smaller collision domain = better performance.

📢 Broadcast Domain

📌 Definition: The set of all devices that receive a broadcast message sent by any one device in the network.
📌 Inside a LAN, by default, if device A sends a broadcast, all devices B, C, D… receive it — even if only A's intended recipient uses it; the rest discard it.
📌 A broadcast domain = N when all N devices receive the broadcast.
📌 Devices in different broadcast domains do not receive each other's broadcasts.
📌 Smaller broadcast domain = less unnecessary traffic.
💡 Key Distinction: Collision domain is about sending simultaneously (physical media sharing). Broadcast domain is about who receives a broadcast message. A device can reduce collisions without reducing broadcasts — or reduce both — depending on its layer.
2

Baseline — Shared Bus with N Devices (No Intermediate Device)

Imagine a simple bus topology where N devices (A, B, C, D…) all share a single wire — a shared medium. This is the starting point before any networking device is added.

📴 Shared Bus — N Devices, No Intermediate Device
All N devices share a single medium. If any two transmit at the same time, they collide. A broadcast from A reaches all N devices. Both domains = N.
📌 Base rule to remember: Without any intermediate networking device — Collision Domain = N (all devices can collide) and Broadcast Domain = N (all devices receive broadcast). Every networking device we study below is measured against this baseline.
3

Repeater — No Change in Either Domain

When you insert a repeater between two segments of the bus (cutting the wire and putting a repeater in the middle), you might expect some isolation — but there is none. The repeater's only job is to regenerate and boost the electrical signal. It has no buffer, no memory, no store-and-forward logic.

If A sends a message and E sends a message simultaneously, the repeater does not hold either — it just amplifies both. The signals still collide. The repeater treats both segments as one — there is no boundary.

🔁 Repeater Inserted — No Domain Boundary Created
A repeater just boosts signal strength. It has no intelligence to block or hold signals. If A and E transmit simultaneously, both signals pass through the repeater and collide.
📌 Why no change? A repeater operates at Layer 1 (Physical Layer). It has no buffer, no store-and-forward, no MAC table. It simply amplifies the signal. It cannot separate collision domains or stop broadcast frames.

Hub (Multiport Repeater) — No Change in Either Domain

A hub is simply a multiport repeater. Where a repeater has 2 ports, a hub has 4, 8, 16, or 24+ ports — but the logic is identical. It is a pure Layer 1 device with no software, no buffer, no MAC table.

When any device sends a signal into the hub, the hub broadcasts it out of every other port simultaneously. If multiple devices transmit at once, all their signals collide inside the hub. The hub cannot separate them or queue them.

💠 Hub — Still NO Domain Separation
All 4 devices share one collision domain through the hub. A broadcast from A reaches B, C, D. The hub has no intelligence to separate them.
📌 Same as repeater, just more ports: Hub = multiport repeater. The funda (principle) is the same — Layer 1, no buffer, no intelligence. Collision domain remains N. Broadcast domain remains N. No change in either domain.

Bridge — Reduces Collision Domain, NO Change in Broadcast

When you replace the repeater with a bridge, something important changes. A bridge is a Layer 2 device — it uses the store-and-forward technique. It has an internal buffer (memory). When a frame arrives on Port 1, the bridge stores it in its buffer and then forwards it to Port 2 based on the destination MAC address.

Why does this reduce collisions? If A sends a frame and simultaneously E sends a frame, the bridge stores A's frame in its buffer and E's frame separately. Because they are stored separately, they never collide at the bridge. The two segments are now independent collision domains.

Why does broadcast NOT change? A bridge operates at Layer 2 — the Data Link Layer. When a broadcast frame (destination = FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF) arrives at the bridge from Port 1, the bridge does not block it. It forwards the broadcast to Port 2 as well. The broadcast reaches all devices on both sides. Therefore the broadcast domain remains N.

🔃 Bridge — Splits Collision Domains, Passes All Broadcasts
Bridge creates 2 separate collision domains (A,B in one; E,F in another). But broadcasts from A still reach E and F — broadcast domain is unchanged at N.
✅ Why store-and-forward stops collisions: The bridge has a buffer. A's frame is stored in the buffer. E's frame arrives and is also stored separately. Now the bridge forwards A's frame to E's segment when the segment is free. The two frames never physically meet on the wire at the same time — hence no collision between segments.

Switch — Reduces Collision Domain (= 0), NO Change in Broadcast

A switch is a multiport bridge — it is the same fundamental device as a bridge, but with many ports. The logic is identical: Layer 2, store-and-forward, MAC address table, internal buffer.

Because every port on a switch is its own dedicated collision domain with a full-duplex link, collisions are effectively zero. Device A has its own private link to the switch and cannot collide with B, C, or D.

However, like the bridge, the switch is still a Layer 2 device. When a broadcast frame (FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF) arrives, the switch forwards it to all other ports. The broadcast domain is therefore not changed — all N devices still receive the broadcast.

💠 Switch — Each Port = Its Own Collision Domain, Broadcasts Still Flood All Ports
Each port of the switch is its own collision domain with a full-duplex link → 0 collisions. But broadcasts still go to all ports — broadcast domain unchanged.
📌 Switch vs Bridge — the only difference: Bridge has 2 ports (2 collision domains); Switch has N ports (N collision domains, all = 0 collisions). The broadcast domain behaviour is identical in both — neither can stop a Layer 2 broadcast.

7

Router — Reduces BOTH Collision Domain AND Broadcast Domain

A router is the most intelligent common networking device. It operates at Layer 3 (Network Layer) and uses IP addresses. A router connects two or more different networks (LANs).

Since the router is above the switch (Layer 3 vs Layer 2), it naturally inherits the collision isolation that a switch provides — collision domain is reduced.

But the crucial difference from a bridge/switch is the broadcast domain. A router is intelligent enough to read and understand IP broadcast addresses. By default, a router does NOT forward broadcasts from one network (LAN) to another. A broadcast from LAN-1 stays inside LAN-1 and does not reach LAN-2. This is why routers reduce the broadcast domain.

🌐 Router — Separate Collision AND Broadcast Domains Per LAN
Router separates both collision and broadcast domains. A broadcast from LAN-1 (A, B, C) stays within LAN-1 and never reaches LAN-2 (X, Y, Z). The router is the key device for broadcast domain isolation.
📌 Limited vs Direct Broadcast — The Router's Intelligence:
A router understands two types of broadcast:
• Limited broadcast (255.255.255.255) — stays inside the local network only. Router does NOT forward it.
• Direct broadcast (e.g., 192.168.1.255) — targeted at a specific subnet. Router can forward it to that specific network if configured.

8

Gateway — Reduces Both Domains (Works at Layer 4+)

A gateway operates above the router — typically at Layer 4 (Transport Layer) and above, up to Layer 7 (Application Layer). A gateway is used when two completely different technologies need to communicate — for example, an IP network connecting to a Fiber Channel or ATM network. The gateway acts as a translator between the two protocols.

Since a gateway is a higher-layer device, it inherits all the properties of lower-layer devices. Just as a car's top model includes everything from the base model plus extras — a gateway includes collision reduction AND broadcast reduction plus protocol translation.

📌 The "Base Model" Rule: Think of networking devices as car models. A hub (base model) has minimal features. As you go up — bridge → switch → router → gateway (top model) — each device includes everything the lower device does, plus additional capabilities. Collision domain reduces at bridge/switch level; broadcast domain reduces at router level; gateways do both plus protocol translation.
