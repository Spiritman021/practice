56

"sort", "type", "kind" → Always Singular Verb  ·  Plural forms → Plural Verb

sort / type / kind = singular verb · sorts / types / kinds = plural verb · Noun after "of" can be singular or plural

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 56): The words like "sort, type, kind, etc." are always followed by a Singular verb.
this/any/that + sort/type/kind + of + [noun] → Singular Verb (is/was/has…)
these/those/all + sorts/types/kinds + of + [noun] → Plural Verb (are/were/have…)
💡
Why singular?
The subject is "sort" / "type" / "kind" — not the noun after "of". Since "sort"/"type"/"kind" is singular, the verb is singular. "This type of products" → subject = "type" (singular) → verb = "is".
★ All Examples from Book & Class
Singular (sort/type/kind) → singular verb
✓
This type of article is sold in the market. [Correct]
"type" = singular → "is". ✓
✓
This sort of camera is very expensive. [Correct]
"sort" = singular → "is". ✓
✓
Any kind of racism is abhorrent to me. [Correct]
"kind" = singular → "is". ✓
Common exam mistake — class example
✗
This kind of products are available at his shop. [Incorrect]
Subject = "kind" (singular) → verb must be "is", not "are". ✗
✓
This kind of products is available at his shop. [Correct]
"kind" (singular) → "is". ✓
Plural (sorts/types/kinds) → plural verb
✓
These kinds of products are available at his shop. [Correct]
"kinds" = plural → "are". ✓
✓
All types of documents are required. [Correct]
"types" = plural → "are". ✓

📋 Rule 56 — Quick Recap

  • sort / type / kind (singular) → singular verb: "This type of article is…" ✓ | "Any kind of racism is…" ✓
  • sorts / types / kinds (plural) → plural verb: "These kinds of products are…" ✓
  • The noun after "of" can be singular or plural — only the article word (sort/type/kind) determines the verb
  • "This kind of products are…" ✗ → "This kind of products is…" ✓
57

"in" (static) · "inside" (movement in) · "into" (movement + change of state) · enter · inside out · upside down

in = already inside · inside = from outside to inside · into = coming toward + change of form/state · enter = no preposition needed

→ The Three Prepositions — in · inside · into
★ From the book (Rule 57): Use of the prepositions "in", "Inside" and "into":
(1) "inside" implies movement — whenever we move from outside to inside.
(2) "in" implies static — whenever something is already inside.
(3) "into" implies coming toward something — moving to a position inside something or showing a change in state.
in= Static · Already inside
Use when something/someone is already inside — no movement involved.

✓ "I am in the class."
✓ "The chlorine in the pool makes my eyes sore."
✓ "The book is in the bag."
inside= Movement (outside → inside)
Use when moving from outside to inside. No change of state.

✓ "I came inside the class."
✓ "He tried to get inside her mind."
✓ "Come inside the house."
into= Movement + Change of state
Use when (1) moving outside to inside OR (2) change of form/state.

✓ "Come into the house."
✓ "Water turns into ice." (change)
✓ "Translate into German." (change)
✓ "He jumped into the well."
✓ "The fruit can be made into jam."
✓ "She wove her ideas into a story."
in = already inside (static)  ·  inside = from outside to inside (movement)  ·  into = movement toward + change of form/state
All book examples for "into" — movement + change of state
✓
Come into the house. [Correct]
Movement from outside to inside → "into". ✓
✓
He threw the letter into the fire. [Correct]
Letter goes into fire + changes state (burns). ✓
✓
Can you translate this passage into German? [Correct]
Change of form (language changed). ✓
✓
The fruit can be made into jam. [Correct]
Change of form (fruit → jam). ✓
✓
He jumped into the well. [Correct]
Movement outside to inside + change of position/state. ✓
✓
She wove her ideas into a story. [Correct]
Change of form (ideas → story). ✓
✓
Water turns into ice. [Correct]
Change of state (liquid → solid). ✓
💡
inside vs into — when can BOTH be used?
When someone physically moves from outside to inside a place — BOTH "inside" and "into" are correct:
"I came inside the class." ✓ and "I came into the class." ✓
BUT for change of form/state — only "into" works, not "inside":
"Water turns into ice." ✓ | "Water turns inside ice." ✗
→ "enter" — No Preposition Needed · Exception: "enter into" (phrasal verb)
"Enter" already means "going from outside to inside" → never use any preposition after "enter" for physical movement.
"Enter into" is a separate phrasal verb meaning to sign/agree (an agreement/treaty).
✗
He entered in the house. [Incorrect]
"enter" already means going inside → no preposition needed. ✗
✓
He entered the house. [Correct]
"entered" alone is sufficient — no preposition. ✓
✗
He entered into the class. [Incorrect for physical entry]
Physical movement → "enter" alone is correct. ✗
✓
He entered into an agreement with Ram. [Correct — phrasal verb]
"Enter into" (phrasal verb) = to sign/agree something. ✓
⭐
Exception — "enter into" as phrasal verb:
When "enter into" refers to agreements, treaties, negotiations — it IS correct and means "to formally agree / sign".
"The two countries entered into a peace treaty." ✓
→ "Inside Out" and "Upside Down"
★ From the book: *NOTE: "Inside out" is completely different from "In" and "Inside". It means if something is inside out, it has the usual inside part on the outside and the usual outside part on the inside.
There is one more meaning of "Inside out" which is "completely".
inside out
Meaning 1: Something worn/placed reversed (उल्टा)
✓ "She wore the T-shirt inside out."
✓ "She had her sweater inside out."

Meaning 2: Completely (पूरी तरह से जानना)
✓ "I know my husband inside out." [completely]
✓ "I know you inside out."
upside down
Meaning: Toppled / turned over / inverted (उल्टा कर देना)

✓ "The bottle was turned upside down."
✓ "The painting is upside down."
✓ "Everything was turned upside down."

(Like turning a bottle so its bottom faces up)

📋 Rule 57 — Quick Recap

  • in = static (already inside): "I am in the class." ✓ | "chlorine in the pool" ✓
  • inside = movement from outside to inside: "I came inside the class." ✓
  • into = (1) movement from outside to inside OR (2) change of form/state: "translate into German" ✓ | "water turns into ice" ✓
  • enter = no preposition for physical movement: "He entered the house." ✓ | "He entered in the house." ✗
  • enter into = phrasal verb: to sign an agreement/treaty. "entered into an agreement" ✓
  • inside out = (1) reversed/worn backwards (2) completely. upside down = toppled/inverted
58

"with" for Non-Living Things · "by" for Living Things · Exceptions

with = non-living instrument/tool · by = living agent · Exception: "by" with watch/clock + time · walk on foot (not by foot)

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 58): Use of the prepositions "with" and "by":
(1) We use "with" when we refer to non living things.
(2) We use "by" when we refer to living things.
with → Non-Living Things
Use "with" when the instrument/tool is a non-living thing.

✓ "We write with a pen." [Pen = non-living]
✓ "She wiped her lipstick off with a tissue." [Tissue = non-living]
✓ "He hit the dog with a stick." [Stick = non-living]
by → Living Things
Use "by" when the agent/doer is a living thing.

✓ "The motorcycle was driven by a tiny bald man." [person = living]
✓ "The book was translated by a well-known author." [person = living]
✓ "Any painting by Van Gogh is worth a fortune." [person = living]
with → non-living instrument/tool  ·  by → living agent/person
→ Exceptions — "by" with Watch/Time · "on foot" (not "by foot")
★ From the book: *NOTE: Whenever we talk about "watch", we use "by".
"What is the time by your watch?" [In your watch is incorrect]
✗
What is the time in your watch? [Incorrect]
Watch uses "by" — not "in" or "with". ✗
✓
What is the time by your watch? [Correct]
"by" is always used with watch/clock. ✓
✗
We walk by foot. [Incorrect]
"by foot" is wrong — use "on foot". ✗
✓
We walk on foot. [Correct]
"on foot" is the correct expression. ✓
"by" with Time — deadline/limit meaning
✓
She had promised to be back by five o'clock. [Correct]
"by 5 o'clock" = before/at the latest 5 o'clock (time deadline). ✓
✓
I have got to be home by seven. [Correct]
"by seven" = at the latest by 7 (time deadline). ✓
💡
"by" + time = deadline:
When "by" is used with a time, it means "at the latest by that time" / "before or at that time". This is completely different from "by" with living things. "Back by 5" = at the very latest, 5 pm.

📋 Rule 58 — Quick Recap

  • with = non-living tool/instrument: "write with a pen" ✓ | "hit with a stick" ✓ | "wiped with a tissue" ✓
  • by = living agent/person: "driven by a man" ✓ | "translated by an author" ✓ | "painting by Van Gogh" ✓
  • Exception: Watch → always "by": "What is the time by your watch?" ✓ | "in your watch" ✗
  • Exception: "walk on foot" ✓ | "walk by foot" ✗
  • "by" + time = deadline: "back by 5 o'clock" = at the latest 5 pm ✓
