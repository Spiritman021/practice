21

Certain Words Never Take a Possessive Adjective

separation, excuse, mention, favour, pardon, leave, report, sight — never use my/your/his/her/its/our/their before these

→ What is a Possessive Adjective?

Adjective = a word that tells us about a noun or pronoun.
Possessive Adjective = an adjective that tells us about a noun/pronoun AND shows possession (किसका है).

my your his her its our their
★ From the book (Rule 21): Certain words like "separation, excuse, mention, favour, pardon, leave, report, sight" etc. do not take Possessive Case before them.
★ Words That NEVER Take a Possessive Adjective

Mark these words — you can NEVER put my / your / his / her / its / our / their before them:

separation excuse mention favour pardon leave report sight
✗   your/his/her/my + [special word]   is WRONG
✓   [special word] + of + pronoun   is CORRECT
💡
The Fix: Replace Possessive Adjective with word + of + pronoun/noun.
"your separation" ✗ → "separation from you" ✓
"his mention" ✗ → "the mention of him" ✓
"your favour" ✗ → "a favour from you" ✓
"I beg your pardon" ✗ → "I beg pardon of you" ✓
"at his sight" ✗ → "at the sight of him" ✓
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
FAVOUR
✗
I want your favour. [Incorrect]
"your" is a Possessive Adjective. Cannot use it before "favour". ✗
✓
I want a favour from you. [Correct]
"favour" + "from you" (preposition + pronoun). ✓
SEPARATION
✗
Your separation pains me a lot and I become sad. [Incorrect]
"your" is a Possessive Adjective. Cannot be placed before "separation". ✗
✓
Separation from you pains me a lot and I become sad. [Correct]
"separation" + "from you". ✓
MENTION
✗
His mention in the story received great applause. [Incorrect]
"his" is a Possessive Adjective. Cannot use before "mention". ✗
✓
The mention of him in the story received great applause. [Correct]
"mention" + "of him". ✓
SIGHT
✗
At his sight, the robbers fled. [Incorrect]
"his" is a Possessive Adjective. Cannot use before "sight". ✗
✓
At the sight of him, the robbers fled. [Correct]
"sight" + "of him". ✓
PARDON
✗
I beg your pardon. [Incorrect]
"your" is a Possessive Adjective. Cannot use before "pardon". ✗
✓
I beg pardon of you. [Correct]
"pardon" + "of you". ✓

📋 Rule 21 — Quick Recap

  • Words: separation, excuse, mention, favour, pardon, leave, report, sight — never take a Possessive Adjective (my/your/his/her/its/our/their) before them.
  • Instead, use: [word] + of/from + pronoun/noun
  • ✗ "Your separation" | ✓ "Separation from you"
  • ✗ "His mention" | ✓ "The mention of him"
  • ✗ "At his sight" | ✓ "At the sight of him"
  • ✗ "I beg your pardon" | ✓ "I beg pardon of you"
22

Each Other · One Another · Other · Another

Reciprocal pronouns + the difference between Other and Another

→ Reciprocal Pronouns: Each Other vs One Another

Reciprocal Pronouns are pronouns that show that two or more people are doing something to/for each other (आपस में). There are only two:

EACH OTHER — For Two People
  • Used when exactly two people are involved
  • "Peter and Mary helped each other."
  • Only two in the group → each other ✓
ONE ANOTHER — For More than Two
  • Used when more than two people are involved
  • "Citizens of a country should protect one another."
  • Group has many → one another ✓
★ From the book (Rule 22): We use "each other" and "one another" to show that each person in a group of two or more people does something to the others. There is very little difference between each other and one another. "Each other" is generally used for two people while "one another" is used for more than two people.
Examples
✗
All citizens should love each other. [Incorrect]
All citizens = many (more than 2) → one another should be used. ✗
✓
All citizens should love one another. [Correct]
More than 2 people → one another. ✓
✓
Peter and Mary helped each other. [Correct]
Exactly 2 people (Peter & Mary) → each other. [Peter helped Mary and Mary helped Peter] ✓
✓
We sent one another Christmas cards. [Correct]
"we" = more than 2 → one another. ["We sent them a Christmas card and they sent us a Christmas card"] ✓
Note: "We sent one other Christmas cards" is also correct as it shows two groups exchanging cards (each group = the other group).
→ Other vs Another — The Difference
OTHER — "The Second One" / "Different"
  • Meaning: दूसरा (the second of two items, or a different set)
  • Refers to the second group/person from a known pair or set
  • Plural: others
  • Used with singular or plural nouns
ANOTHER — "One More" / "Additional"
  • Meaning: एक और / अतिरिक्त (one more, additional)
  • Written as one word: "another" (an + other merged)
  • Shows something extra is added or requested
  • Can also mean 'an alternative' or 'different'
★ NOTE from book: Other is an adjective meaning "different" or "the second of two items". When we use 'an' before 'other', we write it as one word: another. It means 'additional' or 'one more'. Another can be used before a plural noun when that noun is preceded by a number or words/phrases such as a couple of, few etc.
Other — "दूसरा / second group" Examples
✓
Some children learn quickly but other children need more time.
Group 1: children who learn quickly. Group 2 (= other): children who need more time. ✓
✓
Have you got any other questions?
Different / additional questions (from the second set). ✓
✓
Only half of the guests have arrived. Where are the others? [Plural]
The other group (= second half) of guests. Others = plural form. ✓
Another — "एक और / additional" Examples
✓
This cake is delicious. Could I have another slice please?
एक और slice चाहिए → additional → another. ✓
✓
The investigation will take another two weeks. [Plural noun]
Another = additional + number + plural noun. ✓
✓
This place isn't great. Is there another place we could go to?
"Another" can mean 'alternative' or 'different'. ✓
💡
Quick Test:
→ If you can say "the second one" or "the rest" → use other
→ If you can say "one more" or "additional" → use another

One other / one another: Both can be correct depending on context. "We sent one other Christmas cards" (two groups exchanging) vs "one another" (reciprocal for many).

📋 Rule 22 — Quick Recap

  • Each other = exactly 2 people (आपस में — दोनों); One another = more than 2 people
  • "Peter and Mary helped each other" ✓ (2 people)
  • "All citizens should love one another" ✓ (many people)
  • Other = दूसरा / the second set (the rest); Another = एक और / additional / alternative
  • "Other children need more time" ✓ (second group); "Could I have another slice?" ✓ (one more)
  • Plural of "other" is others. "Another" is always singular (except with numbers: "another two weeks" ✓)
23

Words That Always Come in Fixed Pairs

"Seldom or never" · "Seldom, if ever" · "Little or nothing" · "Little, if anything" + Partial Inversion

→ The Core Rule

There are certain words which always come in pairs. There are exactly four valid combinations. No other combinations are allowed.

★ From the book (Rule 23): The word "Seldom" is an Adverb and when a sentence begins with "seldom", the law of inversion will be followed.
We can either use "Seldom or never" or "Seldom, if ever", but we cannot use "Seldom or ever".

The phrase "Little or nothing" means Hardly anything. We can use "Little or nothing" or "Little, if anything", but we cannot use "Little or anything".
Word Valid Pair 1 (+ "and") Valid Pair 2 (+ "if") ❌ INVALID
Seldom
(बहुत कम / ना के बराबर)		 Seldom or never ✓ Seldom, if ever ✓ Seldom or ever ✗
Little
(ना के बराबर)		 Little or nothing ✓ Little, if anything ✓ Little or anything ✗
→ Partial Inversion (When Adverb Starts the Sentence)

When an adverb like Seldom or Little comes at the beginning of a sentence, the law of Partial Inversion must be followed — i.e., a Helping Verb comes before the Subject.

Adverb (at start) → Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb
(NOT: Adverb + Subject + Main Verb)
⚠ Exception: If there is a comma after the adverb (the comma belongs to additional information between two commas), then inversion is NOT required. The comma here is for the bracketed extra info — not for the adverb itself.
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Seldom Combinations + Inversion (from book examples)
✗
(I) Seldom or never he comes to my house. [Incorrect]
Wrong combination — "Seldom or never" is correct pairing BUT inversion not followed. No helping verb before "he". ✗
✗
(II) Seldom or ever he comes to my house. [Incorrect]
WRONG PAIR — "Seldom or ever" is an invalid combination. Use "Seldom or never" or "Seldom, if ever". ✗
✓
(III) Seldom, if ever, does he come to my house. [Correct]
Valid pair: "Seldom, if ever" ✓. Inversion followed: "does he come" (Helping verb + Subject + Main verb). ✓
✓
(IV) Seldom or never does he come to my house. [Correct]
Valid pair: "Seldom or never" ✓. Inversion followed: "does he come". ✓
Why are I and II wrong even for pairing? Even if the pair is correct (Seldom or never), the inversion must be followed. Seldom or never does he come ✓. The two commas in (III) are only around the extra phrase "if ever" — they do not cancel inversion.
Little Combinations + Inversion
✓
I can find little or nothing on this table. [Correct]
Valid pair: "little or nothing" ✓. (No inversion needed as "little" not at start here.) ✓
✓
I can find little, if anything, on this table. [Correct]
Valid pair: "little, if anything" ✓. ✓
✗
I can find little if nothing on this table. [Incorrect]
WRONG — "little if nothing" is not a valid combination. ✗
✗
I can find little or anything on this table. [Incorrect]
WRONG — "little or anything" is not a valid combination. ✗

📋 Rule 23 — Quick Recap

  • Only 4 valid combinations: Seldom or never ✓ | Seldom, if ever ✓ | Little or nothing ✓ | Little, if anything ✓
  • Invalid: "Seldom or ever" ✗ | "Little or anything" ✗ | "Little if nothing" ✗
  • When Seldom/Little starts the sentence → Partial Inversion required: Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb
  • "Seldom, if ever, does he come to my house" ✓ (inversion: does + he)
  • Comma inside a pair (e.g., "Seldom, if ever") is for bracketed info only — inversion still applies
24

"Very" vs "Much" — With Degrees of Adjectives

Very = Positive degree & Superlative with "the" | Much = Comparative degree & Past Participle | "the very best" vs "much the best"

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 24):
Whenever we use "very" with Superlative degree, article "the" is always used before "very" i.e. "the very superlative".
"Very" modifies present participle used as an adjective, adverb and adjective in the positive degree.

However, when we use "much" with Superlative degree, article "the" is used before the superlative i.e. "much the superlative".
"Much" is used with a comparative degree and past participle.
VERY — Used with:
  • Positive degree adjectives (smart, beautiful, tall)
  • Present Participle as adjective (very interesting, very tiring)
  • Superlative degree → "the very best", "the very worst"
  • When used with superlative → article "the" comes first: "the very best"
MUCH — Used with:
  • Comparative degree adjectives (smarter, taller, more beautiful)
  • Past Participle as adjective (much interested, much surprised)
  • Superlative degree → "much the best", "much the worst"
  • When used with superlative → "much" comes first: "much the best"
VERY + Positive Degree ✓  |  the VERY + Superlative ✓
MUCH + Comparative Degree ✓  |  MUCH the + Superlative ✓  |  MUCH + Past Participle ✓
→ Past Participle vs Present Participle (as Adjectives)
Past Participle — 3rd form of verb as adjective
  • Example: interested, surprised, exhausted, broken
  • Used with MUCH: "He is much interested." ✓
  • "I was much exhausted after the match." ✓
Present Participle — V1 + ing as adjective
  • Example: interesting, tiring, boring, amazing
  • Used with VERY: "It is a very interesting novel." ✓
  • "It is a very interesting book." ✓
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
The Much Best / The Very Best / Much the Best / Very the Best
✗
He is the much best student of my class. [Incorrect]
WRONG — "much" cannot be placed between "the" and a superlative this way. ✗
✗
He is very the best student of my class. [Incorrect]
WRONG — "very" must come after "the" when used with superlative: "the very best". ✗
✓
He is the very best student of my class. [Correct] — means "really best"
CORRECT — "the" + "very" + superlative. Very modifies the superlative "best". ✓
✓
He is much the best student of my class. [Correct] — means "decidedly best"
CORRECT — "much" + "the" + superlative. Much modifies the superlative. ✓
Much with Comparative Degree
✓
He is much smarter than all other students. [Correct]
Much + comparative degree "smarter" ✓
✓
She is much more beautiful than her sister. [Correct]
Much + comparative degree "more beautiful" ✓
Very with Positive Degree & Present Participle
✓
It is a very interesting novel. [Correct]
Very + Present Participle "interesting" (V1+ing as adjective). ✓
✓
He is much interested. [Correct]
Much + Past Participle "interested" (V3 as adjective). ✓
⚠ Exception — Very CAN be used with Past Participle (when describing a person's state)
★ Exception (from class): Normally, much is used with Past Participle. But very CAN be used with a Past Participle when the Past Participle is describing the state / condition of a person (not an action). In that case, very is perfectly correct.
✓
I was very exhausted after the match. [Correct — Exception]
"exhausted" = Past Participle. But here it describes the state of the person (how I felt). So "very" is correct as an exception. ✓
💡
Quick Memory Trick:
VERY = goes with Positive degree and Present Participle (verb + ing as adjective)
MUCH = goes with Comparative degree and Past Participle (verb 3rd form as adjective)

Superlative:
→ "the very best" ✓ (= really the best)
→ "much the best" ✓ (= decidedly the best)

📋 Rule 24 — Quick Recap

  • VERY → Positive degree ("very smart") + Present Participle ("very interesting") + the very + Superlative ("the very best")
  • MUCH → Comparative degree ("much smarter") + Past Participle ("much interested") + much the + Superlative ("much the best")
  • "He is the very best" ✓ (really best) | "He is much the best" ✓ (decidedly best)
  • "He is the much best" ✗ | "He is very the best" ✗
  • Past Participle = V3 acting as adjective (interested, surprised) → use much
  • Present Participle = V1+ing acting as adjective (interesting, tiring) → use very

✏️ Practice — Very or Much?

✓
She is much more beautiful than her sister.
Much + comparative degree ✓
✓
It is a very interesting novel.
Very + present participle as adjective ✓
✓
He is the very best student.
the + very + superlative ✓
✓
He is much the best student.
much + the + superlative ✓
✗
He is the much best student.
WRONG — cannot put "much" between "the" and superlative. ✗
✓
He is much smarter than all others.
Much + comparative degree ✓

The Use of "Enough"

Enough = Adjective (before Noun)  |  Enough = Adverb (after Adjective) — position decides the role

→ Adjective & Adverb — Quick Definitions

Adjective = a word that tells us about a noun or pronoun.
Adverb = a word that tells us about a verb, adjective, another adverb, or the whole sentence.
(Adverb is like the पड़ोस वाली आंटी — knows about everyone!)

★ From the book (Rule 25): "Enough" plays the role of both Adverb and Adjective.
When used as an Adjective, it is placed before the noun or pronoun i.e. "enough noun".
When used as an Adverb, it is placed after the adjective i.e. "adjective enough".
Enough as Adjective → BEFORE the Noun   (enough + noun) ✓
Enough as Adverb → AFTER the Adjective   (adjective + enough) ✓
→ The Two Roles of "Enough"
ENOUGH as ADJECTIVE
  • Tells us about a noun
  • Position: BEFORE the noun
  • Pattern: enough + noun
  • e.g. "There is enough water in the bottle."
  • e.g. "He has enough sugar."
  • e.g. "Test reports indicate that it develops enough power."
ENOUGH as ADVERB
  • Tells us about an adjective
  • Position: AFTER the adjective
  • Pattern: adjective + enough
  • e.g. "She is smart enough to handle it."
  • e.g. "You are not big enough for basketball."
  • e.g. "A landing between the ground and first floor is ample enough."
⚠️
"Enough" cannot play both roles at the same time.
It is either an Adjective (before noun) OR an Adverb (after adjective) — never both in one sentence.
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Enough as ADJECTIVE (before noun)
✓
There is enough water in the bottle. [Correct]
"water" = noun. "enough" tells us about the noun → Adjective. Placed BEFORE noun. ✓
✓
He has enough sugar. [Correct]
"sugar" = noun. "enough" → Adjective. BEFORE noun. ✓
✓
On neither of those trips was there enough sun to get any decent photographs. [Correct]
"sun" = noun. "enough" → Adjective. BEFORE noun. ✓
✓
Test reports indicate that it develops enough power for the job and is flexible. [Correct]
"power" = noun. "enough" → Adjective. BEFORE noun. ✓
Enough as ADVERB (after adjective)
✓
She is smart enough to handle it. [Correct]
"smart" = adjective. "enough" tells us about the adjective → Adverb. Placed AFTER adjective. ✓
✓
You are not big enough for basketball. [Correct]
"big" = adjective. "enough" → Adverb. AFTER adjective. ✓
✓
A landing between the ground and first floor is ample enough to be used as a study. [Correct]
"ample" = adjective. "enough" → Adverb. AFTER adjective. ✓
Wrong placement — Enough before Adjective (INCORRECT)
✗
She is enough beautiful to win the Miss India competition. [Incorrect]
WRONG — "enough" placed BEFORE the adjective "beautiful". When modifying an adjective, enough must come AFTER it. ✗
✓
She is beautiful enough to win the Miss India competition. [Correct]
CORRECT — "enough" placed AFTER adjective "beautiful" → Adverb role. ✓
💡
Quick Rule:
Ask: What is "enough" describing?
→ If it describes a noun (water, sugar, power) → it is an Adjective → comes BEFORE the noun.
→ If it describes an adjective (smart, big, beautiful) → it is an Adverb → comes AFTER the adjective.

📋 Rule 25 — Quick Recap

  • Enough as Adjective → BEFORE noun: "enough water" ✓, "enough sugar" ✓, "enough power" ✓
  • Enough as Adverb → AFTER adjective: "smart enough" ✓, "big enough" ✓, "beautiful enough" ✓
  • "She is enough beautiful" ✗ → "She is beautiful enough" ✓
  • "There is enough water" ✓ (Adjective, before noun) | "She is smart enough" ✓ (Adverb, after adjective)
  • Enough cannot play both roles at the same time in one sentence.

Rule 22 — Each Other · One Another · Other · Another Reciprocal Pronouns
Each other:For exactly 2 people. "Peter and Mary helped each other." ✓
One another:For more than 2 people. "All citizens should love one another." ✓
Other:Means "the second/different one". Plural = others. "Some children learn fast but other children need more time." ✓
Another:Means "one more / additional / alternative". "Could I have another slice?" ✓ | "The investigation will take another two weeks." ✓

Rule 23 — Fixed Word Pairs (Seldom/Little) + Inversion Word Pairs
Valid Pairs:Seldom or never ✓ | Seldom, if ever ✓ | Little or nothing ✓ | Little, if anything ✓
Invalid:"Seldom or ever" ✗ | "Little or anything" ✗ | "Little if nothing" ✗

Note:Comma in "Seldom, if ever" = extra info (not for the adverb). Inversion still required.

Rule 24 — Very vs Much with Degrees Very / Much
VERY → used with:Positive degree ("very smart"), Present Participle as adjective ("very interesting"), Superlative → "the very best" ✓
MUCH → used with:Comparative degree ("much smarter"), Past Participle as adjective ("much interested"), Superlative → "much the best" ✓
Key Examples:"He is the very best" ✓ (really) | "He is much the best" ✓ (decidedly) | "He is the much best" ✗ | "He is very the best" ✗
⚠ Exception:Very CAN be used with Past Participle when it describes the state/condition of a person. e.g. "I was very exhausted after the match." ✓ (exhausted = state of person)
25
Rule 25 — The Use of "Enough" Enough
Enough as Adjective:Placed BEFORE the noun. Describes the noun. e.g. "enough water", "enough sugar", "enough power" ✓
Enough as Adverb:Placed AFTER the adjective. Describes the adjective. e.g. "smart enough", "big enough", "beautiful enough" ✓
Classic Error:"She is enough beautiful" ✗ → "She is beautiful enough" ✓
Key Test:What does "enough" describe? Noun → before noun (Adjective). Adjective → after adjective (Adverb).
