26

The Use of Adverb "as"

Some verbs take "as" · Some verbs NEVER take "as" — examiner's favourite!

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 26):
"as" CAN be used as an adverb with these given words:
treat, define, regard, describe, respect, depict, portray, view, know, etc.

However, "as" CANNOT be used as an adverb with these words:
elect, name, think, consider, call, make, choose, appoint, etc.
✓ TAKE "as"
treat define regard describe respect depict portray view know
✗ NEVER TAKE "as"
elect name think consider call make choose appoint
💡
Examiner's Favourite: The most frequently tested words in exams are call, elect, consider. Always check — if any of these appear followed by "as", it is wrong.
✓ Examples — Verbs That TAKE "as"
✓
Biology is defined as the science of living beings.
"define" → takes "as". ✓
✓
He is regarded as a God in India.
"regard" → takes "as". ✓
✓
She described Gary as shy.
"describe" → takes "as". ✓
✓
His latest film is described as a 'romantic comedy'.
"describe" → takes "as". ✓
✓
She is treated as a child.
"treat" → takes "as". ✓
✗ Examples — Verbs That NEVER Take "as"
✗
She was elected as Chair of the Board of Governors. [Incorrect]
"elect" NEVER takes "as". Remove "as". ✗
✓
She was elected Chair of the Board of Governors. [Correct]
No "as" after "elected". ✓
✗
He has just been appointed as director of the publishing division. [Incorrect]
"appoint" NEVER takes "as". ✗
✓
He has just been appointed director of the publishing division. [Correct]
No "as" after "appointed". ✓
✗
He thinks her as a fool. [Incorrect]
"think" NEVER takes "as". ✗
✓
He thinks her a fool. [Correct]
No "as" after "thinks". ✓
✗
The teacher called him as stupid. [Incorrect]
"call" NEVER takes "as". ✗
✓
The teacher called him stupid. [Correct]
No "as" after "called". ✓

📋 Rule 26 — Quick Recap

  • With "as": treat, define, regard, describe, respect, depict, portray, view, know
  • Without "as": elect, name, think, consider, call, make, choose, appoint
  • "Biology is defined as the science…" ✓ | "She was elected as Chair" ✗ → "She was elected Chair" ✓
  • "He thinks her as a fool" ✗ → "He thinks her a fool" ✓
  • "The teacher called him as stupid" ✗ → "The teacher called him stupid" ✓

The Use of Conjunction "Although"

"Although" never takes "but"  |  "yet" is optional  |  comma is used in modern English

→ The Core Rule

"Although" means "in spite of something". It is a subordinating conjunction. The clause it introduces is a subordinate clause which needs a main clause to complete it. Sometimes "although" takes "yet" in the main clause.

★ From the book (Rule 27): "Although" as a subordinate clause never takes "but" in the main clause. "Yet" is optional — it can or cannot be used. However, "but" can never be used.
ConstructionStatusType
Although … , [comma] …✓ CorrectModern English (most preferred)
Although … , yet …✓ CorrectTraditional English (optional)
Although … , but …✗ WRONGNever allowed — ever
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Although + comma (Modern English — most used)
✓
Although she is rich, she thinks twice before spending a penny. [Correct]
Modern English: "although" + comma. No "yet", no "but". ✓
✓
Although he is smart, he fails every time. [Correct]
Modern English: "although" + comma only. ✓
Although + yet (Traditional English — optional)
✓
Although she is rich, yet she thinks twice before spending a penny. [Correct]
Traditional English: "although + yet" is a valid combination. ✓
✓
Although he is smart, yet he fails every time. [Correct]
"although + yet" — correct in traditional English. ✓
Although + but (ALWAYS WRONG)
✗
Although she is rich, but she thinks twice before spending a penny. [Incorrect]
"although + but" is NEVER allowed. "but" cannot come after "although". ✗
✗
Although he is smart, but he fails every time. [Incorrect]
"although + but" = WRONG. Always. ✗
💡
Quick Trick:
"Although" = हालांकि / यद्यपि
→ + comma only (Modern) ✓
→ + yet (Traditional, optional) ✓
→ + but = NEVER ✗

Both "although…," and "although…, yet" are correct but the modern form (comma only) is more preferred.

📋 Rule 27 — Quick Recap

  • "Although" = subordinating conjunction meaning "in spite of something"
  • "Although…, [comma]…" ✓ (Modern English — most preferred)
  • "Although…, yet…" ✓ (Traditional English — optional, can/cannot use)
  • "Although…, but…" ✗ (NEVER — always wrong)
  • "Although she is rich, she thinks twice…" ✓ | "Although she is rich, but…" ✗
28

Non-Continuous Verbs (Stative Verbs)

These verbs are NEVER used in ANY Continuous tense — Possession · Perception · Feeling categories

→ The Core Rule

There are certain verbs which are never used in any Continuous tense. This means you cannot use is/am/are/was/were + V1+ing with these verbs.

★ From the book (Rule 28): There are certain verbs that do not exist in the "-ing" form. These verbs are NEVER used in:
Present Continuous | Past Continuous | Present Perfect Continuous | Past Perfect Continuous | Future Continuous | Future Perfect Continuous

Simple rule: Any tense with the word "Continuous" → these verbs CANNOT use their -ing form.
✗   is/am/are/was/were/has been/have been + [stative verb] + ing   = WRONG
✓   Use Simple Present or Simple Past instead
→ The Three Categories of Stative Verbs
POSSESSION
  • own
  • belong
  • have
  • include
  • possess
  • owe
  • contain
  • compromise
  • consist
PERCEPTION
  • see
  • smell
  • taste
  • hear
  • feel
  • appear
  • seem
  • resemble
  • notice
FEELING
  • love
  • know
  • think
  • hate
  • want
  • need
  • desire
  • wish
  • like
★ All Examples — from Book & Class

Possession → no continuous. ✗
✓

PERCEPTION verbs in continuous = wrong
✗

Perception → no continuous. ✗
✓

Simple present. ✓
✗

Perception → no continuous. ✗
✓

FEELING verbs in continuous = wrong
✗

Feeling → no continuous. (McDonald's tagline is grammatically wrong!) ✗
✓

✗

Feel = stative verb. In written English, not used in continuous. ✗

★ Special: When "HAVING" IS Correct

"Have" is a possession verb, so normally "having" is wrong. But there are two cases where "having" is correctly used:

Case 1 — Having + V3 (Perfect Participle)
  • When "having" is followed by Verb's 3rd form
  • This is called Perfect Participle
  • "Having done my work, I went to play." ✓
  • Meaning: after completing the work
Case 2 — Having = Consuming / Eating
  • When "having" means eating / consuming / ingesting
  • NOT in the sense of possession
  • "I am having lunch right now." ✓
  • "I am having tea." ✓
★ From the book — "Having" four cases:
(I) I am having a book. [Incorrect] — "have" denotes possession → use "have".
(II) I have a book. [Correct]
(III) I am having lunch now. [Correct] — consuming, not possession.
(IV) Having done the work, he went to play. [Correct] — Perfect Participle (having + V3).

In sentences (III) and (IV), "have" is not used in the sense of possession or belongingness or ownership, thus "having" is correctly used.
→ When -ing Form IS Allowed (Important Exceptions)

The rule says these verbs cannot be used in Continuous tense. But the -ing form itself is allowed in other contexts:

✓
Seeing a dog, he started to cry. [Correct]
"seeing" is NOT in Present Continuous tense here. It is a Participle used in a participial phrase. No "is/am/are/was/were" before it. ✓
✓
He is a very loving person. [Correct]
"loving" here is used as an Adjective, NOT a verb. No Continuous tense involved. ✓
✓
He described his work in loving detail. [Correct]
"loving" = Adjective (describing "detail"). Simple Past tense. No Continuous. ✓
⚠️
Do NOT rote-learn "these verbs never take -ing".
The correct rule is: these verbs cannot be used in any Continuous tense.
Their -ing form as adjective, participle, or gerund is perfectly fine.
💡
The One-Line Rule:
If any tense has the word "Continuous" in it → stative verbs CANNOT use their -ing form.
Present Continuous ✗ | Past Continuous ✗ | Present Perfect Continuous ✗ | Past Perfect Continuous ✗ | Future Continuous ✗

📋 Rule 28 — Quick Recap

  • 3 categories: Possession (own, belong, have, include, possess, owe, contain, consist…) | Perception (see, smell, taste, hear, feel, appear, seem, resemble, notice) | Feeling (love, know, think, hate, want, need, desire, wish, like)
  • These verbs CANNOT be in ANY Continuous tense (Present/Past/Perfect Continuous)
  • "I am owning a car" ✗ → "I own a car" ✓ | "This sauce is tasting" ✗ → "This sauce tastes" ✓
  • "I am loving it" ✗ → "I love it" ✓ | "She has been hating jazz" ✗ → "She has always hated jazz" ✓
  • Having allowed: (1) Having + V3 (Perfect Participle) "Having done my work…" ✓ (2) Having = eating/consuming "I am having lunch" ✓
  • -ing as Adjective is fine: "He is a loving person" ✓ | Participial phrase: "Seeing a dog, he cried" ✓

Rule 27 — Use of "Although" Although
Although + comma:"Although she is rich, she thinks twice…" ✓ (Modern English — most preferred)
Although + yet:"Although she is rich, yet she thinks twice…" ✓ (Traditional — optional)
Although + but:"Although she is rich, but…" ✗ NEVER. "but" can NEVER follow "although".
