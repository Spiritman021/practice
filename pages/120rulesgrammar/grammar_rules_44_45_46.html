Use of Apostrophe ('s) — Possession, Hissing Sound & Exceptions

Apostrophe ('s) shows possession · NOT used with non-living things · Exceptions: time, weight, distance, place, idiom, unit, heavenly body, personification

→ Concept 1: How Apostrophe ('s) Shows Possession
★ Rule: Apostrophe ('s) is used to show possession (belonging / हक).
Shyam's car = Car of Shyam  |  Sangeeta's car = Car of Sangeeta
Both forms are correct: either Shyam's car or car of Shyam.
[Owner]'s [Thing]  =  [Thing] of [Owner]
Shyam's car = Car of Shyam · Sangeeta's car = Car of Sangeeta
Hissing Sound Rule — remove 's' to avoid hissing
Hissing Sound: When adding 's causes a hissing sound (e.g., Manas's → Manass sound), remove the extra 's' and keep only the apostrophe.

Manas's car → sounds like "Manass car" (hissing ✗) → write Manas' car ✓
Students's rights → sounds like "Studentss rights" (hissing ✗) → write Students' rights ✓

Rule: When hissing sound occurs → remove the last 's', keep the apostrophe.
✗
Manas's car [Hissing sound → Incorrect]
Manas + 's = "Manass" — hissing sound. ✗
✓
Manas' car [Correct]
Remove 's' after apostrophe to avoid hissing. ✓
✗
Students's rights [Hissing → Incorrect]
Students + 's = "Studentss" — hissing. ✗
✓
Students' rights [Correct]
Students' (only apostrophe, no extra 's'). ✓
⭐
Exception — "else": "somebody else's car" — even though it creates a hissing sound, this IS correct. This is a special exception for "else".
→ Concept 2: Apostrophe ('s) is NOT Used with Non-Living Things
★ From the book (Rule 44): Apostrophe ('s) is not used with non-living things.
For non-living things → use "of" instead.
Wrong (Apostrophe with non-living)→Correct (use "of") Table's legs [Incorrect]→Legs of the table [Correct] Door's bolt [Incorrect]→Bolt of the door [Correct] Pen's ink [Incorrect]→Ink of the pen [Correct] Book's page [Incorrect]→Page of the book [Correct] Chair's legs [Incorrect]→Legs of the chair [Correct] Bottle's cap is damaged [Incorrect]→The cap of the bottle is damaged [Correct]
→ Concept 3: Exceptions — When Non-Living Things CAN Take ('s)
★ From the book: *NOTE: There are some exceptions to this rule. When we talk about time, weight, distance, place, idiom, unit, personification, or heavenly body, we can use Apostrophe ('s) with them.
⏰ Time ⚖ Weight 📏 Distance 🗺 Place 💬 Idiom 📦 Unit 🌟 Heavenly Body 🎭 Personification

💡
Personification (मानवीकरण):
When a non-living thing is given human/living qualities — like poets who describe the moon's feelings, the pen's pain, the bottle's tears — that non-living thing is treated as living. So Apostrophe ('s) is correct in such cases.
→ Concept 4: Apostrophe ('s) is NEVER Used with Pronouns
★ From the book: *NOTE: The following pronouns are already possessive and do not require apostrophes: yours, ours, its, theirs, his, hers, and whose.
⚠️
Common Letter-Writing Mistake:
Many students write "Your's obediently" or "Your's faithfully" at the end of letters.
This is WRONG. Never add apostrophe to possessive pronouns.
Correct: "Yours obediently" / "Yours faithfully" (NO apostrophe). ✓
✗
Your's obediently [Incorrect]
"your's" — pronouns never take apostrophe. ✗
✓
Yours obediently [Correct]
"yours" is already possessive — no apostrophe needed. ✓
Pronouns already possessive — NEVER add apostrophe:
yours · ours · its · theirs · his · hers · whose
Remember: it's = "it is" (contraction) · its = possessive (no apostrophe)

📋 Rule 44 — Quick Recap

  • Apostrophe ('s) shows possession: Shyam's car = Car of Shyam
  • Hissing sound (Manas's → "Manass") → remove last 's' → Manas' car ✓
  • Exception: "somebody else's" — hissing but still correct
  • Non-living things → use "of": legs of the table ✓ | table's legs ✗
  • Exceptions where ('s) IS allowed with non-living: time · weight · distance · place · idiom · unit · heavenly body · personification
  • Pronouns (yours/ours/its/theirs/his/hers/whose) → NEVER add apostrophe · "Yours faithfully" ✓ | "Your's faithfully" ✗

Past Point of Time → Always Simple Past Tense

yesterday · last week · last year · ago · in 1990 · 10 days ago → ALWAYS Simple Past · Never Perfect Tense

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 45): Whenever a point of time in the past is mentioned in the sentence (i.e., yesterday, 10 days ago, etc.), we always use "Simple Past" tense.
Past Point of Time (yesterday / last week / ago / in 1990…) → Simple Past ONLY
NEVER use Present Perfect (have/has + V3) or Past Perfect (had + V3)
Common Past Point of Time Markers
yesterday last week last month last year ago in 1990 10 days ago
★ All Examples from Book & Class
Example 1 — yesterday
✗
I have gone to the market yesterday to purchase a car. [Incorrect]
"yesterday" = past point of time → Simple Past needed. "have gone" = Present Perfect ✗. Also: "there" not "their". ✗
✓
I went to the market yesterday to purchase a car. [Correct]
"yesterday" → Simple Past "went". ✓
Example 2 — last week
✗
The company's shares have dived by 90p to 165p on the stock market last week. [Incorrect]
"last week" = past point of time → Simple Past. "have dived" = Present Perfect ✗
✓
The company's shares dived by 90p to 165p on the stock market last week. [Correct]
"last week" → Simple Past "dived". ✓
Class Examples
✗
I had gone there yesterday. [Incorrect]
"yesterday" = past point of time → Past Perfect "had gone" is also wrong. Must be Simple Past. ✗
✓
I went there yesterday. [Correct]
Simple Past "went". Note: "there" (वहां) not "their" (उनका). ✓
💡
Memory Trick:
Past Point of Time = exact moment when something happened (we know the date/time).
Simple Past = used when we know exactly when something happened.
Present Perfect = used when we do NOT know exactly when (or just recently happened).

📋 Rule 45 — Quick Recap

  • Past Point of Time (yesterday / last week / ago / in 1990) → Simple Past tense ONLY
  • NEVER use Present Perfect (have/has + V3) with past point of time
  • NEVER use Past Perfect (had + V3) with past point of time
  • "I went there yesterday." ✓ | "I have gone there yesterday." ✗ | "I had gone there yesterday." ✗
  • "Shares dived last week." ✓ | "Shares have dived last week." ✗

Words Never Used with "most" / Comparative Degree

unique · excellent · perfect · ideal · entire · complete · chief · round · total · impossible etc. — these words are already absolute · no comparison possible

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 46): There are certain words like "unique, excellent, perfect, ideal, entire, complete, etc." which are never used with "most".
These words are absolute in meaning — they cannot be compared or made more superlative.
These absolute words → NEVER use "most", "more", "almost", "completely", "very" before them
Unique = already the most unique · Perfect = already perfectly perfect · Excellent = already the best

Why can't we compare these?
"Perfect" = already the best possible state. How can something be "more perfect"?
"Unique" = already one-of-a-kind. How can something be "more unique"?
"Impossible" = already cannot be done. "More impossible" makes no sense.
"Round" = already perfectly circular. "More round" is illogical.
