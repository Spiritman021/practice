54

⭐ "know" — Always Followed by a Relative Word

After "know" — always insert how / when / why / where / what / whether etc. · NEVER directly follow "know" with infinitive "to"

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 54): Whenever the word "know" is used in a sentence, it is not directly followed by the infinitive "to". We use the words like "how, when, why, where, etc." to make the sentence grammatically feasible.
know + to → WRONG
know + [how / when / why / where / what / whether / who / which / that] → CORRECT
The Relative Words that MUST follow "know"
how when why where what whether who which that
These words are called Relative Pronouns / Relative Adverbs / Conjunctions.
They connect two ideas — the knowing and the thing known. Without them the sentence is incomplete.
★ All Examples from Book & Class
The most common mistake — "know to + V1"
✗
I know to swim. [Incorrect]
"know" cannot be directly followed by "to". ✗
✓
I know how to swim. [Correct]
Relative word "how" inserted between "know" and "to". ✓
✗
I don't know to make tea. [Incorrect]
"know" cannot directly precede "to". ✗
✓
I do not know how to make tea; hence you should help me out. [Correct]
Relative word "how" inserted. ✓
Book examples — from the text
✓
I do not know what all the fuss is about. [Correct]
Relative pronoun "what" after "know". ✓
✓
Do you know where the Post Office is? [Correct]
Relative adverb "where" after "know". ✓
Class examples — more practice
✓
I know when to answer the question. [Correct]
Relative adverb "when" after "know". ✓
✓
I know what he is asking. [Correct]
Relative pronoun "what" after "know". ✓
✓
I know why he left. [Correct]
Relative adverb "why" after "know". ✓
✓
I know whether she will come or not. [Correct]
Conjunction "whether" after "know". ✓
💡
Why is a relative word needed?
"I know swim" is incomplete — you need to specify what exactly you know about swimming.
"I know how to swim" — now it's complete: you know the method of swimming.
The relative word bridges two ideas and makes the sentence meaningful.

📋 Rule 54 — Quick Recap ⭐

  • "know" is NEVER directly followed by "to" → always insert a relative word first
  • Relative words: how · when · why · where · what · whether · who · which · that
  • "I know how to swim." ✓ | "I know to swim." ✗
  • "I don't know how to make tea." ✓ | "I don't know to make tea." ✗
  • "I know what he is asking." ✓ | "Do you know where the post office is?" ✓
55

else → but  ·  other → than  ·  rather → than

"else" is always followed by "but" · "other" and "rather" are always followed by "than" · Never use "else…than"

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 55): The adverb "else" is always followed by "but" while the adverbs "other" and "rather" are always followed by "than".
Adverb Must use Example
else but "It is nothing else but foolishness."
other than "He met no other person than Ram."
rather than "I would rather stay home than go out."
else → but  ·  other → than  ·  rather → than
NEVER: else…than ✗  |  NEVER: other…but ✗  |  NEVER: rather…but ✗
★ All Examples from Book & Class
else → but
✓
It is nothing else but mere foolishness. [Correct]
"else" → always followed by "but". ✓
✓
Everybody else has agreed but you. [Correct]
"else" → followed by "but". ✓ (Everyone agreed, but you didn't)
✗
It is nothing else than foolishness. [Incorrect]
"else" must be followed by "but", never "than". ✗
other → than
✓
He met no other person than Ram. [Correct]
"other" → always followed by "than". ✓ (He met nobody except Ram)
✗
He met no other person but Ram. [Incorrect]
"other" must be followed by "than", not "but". ✗
rather → than
✓
I think I would rather stay at home this evening than go out. [Correct]
"rather" → always followed by "than". ✓
✓
I would rather take coffee than do that rather than putting my life into stake. [Correct]
"rather" → followed by "than". ✓
✗
I would rather stay home but go out. [Incorrect]
"rather" must be followed by "than", not "but". ✗
💡
Memory Trick:
ELSE = sounds like "otherwise" → use BUT (contrast)
OTHER / RATHER = involve comparison → use THAN (comparison)

Quick test: if the word is "else" → always write "but" after it. If the word is "other" or "rather" → always write "than" after it.

📋 Rule 55 — Quick Recap

  • else → but: "nothing else but foolishness" ✓ | "everybody else agreed but you" ✓ | "else than" ✗
  • other → than: "no other person than Ram" ✓ | "other but" ✗
  • rather → than: "would rather stay home than go out" ✓ | "rather but" ✗
  • NEVER: else…than ✗ · NEVER: other…but ✗ · NEVER: rather…but ✗
