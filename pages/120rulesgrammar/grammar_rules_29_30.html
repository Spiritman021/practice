Perfect Tense with Time-Signal Words

ever · never · yet · already · now · just · just now · always · so far · recently · lately · up to now — always demand Perfect Tense

→ The Core Rule
★ From the book (Rule 29): There are certain words which are always used in Perfect Tense:
ever, never, yet, already, now, always, occasionally, often, lately, recently, so far, up to now, up to the present, just now.

If these words are present in a sentence AND the task is completed → always and only use Perfect Tense.
ever never yet already now just just now always so far recently lately up to now up to the present occasionally often
→ Present Perfect vs Past Perfect — How to Choose
Present Perfect — has/have + V3
  • Action completed just now / in present
  • No specific past time mentioned
  • "Have you ever seen the Taj Mahal?" ✓
  • "I have not spoken to her yet." ✓
  • "The train has already left." ✓
  • "He has gone there already." ✓
Past Perfect — had + V3
  • Two past events — action completed before another past event
  • "Past of past"
  • "The concert had already begun by the time we arrived." ✓
  • "He had gone there already." ✓ (long before)
  • "The train had already left." ✓ (before we reached)
Time-signal word + Task completed → Perfect Tense
Action just done (present) → has/have + V3 (Present Perfect)
Action done before another past action → had + V3 (Past Perfect)
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
EVER — Have you ever…?
✗
Did you ever see the Taj Mahal? [Incorrect]
"ever" demands Perfect Tense. Simple past "did/see" is wrong here. ✗
✓
Have you ever seen the Taj Mahal? [Correct]
Present Perfect: have + seen. ✓
YET — "अब तक" (up to now, in negatives/questions)
✓
I have not spoken to her yet. [Correct]
"yet" = अब तक. Present Perfect used. ✓
ALREADY — Present Perfect OR Past Perfect both possible
✗
He went there already. [Incorrect]
Simple past with "already" = wrong. ✗
✓
He has gone there already. [Correct — Present Perfect]
Just went (recent) → Present Perfect. ✓
✓
He had gone there already. [Correct — Past Perfect]
Had gone long before → Past Perfect. Both are correct. ✓
✓
The concert had already begun by the time we arrived. [Correct]
Two past events: concert began BEFORE we arrived → Past Perfect. ✓
✓
The train has already left. [Correct]
Train just left (present) → Present Perfect. ✓
JUST NOW
✓
He has gone just now. [Correct]
"just now" = अभी अभी. Present Perfect. ✓
UP TO NOW / SO FAR
✗
Aman completed his work up to now. [Incorrect]
Simple past "completed" with "up to now" = wrong. ✗
✓
Aman has not completed his work up to now. [Correct]
"up to now" = अब तक. Present Perfect. ✓
I went to Paris last summer… (specific past time = simple past — NO perfect needed)
✓
I went to Paris last summer. [Correct]
"last summer" = specific past time mentioned. Simple past is correct here. (No time-signal word used.) ✓
The train already left… (without "has/had" = wrong)
✗
The train already left. [Incorrect]
"already" demands Perfect Tense. ✗
✓
The train has already left. [Correct]
Present Perfect. ✓

📋 Rule 29 — Quick Recap

  • Time-signal words: ever, never, yet, already, now, just, just now, always, so far, recently, lately, up to now, up to the present
  • If these words are used + task is completed → always use Perfect Tense
  • Action just completed (present) → has/have + V3 (Present Perfect)
  • Action completed before another past event → had + V3 (Past Perfect)
  • "Did you ever see the Taj Mahal?" ✗ → "Have you ever seen the Taj Mahal?" ✓
  • "The train already left" ✗ → "The train has already left" ✓
  • "The concert had already begun by the time we arrived." ✓ (Past Perfect — two past events)

Subject-Verb Agreement with Collective Nouns

Same task → Singular verb  |  Different tasks → Plural verb  |  Some words are always Plural (not collective nouns!)

→ What is a Collective Noun?

A Collective Noun is when we give one name to a whole group.

GroupCollective Noun
Many playersteam
Many studentsclass
Many fishshoal / school
Many people (gathered)crowd
Angry peoplemob
Group of judgesjury
Spectators watchingaudience
Soldiersarmy
→ The Core Rule — Singular or Plural Verb?
★ From the book (Rule 30):
When a collective noun denotes that the whole group is working together on the same task → use a singular verb.
When a collective noun denotes that the whole group is working on different tasks → use a plural verb.
Same Task → SINGULAR Verb (is/has/does)
  • All units doing one common thing
  • "The army is marching towards the Himalayas." ✓
  • "The audience is watching the movie." ✓
  • "The jury has taken the decision." ✓ (unanimous)
Different Tasks → PLURAL Verb (are/have/do)
  • Units doing different things
  • "The audience are taking their seats." ✓ (each person → different seat)
  • "The army are changing their dresses." ✓ (each person's own dress)
  • "The jury are divided in their opinion." ✓ (different opinions)
💡
Quick Test:
Ask — are ALL units doing the exact same thing?
→ Yes, one thing together → Singular verb (is/has)
→ No, each doing something different → Plural verb (are/have)
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Same task → Singular verb (is / has)
✓
The audience is watching the movie. [Correct]
All watching the same movie → Singular verb "is". ✓
✓
The army is marching towards the Himalayas. [Correct]
Whole army marching together (same direction) → Singular verb "is". ✓
✓
The jury has taken the decision [unanimously]. [Correct]
All members took one same decision → Singular verb "has". ✓
Different tasks → Plural verb (are / have) — all three are WRONG with "is"
✗
The audience is taking the seats. [Incorrect]
Each person sits on a DIFFERENT seat → plural verb needed. ✗
✓
The audience are taking the seats. [Correct]
Different seats → plural "are". ✓
✗
The army is changing their dresses. [Incorrect]
Each soldier's OWN dress (different) → plural verb needed. ✗
✓
The army are changing their dresses. [Correct]
Different dresses → plural "are". ✓
✗
The jury is divided in their opinion. [Incorrect]
Divided = different opinions → plural verb needed. ✗
✓
The jury are divided in their opinion. [Correct]
Different opinions → plural "are". ✓
⚠ Exceptions — These Words Look Like Collective Nouns But Are ALWAYS Plural
⚠️
Do NOT apply the Collective Noun rule to these words!
These are Plural Nouns (not collective nouns). They always take a Plural Verb — no exceptions.
★ From the book (Rule 30 — NOTE): Words like "police, vermin, cattle, clergy, gentry, poultry, folk, peasantry, people, infantry, cavalry" etc. are "plural nouns" and NOT "collective nouns". Plural noun always takes a plural verb.
police Always plural → "are"
cattle Milk-giving animals → always plural
vermin Insects that destroy crops → always plural
clergy People with religious duties → always plural
gentry Upper-class folk → always plural
folk People → always plural
peasantry Farming class → always plural
poultry Chickens/birds → always plural
infantry Soldiers on foot → always plural
cavalry Soldiers on horseback → always plural
people Persons → plural; Country → singular (see below)
Examples — Always Plural Verb
✓
The police are investigating the case. [Correct]
police = plural noun → always "are". ✓
✓
Cattle are wandering in the farm. [Correct]
cattle = plural noun → always "are". ✓
✓
People want to attain all worldly pleasures. [Correct]
people = persons → plural verb. ✓
→ Special: "People" vs "Peoples"
★ From the book (Rule 31 — NOTE): Sometimes the word "people" is used in the meaning of "a country" instead of "persons". In that case we use a singular verb. "Peoples" means countries or people belonging to different nationalities.
people = persons (most common)
  • Meaning: लोग / व्यक्ति
  • Verb: Plural
  • "People are investigating the case." ✓
  • "People want to be happy." ✓
people = a country / peoples = nationalities
  • people = एक देश → Singular verb possible
  • "The Indian people is proud of its culture." ✓
  • peoples = अलग-अलग देशों के लोग
  • "Peoples" means different nationalities.

📋 Rule 30 — Quick Recap

  • Collective Noun = one name given to a group (army, jury, audience, team, class, crowd, mob)
  • All units doing same task → Singular verb (is/has): "The army is marching" ✓ | "The jury has taken the decision" ✓
  • Units doing different tasks → Plural verb (are/have): "The audience are taking seats" ✓ | "The jury are divided" ✓
  • Always Plural (not collective nouns): police, cattle, vermin, clergy, gentry, folk, peasantry, poultry, infantry, cavalry, people (as persons)
  • "The police are" ✓ | "Cattle are" ✓ — NEVER singular for these words
  • people = persons → plural verb | people = a country → singular verb | peoples = different nationalities
