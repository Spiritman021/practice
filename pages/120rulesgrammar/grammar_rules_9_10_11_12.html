9

Prepositions of Time — "Since" and "For"

3 concepts — Point of Start vs Duration · Tense rules · Since + Simple Past

→ What are Since & For used for?

Both "since" and "for" are used in sentences where the speaker wants to talk about something that started in the past and continues into the present.

WordUsed forDefinitionExamples
FOR Duration (Period of time) Used to talk about an amount of time or space. The amount does not need to be exact. Used for "duration". for 10 years, for 6 months, for 6 hours, for 6 minutes, for 6 seconds, for days, for weeks, for months, for years
SINCE Point of Start Used to denote a specific point of time. "Since" can refer to a point after a specific time or event in the past, or to a particular point beginning sometime in the past and continuing until the present. since 2011, since Wednesday, since January, since childhood, since my father moved to Delhi, since she got married, since last Tuesday, since 2008, since midnight
★ NOTE from the book: "for" is used to specify a period of time while "since" is used to specify a particular point in time.
Concept 1 Since = Point of Start  |  For = Duration
SINCE → When did it start? (Point of Start)   |   FOR → How long has it been going on? (Duration)
Examples — FOR (Duration)
✓
He has been here for six months.
"Six months" = a duration (how long). → use FOR. ✓ [for + a period of time]
✓
He has been learning English for 10 years.
"10 years" = a duration. → use FOR. ✓
✓
He has been here for six months and still cannot speak a word of German.
[for + a period of time] ✓
Examples — SINCE (Point of Start)
✓
He has been here since last week.
"Last week" = specific starting point (when did he arrive?). → use SINCE. ✓
✓
I have known her since the beginning of last week.
[since + a particular point in time] ✓
✓
He has been learning English since childhood.
"Childhood" = when it started. → use SINCE. ✓
✓
I have known her since the beginning of this week.
Point of start = beginning of this week → SINCE. ✓
💡
Quick test:
Ask — "When did it start?" → If you get a specific time/event → use SINCE.
Ask — "How long has it been going on?" → If you get a time period → use FOR.
Concept 2 Only Perfect or Perfect Continuous Tense is used before Since/For (when denoting time)

When "for" or "since" is used to talk about time, only two tenses can come before them. No other tense is allowed.

Allowed TenseStructureExample
Perfect Tense has / have / had + V3    (or: will have / shall have + V3) I have had this watch since 1965.
Perfect Continuous Tense has / have / had + been + V1+ing It has been raining since 8 a.m.
★ NOTE from the book: Only the Perfect Tense or Perfect Continuous Tense is used before "since" and "for" when they denote time.
Examples — Book examples
✓
It has been raining since 8 a.m.
Present Perfect Continuous Tense (has been + V1+ing) before "since". ✓ [Present Perfect Continuous Tense]
✓
I have had this watch since 1965.
Present Perfect Tense (have + V3) before "since". ✓ [Present Perfect Tense]
Wrong Tense Examples
✗
I taught English for two years.
WRONG — Simple Past tense "taught" cannot be used before "for" when it denotes time. Must use Perfect or Perfect Continuous: "I have taught" or "I have been teaching". ✗
✗
She teaches the subject since she got married.
WRONG — Simple Present tense cannot be used before "since" when it denotes time. Must use: "She has taught" or "She has been teaching". ✗
✓
I have taught / have been teaching English for two years.
Perfect or Perfect Continuous → both correct ✓
✓
She has taught / has been teaching the subject since she got married.
Perfect or Perfect Continuous → both correct ✓
Concept 3 "Since" is always followed by Simple Past Tense

This is an important distinction: the tense before "since" is Perfect/Perfect Continuous. But the tense after "since" (the clause that "since" introduces) must always be Simple Past Tense — only the second form of the verb (V2), nothing else.

[Perfect / Perfect Continuous] + since + [Simple Past (V2 only)]
★ NOTE from the book: "since" is always followed by Simple Past tense.
Examples
✗
She has been living here since my father had moved to Delhi.
WRONG — After "since", you cannot use Past Perfect "had moved". Must be Simple Past: "moved". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
She has been living here since my father moved to Delhi.
CORRECT — "moved" = Simple Past (V2) after "since". ✓ [Correct]
✗
I had been waiting for him since I had completed my graduation.
WRONG — After "since", Past Perfect "had completed" is wrong. Must be Simple Past. ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
I had been waiting for him since I completed my graduation.
CORRECT — "completed" = Simple Past (V2) after "since". ✓ [Correct]
💡
Summary for Rule 9:
Before since/for → Perfect or Perfect Continuous Tense.
After "since" → Simple Past ONLY (just V2, never "had + V3").

📋 Rule 9 — Quick Recap

  • FOR = Duration (how long) → for 6 months, for 10 years, for 3 hours
  • SINCE = Point of Start (when it began) → since 2011, since childhood, since Monday
  • Before since/for (denoting time) → ONLY Perfect or Perfect Continuous Tense. Never Simple Past, Simple Present etc.
  • After "since" → ONLY Simple Past (V2). Never "had + V3" (Past Perfect) after since.
  • Formula: [has/have/had been + V1+ing] + since + [V2]

10

Two Actions Occurring in the Past

Earlier action → Past Perfect (had + V3)  |  Later action → Simple Past (V2)

→ The Core Rule

When two actions both occurred in the past, the tense used for each depends on which one happened first (earlier) and which happened second (later).

Earlier / Older Action → Past Perfect (had + V3)
Later / Newer Action → Simple Past (V2 or did + V1)
ActionWhich happened first?Tense usedStructure
Earlier action (पहले हुआ) Happened FIRST in time Past Perfect had + V3
Later action (बाद में हुआ) Happened SECOND in time Simple Past V2 / did + V1
From the book: When two actions occurred in the past, the action which happened earlier is denoted by using Past Perfect Tense and the action which happened later is denoted by using Simple Past Tense.
1 With "BEFORE" — Earlier action = Past Perfect

When "before" is used: the action before the word "before" happened earlier → Past Perfect. The action after "before" happened later → Simple Past.

✗
I fought with her before we had a breakup.
WRONG — Fighting happened BEFORE the breakup. It is the earlier action → must use Past Perfect "had fought". The breakup (later) → Simple Past "had a breakup" is correct. ✗
✓
I had fought with her before we had a breakup.
CORRECT — Fighting (earlier) → Past Perfect "had fought". Breakup (later) → Simple Past "had a breakup". ✓
✗
The patient died before the doctor arrived.
WRONG — Patient dying happened BEFORE doctor arriving → earlier action → must be Past Perfect. ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
The patient had died before the doctor arrived.
CORRECT — Patient died (earlier) → Past Perfect "had died". Doctor arrived (later) → Simple Past "arrived". ✓ [Correct]
2 With "AFTER" — Earlier action = Past Perfect (in the clause after "after")

When "after" is used: the action in the clause after "after" happened earlier → Past Perfect. The main action (before "after") happened later → Simple Past.

💡
Logic: "After he came back from America" → he came FIRST, then talked. So "coming back" = earlier = Past Perfect.
✓
He talked to me after he had come back from America.
Coming back (earlier, in "after" clause) → Past Perfect "had come". Talking (later) → Simple Past "talked". ✓
✗
He had finished the work after we had met him.
WRONG — Finishing work happened AFTER meeting. Meeting is the earlier action → Past Perfect. But finishing (later action) → must be Simple Past "finished". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
He finished the work after we had met him.
CORRECT — Finishing (later) → Simple Past "finished". Meeting (earlier) → Past Perfect "had met". ✓ [Correct]
✓
I finished the book after I had returned from college.
Returning (earlier) → Past Perfect "had returned". Finishing (later) → Simple Past "finished". ✓
✗
The patient died before the doctor arrived.
WRONG — Patient dying is the earlier action → must be Past Perfect "had died". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
The patient had died before the doctor arrived.
CORRECT. ✓ [Correct] — Earlier action = Past Perfect. Later action = Simple Past.

📋 Rule 10 — Quick Recap

  • Two past actions → Earlier (पहले) → Past Perfect (had + V3)
  • Two past actions → Later (बाद में) → Simple Past (V2)
  • With "BEFORE": action before "before" = earlier = Past Perfect
  • With "AFTER": action in the "after" clause = earlier = Past Perfect; main action = later = Simple Past
  • Memory trick: Earlier = पहले = had + V3    |    Later = अभी = V2

Possessive Adjective Before a Gerund

We use a Possessive Adjective (my/our/his/her/their/your) before a Gerund

→ What is a Gerund?

A Gerund is formed when V1 + ING (the first form of a verb + "ing") is used as a noun.

Gerund = V1 + ING    (when playing the role of a Noun)
From the book: "Gerund" → When V1+ING is used as a noun.

Example: "I am addicted to teaching." — "teaching" (V1+ING) is acting as a noun here (answers: addicted to what?) → it is a Gerund.

Gerunds can be identified by asking the noun-question (what? / whom?) from the verb. If V1+ING answers that question, it is a Gerund.

WordTypeWhy?
reading (in "I know his reading every night…")GerundActs as a noun — answers "what?" from the verb "know"
borrowing (in "my borrowing your car")GerundActs as a noun — plays the role of a noun in the sentence
staying (in "my staying with you")GerundActs as a noun
waking up (in "my waking up late")GerundActs as a noun — answers "what?"
→ What is a Possessive Adjective?

A Possessive Adjective is a word that shows ownership/belonging (हक दिखाना). It tells you whose something is — and it always comes before a noun.

Possessive AdjectiveUsed withExample
myI / me"my car", "my idea"
ourwe / us"our house"
youryou"your book"
hishe / him"his pen"
hershe / her"her dress"
theirthey / them"their school"
Important distinction: Possessive Adjective ("my", "his") is different from Possessive Pronoun ("mine", "his", "hers", "ours"). Possessive Adjective always comes before a noun, while Possessive Pronoun stands alone.

"My" is the possessive form of the pronoun "I" or "me". For example, "my book" and "my idea" are possessives. The book and the idea belong to me. "Me" is the objective case of the pronoun.
Rule Possessive Adjective is used BEFORE a Gerund

Rule 11 states: We use a Possessive Adjective (my / our / his / her / their / your) before a Gerund.

Possessive Adjective (my/his/her/our/their/your) + Gerund (V1+ING)
Common mistake: Students often use the Objective Pronoun (me, him, her, us, them) before a Gerund instead of the Possessive Adjective (my, his, her, our, their). This is WRONG.
Examples from the Book
✗
I hope you don't mind me borrowing your car.
WRONG — "me" is an Objective Pronoun. Before the gerund "borrowing", use Possessive Adjective "my". [Incorrect]
✓
I hope you don't mind my borrowing your car.
CORRECT — "my" is a Possessive Adjective used before the gerund "borrowing". [Correct]
✓
I know his reading every night has helped him pass the test.
Possessive Adjective "his" before gerund "reading". [Possessive and Gerund — focus is on reading] ✓
✓
I hope you don't mind my staying with you next week.
Possessive Adjective "my" before gerund "staying". [Possessive and Gerund — focus is on staying] ✓
✓
My father doesn't like my waking up late in the morning.
Possessive Adjective "my" before gerund "waking up". ✓
✓
My father doesn't like my going outside with Ram.
Possessive Adjective "my" before gerund "going". ✓
From the book's analysis: In "I hope you don't mind me borrowing your car" vs "I hope you don't mind my borrowing your car" — notice that the only difference is 'me' versus 'my'. "My" is the possessive form of the pronoun 'I' or 'me'. Thus, the second example is correct — the sentence that uses the possessive "my" — because we use a possessive before a Gerund and "borrowing" is a gerund (plays the role of a noun).

📋 Rule 11 — Quick Recap

  • Gerund = V1 + ING used as a noun (borrowing, reading, staying, waking up, going)
  • Rule: Before a Gerund → always use Possessive Adjective (my/our/his/her/their/your)
  • Never use Objective Pronoun (me/us/him/her/them) before a Gerund
  • "I hope you don't mind my borrowing…" ✓ (not "me borrowing") ✗
  • "I know his reading every night…" ✓ (not "him reading") ✗

12

Law of Inversion

Partial Inversion vs Complete Inversion — when a sentence begins with an Adverb

→ What is Inversion?

Inversion means putting the verb before the subject. It is a literary technique in which the normal order of words is reversed, generally for emphasis or special effect.

Normal Sentence structure: Subject + Helping Verb + Main Verb + …
Inversion structure: Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb + … (OR: Adverb + Main Verb + Subject)
Normal Sentence vs Inverted Sentence

NORMAL (Subject first)

→
My students are working very hard.
Subject (My students) → Helping Verb (are) → Main Verb (working) [Normal sentence]

INVERTED (Adverb first)

→
Never have I seen such a beautiful place.
Adverb (Never) → Helping Verb (have) → Subject (I) [Inversion — emphasises the statement]
When is Inversion used? We generally use Inversion when we begin a sentence with an Adverb. Whenever a sentence starts with an Adverb, the Law of Inversion is followed — the Adverb will always be followed by a verb (helping or main), NOT the subject.
⚠️
Exception: If the Adverb is followed by a comma (,), Inversion does NOT apply. The comma breaks the rule.
Example: "Rarely, he goes there." (comma after adverb → no inversion needed)
→ Two Types of Inversion
TypeStructure after AdverbExample
(1) Partial Inversion Adverb + Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb + … Never have I seen such a beautiful place.
Rarely did he go there.
Seldom have I seen such a beautiful view.
(2) Complete Inversion Adverb + Main Verb + Subject + … Here comes the King!
There goes all our money!
💡
Key difference:
Partial Inversion: Adverb → then HELPING verb → then Subject
Complete Inversion: Adverb → then MAIN verb → then Subject (no helping verb between adverb and subject)
1 Partial Inversion — Adverb + Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb
Adverb→ Helping Verb→ Subject→ Main Verb
Examples
✓
Never have I seen such a beautiful place.
Never (Adverb) + have (Helping Verb) + I (Subject) + seen (Main Verb) → Partial Inversion. Used to emphasise: "In my whole lifetime I have never seen such a beautiful place." [Inversion]
✓
Rarely did he go there.
Rarely (Adverb) + did (Helping Verb) + he (Subject) + go (Main Verb) → Partial Inversion. [Adverb + helping verb + subject + main verb] ✓
✓
Seldom have I seen such a beautiful view.
Seldom (= very rarely) (Adverb) + have (Helping Verb) + I (Subject) + seen (Main Verb) → Partial Inversion. ✓
From the book: Consider the above two examples — whenever we begin a sentence with an Adverb, Law of Inversion is followed, according to which Adverb will always be followed by a helping verb. And whenever the Adverb is followed by a helping verb, we call that a Partial Inversion.
2 Complete Inversion — Adverb + Main Verb + Subject
Adverb→ Main Verb→ Subject
Examples
✓
Here comes the King!
Here (Adverb) + comes (Main Verb) + the King (Subject) → Complete Inversion. [Adverb + main verb + subject] ✓
✓
There goes all our money!
There (Adverb) + goes (Main Verb) + all our money (Subject) → Complete Inversion. ✓
From the book: In the case of Complete Inversion, a main verb is used after the Adverb (no helping verb between adverb and subject).
★ Special Cases — Adverbs that commonly trigger Inversion
★ NOTE — "No sooner…than" (NOT "No sooner…when")
"No sooner" is used as an Adverb which is followed by a helping verb. It is to be noted that "No sooner" always comes with "than" and NOT with "when".

Other adverbs: Hardly, Rarely, Scarcely, Barely — these all come with "when".
"No sooner" → paired with "than" only.
"Hardly / Rarely / Scarcely / Barely" → paired with "when".
No Sooner…Than Examples
✗
No sooner I entered the class when it began to rain.
WRONG — Two errors: (1) "No sooner" must trigger inversion → helping verb must come before subject. (2) "No sooner" pairs with "than", never "when". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
No sooner had I entered the class than it began to rain.
CORRECT — Inversion applied: "had" (helping verb) comes before "I" (subject). "than" used (not "when"). ✓ [Correct]
✗
Hardly I entered the class when it began to rain.
WRONG — "Hardly" must trigger inversion → helping verb must come before subject. ✗
✓
Hardly had I entered the class when it began to rain.
CORRECT — Inversion: "had" (helping verb) before "I" (subject). "when" used correctly with "Hardly". ✓
Neither — Inversion Example
✗
He never goes to the temple. Neither she does.
WRONG — "Neither" is an Adverb starting the second sentence. Inversion must apply → helping verb "does" must come BEFORE subject "she". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
He never goes to the temple. Neither does she.
CORRECT — "Neither" (Adverb) → "does" (Helping Verb) → "she" (Subject). Inversion applied. ✓ [Correct]
The sentence is starting with an adverb "Neither" which will follow the law of inversion. Thus, the helping verb "does" will come before the subject "she".

📋 Rule 12 — Quick Recap

  • Inversion = putting verb before subject. Used when a sentence starts with an Adverb (for emphasis).
  • Partial Inversion: Adverb + Helping Verb + Subject + Main Verb (Never have I…, Rarely did he…)
  • Complete Inversion: Adverb + Main Verb + Subject (Here comes the King!)
  • If Adverb is followed by a comma → Inversion does NOT apply
  • "No sooner" → always pairs with "than" (NEVER "when")
  • Hardly / Rarely / Scarcely / Barely → pair with "when"
  • All these adverbs (Never, Seldom, Rarely, Hardly, Scarcely, Barely, Neither, No sooner, Here, There) trigger Inversion when used at the start of a sentence

Rule 9 — Since / For & Simple Past Tense Green
Before Since:Uses Present Perfect or Present Perfect Continuous. E.g. "She has lived / has been living here since 2020."
After Since:Uses Simple Past (V2 only). E.g. "I haven't seen him since he left school."
10
Rule 10 — Past Action Tenses Orange
Two Past Actions:1st action (earlier) → Past Perfect (had + V3). 2nd action (later) → Simple Past (V2). E.g. "The train had left before I reached the station."
One Past Action:Always use Simple Past (V2). Never use Past Perfect for a single action. E.g. "I met him yesterday." (not had met).
11
Rule 11 — Prepositions & Gerunds Purple
Preposition + Noun:Prepositions must be followed by a noun, pronoun, or noun-equivalent (Gerund V1+ing).
Correct Form:Preposition + V1+ing. E.g. "She is good at painting." (not paint). Exception: Infinitive "to + V1".
12
Rule 12 — The Law of Inversion Blue
Partial Inversion:Adverb + HV + Subject + MV. Triggered by: Hardly, Scarcely, Seldom, Rarely, Never, No sooner. E.g. "Rarely did he speak."
Complete Inversion:Adverb + Main Verb + Subject. Triggered by Here/There. E.g. "Here comes the bus."
