Narration — Sequence of Tenses & Its Exceptions

Main clause past → subordinate clause also past · EXCEPT: Universal Truth · Historical Fact · Proverb · Habitual Fact

→ Sequence of Tenses — The General Rule
★ From the book (Rule 39): NARRATION, Sequence of Tenses.
The tense of the verb does not change even if the Reporting verb is in the past tense for the following special cases (exceptions below).

General Rule: If the principal clause (main clause / reporting verb) is in Past Tense, the subordinate clause will also be in Past Tense.
Reporting verb in Past → Reported clause also in Past
will → would  |  can → could  |  may → might  |  shall → should
Basic Sequence of Tenses Examples
✗
I said that I have done it. [Incorrect]
Main clause "said" = past → subordinate must be past. "have" (present) is wrong. ✗
✓
I said that I had done it. [Correct]
"said" = past → "had done" = past perfect. ✓
✓
She told me that she would meet him. [Correct]
"told" = past → "will" becomes "would". ✓
⚠ Exceptions — Tense Does NOT Change (Stays Simple Present)
⚠️
For these 4 types, Sequence of Tenses does NOT apply.
The subordinate clause stays in Simple Present Tense even if the reporting verb is in past tense.
1Universal Truth
A fact that is always true — cannot be put in past.
Sun rises in the east · Honey is sweet · Earth revolves around the sun

The teacher said that the sun rises in the east. ✓
(NOT "rose" — it's always true)
2Historical Fact
An established fact from history — always stated as fact.

The teacher said, "Kalidas is the Shakespeare of India." [Direct]
The teacher said that Kalidas is the Shakespeare of India. [Indirect] ✓

(NOT "was" — it's a historical fact)
3Proverbs / Sayings
Proverbs and कहावतें — always Simple Present.

Rohit said, "Where there is a will there is a way." [Direct]
Rohit said that where there is a will there is a way. [Indirect] ✓

(NOT "was" — proverbs don't change)
4Habitual Facts / Habits
Regular habits or routines — Simple Present.

He said, "I snooze my alarm every day." [Direct]
He said that he snoozes his alarm every day. [Indirect] ✓

(NOT "snoozed" — it's his ongoing habit)
★ The Most Important Concept — "honey is sweet" vs "the honey is sweet"
★ From the book (Rule 39):
"honey is sweet" = Universal Truth (general fact about all honey) → Simple Present stays
"the honey is sweet" = NOT a universal truth — "the honey" refers to a PARTICULAR honey → must follow Sequence of Tenses → past
Four Critical Sentences from Book — honey / the honey
✗
(I) He told me that honey was sweet. [Incorrect]
"honey is sweet" is a universal truth → must stay in Simple Present "is", not "was". ✗
✓
(II) He told me that honey is sweet. [Correct]
Universal truth → Simple Present tense stays regardless of "told". ✓
✗
(III) He told me that the honey is sweet. [Incorrect]
"the honey" = particular honey = NOT a universal truth → must follow sequence of tenses → past. ✗
✓
(IV) He told me that the honey was sweet. [Correct]
"the honey" = specific honey → follows sequence of tenses → "was". ✓
💡
The Key Distinction:
honey is sweet = General fact about honey (all honey) → Universal Truth → Simple Present
the honey is sweet = This particular honey is sweet → NOT a universal truth → Past (was)

✓

3 — Proverb / Saying
✓

Habitual fact / regular routine → Simple Present stays. ✓

📋 Rule 39 — Quick Recap

  • Sequence of Tenses (General): Main clause past → subordinate clause also past. will→would, can→could, may→might, shall→should
  • 4 Exceptions — tense stays Simple Present: (1) Universal Truth (2) Historical Fact (3) Proverbs (4) Habitual Facts
  • "He told me that honey is sweet." ✓ (universal truth) | "He told me that honey was sweet." ✗
  • "He told me that the honey was sweet." ✓ (specific honey = NOT universal truth)
  • "I said that I had done it." ✓ (sequence of tenses — NOT an exception)
  • Proverbs, historical facts, habitual facts → always Simple Present in indirect speech

little / a little / the little  ·  few / a few / the few  ·  some vs any

Uncountable → little · Countable → few · some (positive) vs any (negative / question)

→ Part A: little / a little / the little — with UNCOUNTABLE Nouns
★ From the book (Rule 40): Use of little, the little and a little.
We use these quantifiers with uncountable nouns (things that cannot be counted: milk, water, knowledge, money, rice…)
little
Hardly any
ना के बराबर
NEGATIVE sense ✗
a little
Some but not much
थोड़ा सा
POSITIVE sense ✓
the little
All that is available
जितना है उतना ही
NEUTRAL sense →
Examples — from Book
✓
I cannot make tea since there is little milk. [Correct]
"little" = hardly any milk (ना के बराबर) → negative sense. ✓
✓
A little knowledge is dangerous. [Correct]
"a little" = some but not much (थोड़ी सी) → positive sense. ✓
✓
He utilized the little water that was available. [Correct]
"the little" = all that was available (जितना था उतना) → neutral sense. ✓
Examples — from Class
✓
I was thirsty and thank God there was a little water in my bottle. [Correct]
भगवान का शुक्र है, थोड़ा पानी था (some → positive) → "a little". ✓
✓
Don't expect tea — there is little milk available. [Correct]
चाय की उम्मीद मत करो → ना के बराबर दूध है → "little". ✓
✓
I prepared tea from the little milk that was available. [Correct]
जितना भी था उससे सब बना दिया → "the little". ✓
→ Part B: few / a few / the few — with COUNTABLE Nouns
★ From the book (Rule 40 — NOTE): Use of few, a few and the few.
We use these quantifiers with countable nouns (things that can be counted: members, students, friends, books…)
few
Hardly any
ना के बराबर
NEGATIVE sense ✗
a few
Some but not many
कुछ / थोड़े से
POSITIVE sense ✓
the few
All that are available
जितने हैं उतने ही
NEUTRAL sense →
Examples — from Book
✓
Few members attended the meeting. [Correct]
"few" = hardly any member attended (ना के बराबर) → negative. ✓
✓
There were a few members present. [Correct]
"a few" = some members were present (थोड़े से) → positive. ✓
✓
He lost the few friends he had. [Correct]
"the few" = all those he had — he lost every single one (जितने थे उतने) → neutral. ✓
💡
little vs few — How to choose:
→ Thing cannot be counted (milk, water, money, knowledge, rice) → use little / a little / the little
→ Thing can be counted (members, students, friends, books) → use few / a few / the few

→ Part C: some vs any
★ From the book (Rule 40 — NOTE): Use of "some" and "any".
The general rule is that "any" is used for questions and negative sentences while "some" is used for positive sentences.
Both may be used with countable and uncountable nouns.
WordUsed inExamples
any Questions & Negative sentences Do we need any rice?
No, we don't need any rice.
some Positive sentences We have some rice in the cupboard.
Would you like some water?
⚠️
Special Exception for "some" in Questions:
When we know the answer will definitely be YES, we can use "some" even in questions.
This usually happens when offering something or asking for something you know they have.
Examples — some used in questions (answer expected = YES)
✓
Would you like some water? [Correct]
Offering water — you know they'll say yes → "some" is fine. ✓
✓
May I have some more chocolate? [Correct]
Requesting — answer expected to be yes → "some" is fine. ✓
✓
Do you have some water? [Correct — if answer clearly yes]
You already know they have water → "some" is correct here. ✓
Standard any in questions and negatives
✓
Do we need any rice? [Correct]
Normal question with no expected answer → "any". ✓
✓
No, we don't need any rice. [Correct]
Negative sentence → "any". ✓
✓
We have some rice in the cupboard. [Correct]
Positive sentence → "some". ✓

📋 Rule 40 — Quick Recap

  • little / a little / the little → with UNCOUNTABLE nouns (milk, water, knowledge)
  • little = hardly any (negative) | a little = some (positive) | the little = all available (neutral)
  • few / a few / the few → with COUNTABLE nouns (members, students, friends)
  • few = hardly any (negative) | a few = some (positive) | the few = all available (neutral)
  • some = positive sentences | any = questions & negative sentences
  • Exception: "some" can be used in questions when the answer is expected to be YES
  • Both "some" and "any" work with countable and uncountable nouns
