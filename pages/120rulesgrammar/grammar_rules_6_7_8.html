6

Units of Nouns

4 concepts — Number+Unit+Noun, Self-explanatory units, Indefinite units & Arbitrary units

→ What is a Unit?

A Unit is something with the help of which we count or measure things.

For example: if I have 5 pens, the word "pens" is what I am counting, and the number "5" tells how many. The measuring tool (like kilogram, centimetre, metre, dozen, rupee, inch, litre) is called the Unit.

Examples of UnitsWhat they measure
Kilogram, gram, poundWeight
Centimetre, metre, inch, foot, mileDistance / Length
Rupee, dollar, poundMoney
Dozen, hundred, thousand, lakh, millionQuantity
Litre, cup, glass, spoonVolume / Amount
Concept 1 Number + Unit + Noun / Adjective → Unit is ALWAYS SINGULAR

When you have the combination Number + Unit + Noun or Number + Unit + Adjective, the Unit is always Singular — never plural.

Number + Unit (Singular) + Noun   |   Number + Unit (Singular) + Adjective
Simple rule: If there is a noun or adjective right after the unit — unit stays singular. The combination acts as a single describing phrase.
Examples
✓
He saw a ten-inch snake.
Number (10) + Unit (inch — singular) + Noun (snake). Unit is singular. [Unit – Singular] ✓
✗
He saw a ten-inches snake.
WRONG — Unit "inches" is plural. With Number + Unit + Noun, unit must always be singular "inch". ✗
✓
She gave me two five-hundred-rupee notes.
Number (two) + Unit (rupee — singular) + Noun (notes). Unit "rupee" stays singular. ✓
✓
She is a ten-year-old child.
Number (10) + Unit (year — singular) + Adjective (old). Unit "year" stays singular. ✓
✗
She is a ten-years-old child.
WRONG — Unit "years" is plural. Must be singular "year" when followed by an adjective/noun. ✗
Concept 2 Number + Unit (alone, no Noun/Adjective) → Depends on whether Unit is Self-Explanatory

When you have only Number + Unit with nothing after (no noun, no adjective), the form of the unit depends on whether it is self-explanatory or not.

Type of UnitMeaningFormExample
Self-Explanatory / Self-Sufficient The unit's meaning is clear on its own — no extra word needed to understand what's being talked about PLURAL I have five rupees. — "rupees" tells us we're talking about money. Clear! ✓
NOT Self-Explanatory The unit's meaning is unclear — you still don't know what is being counted SINGULAR I have ten dozen. — "dozen" tells us nothing. Dozen of what? Unclear. ✓
Examples — Self-Explanatory (Plural Unit)
✓
I have five rupees.
"Rupees" is self-explanatory — everyone knows we're talking about money. → Plural Unit used. [Plural Unit] ✓
Examples — NOT Self-Explanatory (Singular Unit)
✓
He had five dozen.
"Dozen" alone doesn't tell us — dozen of what? Eggs? Pens? Unclear → Not self-explanatory → Singular Unit used. [Singular Unit] ✓
✗
10 millions had been found there.
WRONG — "Million" alone doesn't explain what is being counted (people? rupees? marks?). Not self-explanatory → Unit must be SINGULAR → "10 million". ✗
✓
10 million had been found there.
CORRECT — "Million" is not self-explanatory → Singular form used. ✓
💡
Quick test: Ask yourself — "Does this unit alone tell me WHAT is being talked about?"
YES → Self-explanatory → Plural (rupees, dollars, kilometres)
NO → Not self-explanatory → Singular (dozen, million, hundred)
Concept 3 Indefinite Units — Connected with "OF" → Always Plural + Ascending Order

Sometimes we don't know the exact number — we say "hundreds of", "thousands of", "lakhs of". These are called Indefinite Units (not definite / not exact).

Two rules for Indefinite Units:
1. When units are connected with the word "of" → units are ALWAYS PLURAL.
2. When more than one unit is used → they must always be arranged in ASCENDING ORDER (smaller unit first → larger unit after).
Rule 1 — Units with "of" → Always Plural
✓
Hundreds of thousands of students attended the class.
"Hundreds" and "thousands" are plural because they are connected with "of". ✓
✓
Thousands of lakhs of people work very hard.
Both units plural (connected with "of"). Also in ascending order (thousands → lakhs). ✓
Rule 2 — Ascending Order (Smaller → Larger)
✗
Lakhs of thousands of people work very hard.
WRONG — "Lakhs" is a bigger unit than "thousands". Units must be in ASCENDING ORDER: smaller first → "Thousands of lakhs". ✗
✓
Thousands of lakhs of people work very hard.
CORRECT — Ascending order: thousands (smaller) → lakhs (larger). ✓
★ NOTE from the book: If there is more than one Unit in a sentence, Units are always arranged in ascending order.
Ascending order: tens → hundreds → thousands → lakhs → millions → billions

Example: "Thousands of lakhs of students took the test." [Units arranged in ascending order]
Concept 4 Arbitrary (Random) Units — Handful, Glassful, Fistful etc.

Some units are not standardised — they vary from person to person, home to home, country to country. These are called Arbitrary Units (also called Random Units).

Why "arbitrary"? A "glassful" in my house may be 200ml. In yours it might be 500ml. A "handful" depends on the size of your hand. A "fistful" depends on your fist. These units have no fixed, universal measurement — they change from person to person.
Common Arbitrary Units — Singular & Plural forms
★ Key Rule: When forming the PLURAL of an arbitrary unit, always add 'S' at the END of the whole word — never in the middle.
handful
Plural: handfuls
Open palm full of something
glassful
Plural: glassfuls
A full glass of liquid
fistful
Plural: fistfuls
A closed fist full
spoonful
Plural: spoonfuls
A full spoon of something
mouthful
Plural: mouthfuls
As much as fills the mouth
cupful
Plural: cupfuls
A full cup of something
Common mistake: Do NOT say "glassesfuls" or "glassesfull" — the 'S' goes at the very END only.
✗ two glassesfuls    ✗ two glasses full    ✓ two glassfuls
Examples from the book
✓
He pulled out a handful of coins from his pocket.
"Handful" = singular arbitrary unit. [Unit – Singular] ✓
✓
I use two handfuls of pasta per person.
"Handfuls" = plural form (S added at the end). [Unit – Plural] ✓
✓
Drink a glassful of water with this medicine.
"Glassful" = singular. [Unit – Singular] ✓
✓
She drank two whole glassfuls of orange juice.
"Glassfuls" = plural (S at the end). [Unit – Plural] ✓
✓
He has a fistful of acting awards.
"Fistful" = singular. [Unit – Singular] ✓
✓
All of a sudden, we just started having fistfuls of money.
"Fistfuls" = plural (S at the end). [Unit – Plural] ✓

📋 Rule 6 — Quick Recap

  • Concept 1: Number + Unit + Noun/Adjective → Unit is ALWAYS SINGULAR (ten-inch snake, five-hundred-rupee note, ten-year-old child)
  • Concept 2: Number + Unit alone → Self-explanatory unit = PLURAL (five rupees); Not self-explanatory = SINGULAR (five dozen, 10 million)
  • Concept 3: Indefinite units + "of" → Units ALWAYS PLURAL; Multiple units → arranged in ASCENDING ORDER (thousands of lakhs, not lakhs of thousands)
  • Concept 4: Arbitrary units (handful, glassful, fistful, spoonful, mouthful, cupful) → Plural formed by adding 'S' at the END → handfuls, glassfuls, fistfuls, spoonfuls, mouthfuls, cupfuls

7

Expressions of Time, Money, Speed, Weight, Height & Distance

Singular vs. Plural verb — depends on ONE way or VARIOUS ways

→ The Core Rule

Expressions of time, money, speed, weight, height and distance usually take a Singular verb — but this changes depending on how they are used.

SituationVerbExplanation
Expression used / covered in ONE way Singular verb The whole amount is treated as a single unit — one thing
Expression used / covered in VARIOUS ways Plural verb The amount is split across multiple ways — multiple things
1 Distance — One way vs. Various ways
✓
Ten miles needs to be covered completely on foot.
[Singular verb] — The complete distance (ten miles) is covered by just ONE means (on foot) → treated as one unit → singular verb "needs". ✓
✓
Ten miles need to be covered partly by car and rest on foot.
[Plural verb] — The complete distance (ten miles) has to be covered by TWO means (by car AND on foot) → multiple ways → plural verb "need". ✓
2 Money — One purpose vs. Various purposes
✓
Five thousand rupees is his salary.
[Singular verb] — The money (₹5000) is used/referred to for ONE purpose (salary) → treated as one single amount → singular "is". ✓
✓
Five thousand rupees were spent on food and entertainment.
[Plural verb] — The money (₹5000) was spent on MULTIPLE things (food AND entertainment) → various ways → plural "were". ✓
3 Time & Weight — Single unit = Singular verb
✓
Six weeks is not long enough.
[Singular verb] — Six weeks is treated as one block of time → singular verb "is". ✓
✓
Five kilograms is his weight.
[Singular verb] — Five kilograms treated as one measurement unit → singular "is". ✓

📋 Rule 7 — Quick Recap

  • Expressions of time, money, speed, weight, height and distance → usually take a Singular verb
  • Used / covered in ONE way → Singular verb (Five thousand rupees is his salary)
  • Used / covered in VARIOUS ways → Plural verb (Five thousand rupees were spent on food and entertainment)
  • One means of travel / one purpose / one unit treated together → always singular
  • Multiple means / multiple purposes / amount split → plural verb

Certain Cases of Comparisons

You cannot compare something with itself — use "any other" when needed

→ The Core Logic of Comparison

When comparing something with a group it belongs to, you must use "any other" — because you cannot logically compare something with itself.

Key principle: If Subject A is a member of the group being compared → use "any other" before the group word.
If Subject A is NOT a member of the group being compared → use "all" is fine.
🤔
The logic: If Gold is "more precious than all metals" — but Gold itself IS a metal — that comparison would mean "Gold is more precious than Gold itself", which is absurd. So we say "any other metal" to exclude Gold from the group.
1 Gold vs. Metals — A member comparing with its own group
✗
Gold is more precious than all metals.
WRONG — Gold IS a metal. Comparing gold with "all metals" includes gold itself. This makes the sentence mean "Gold is more precious than gold itself" — meaningless! [Incorrect]
✓
Gold is more precious than any other metal.
CORRECT — "Any other metal" excludes gold from the comparison. Now gold is being compared with all OTHER metals. [Correct]
Analysis (from the book): In the first sentence, "Gold" is itself a metal. At a point of time, comparing gold with "all metals" includes gold itself — making the meaning "Gold is more precious than gold", which makes the sentence wrong.

In the second sentence, gold is compared with any other metals — meaning gold is precious than every other metal except itself. This makes the statement meaningful and logical.
2 Diamond vs. Metals — NOT a member of the group
✓
Diamond is more precious than all metals.
CORRECT — Diamond is NOT a metal. It is a non-metal being compared to all metals. Since diamond is not part of the group "metals", the comparison is logical and valid. ✓ [Correct]
Why is this correct? The above sentence is correct as Diamond, being a non-metal, is compared to all metals. Diamond is not in the group "metals" — so there is no risk of comparing it with itself. "All metals" is perfectly fine here.
3 Kalidasa vs. Poets — Apply the same logic

Key fact needed: Kalidasa was an Indian Sanskrit poet — he was NOT an English poet.

✓
Kalidasa was more famous than all English poets.
CORRECT — Kalidasa was NOT an English poet. He is being compared to "all English poets" — a group he doesn't belong to. No risk of self-comparison. [Correct]
✗
Kalidasa was more famous than all poets.
WRONG — "All poets" includes Kalidasa himself (since Kalidasa is a poet). So it compares Kalidasa with himself — meaningless! Must say "any other poet". [Incorrect]
From the book:
In sentence (IV), Kalidasa is compared with all English poets — which is correct as Kalidasa was not an English poet.

However, in sentence (V), Kalidasa is compared with all poets — which includes even Kalidasa. This makes the sentence wrong or meaningless.
→ Complete Summary — When to use "any other" vs "all"

📋 Rule 8 — Quick Recap

  • When comparing subject with a group it belongs to → use "any other" (to exclude the subject from the group)
  • When comparing subject with a group it does NOT belong to → use "all" is perfectly fine
  • Gold is more precious than any other metal ✓ (Gold IS a metal → use "any other")
  • Diamond is more precious than all metals ✓ (Diamond is NOT a metal → "all" is fine)
  • Kalidasa was more famous than all English poets ✓ (Kalidasa was NOT English → "all" is fine)
  • Kalidasa was more famous than any other poet ✓ (Kalidasa IS a poet → "any other" needed)

Rule 6 — Units of Nouns Teal Blue
Definite Units:Number + Unit + Noun/Adj → Unit is ALWAYS Singular. E.g. "A ten-rupee note" (not ten-rupees).
Indefinite Units:Unit + of + Plural Noun/Pronoun → Unit is PLURAL. E.g. "Thousands of people" (not thousand).
Arbitrary Units:Noun + suffix "-ful". Pluralized at the end. E.g. "Spoonfuls", "Handfuls" (never spoonsful).

Rule 7 — Expressions of Time/Money/Distance Purple
One Way Usage:Amount/expression treated as a single collective unit → Singular Verb. E.g. "Three years is a long time."
Various Ways Usage:Amount/expression split or spent on different things/times → Plural Verb. E.g. "Ten thousand rupees were spent on food and clothing."

Rule 8 — Comparisons & Exclusions Green
Belongs to Group:Compare subject with a group it belongs to → use "any other [Noun]". E.g. "Gold is more precious than any other metal."
Does Not Belong:Compare subject with a group it doesn't belong to → use "all [Noun]s". E.g. "Diamond is more precious than all metals."
