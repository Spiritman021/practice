One of the / Each of the — Noun & Verb Rules

4 key concepts + 1 important exception (Only one of the)

→ The General Rule — "One of the"

The phrase "One of the" follows this structure:

"One of the" = the + PLURAL NOUN + SINGULAR/PLURAL VERB
(whether verb is singular or plural depends on what follows — see concepts below)

First and most important thing to remember:
Whenever you use "One of the" or "Each of the", the noun that follows is ALWAYS PLURAL — no exceptions.

StructureNoun after itCorrect form
One of the ___Always PluralOne of the students / One of the books
Each of the ___Always PluralEach of the students / Each of the animals
✗
One of the student is good.
WRONG — After "One of the", noun must always be plural: students
✓
One of the students is good.
CORRECT — Plural noun "students" used after "One of the". ✓
1 Concept 1 — "One of the" + Plural Noun + Who/Which/That → PLURAL Verb

When "One of the" + plural noun is followed by a relative pronoun (who / which / that), the verb that comes after the relative pronoun is always PLURAL.

Why? The relative pronoun (who/which/that) acts as the subject for the verb that follows it — and it refers back to the plural noun. So the verb must agree with the plural noun → plural verb.
💡
Formula: One of the + [Plural Noun] + who/which/that + Plural Verb (V1)
Examples
✓
He is one of the men who think that they can do anything.
"who" refers to "men" (plural) → verb must be plural → "think" (not "thinks"). ✓
✓
She is one of the girls who think that everything is possible.
"who" refers to "girls" (plural) → plural verb "think". ✓
✓
That is one of the birds that fly over my house at night.
"that" refers to "birds" (plural) → plural verb "fly". [Book example]
"that" here acts as the subject for the verb "fly" → "that" refers to birds (plural noun) → plural verb "fly" is used.
✓
She is one of the mothers who are really working hard to make both ends meet.
"who" acts as the subject for the verb "are" → "who" refers to mothers (plural noun) → plural verb "are". [Book example] ✓
✓
One of the birds which fly over my house is very colourful.
"which" refers to "birds" (plural) → plural verb "fly". ✓
2 Concept 2 — "One of the" + Plural Noun (no Who/Which/That) → SINGULAR Verb

When "One of the" + plural noun is NOT followed by a relative pronoun (who/which/that), the main verb is SINGULAR.

Why? Without a relative pronoun, the subject of the main verb is "one" — which is singular. So the verb agrees with "one" → singular verb.
💡
Formula: One of the + [Plural Noun] (no who/which/that) + Singular Verb (V1+s/es)
Examples
✓
One of the birds is red.
No who/which/that → subject is "one" (singular) → singular verb "is". [Book example] ✓
✓
One of her desires is to become a doctor.
No who/which/that → singular verb "is". ✓
✗
One of the birds are red.
WRONG — No relative pronoun after the noun → verb must be singular "is", not plural "are". ✗
→ Concepts 1 & 2 Side by Side — The Key Difference
Structure Relative Pronoun? Verb Example
One of the + Plural Noun + who/which/that ✅ YES Plural (V1) "One of the birds that fly…"
One of the + Plural Noun ❌ NO Singular (V1+s/es) "One of the birds is red."
⚠ Exception / Concept 3 — "Only One of the" → ALWAYS Singular Verb

This is the most important exception in Rule 5. When "Only" is added before "One of the", the entire rule changes:

Exception Rule: "Only one of the" + Plural Noun → Verb is ALWAYS SINGULAR
— Whether who/which/that is present or not → verb is ALWAYS SINGULAR
— No concept applies here. One word "Only" changes everything.
StructureWho/Which/That?Verb
Only one of the + Plural Noun + who/which/that ✅ Present SINGULAR always
Only one of the + Plural Noun ❌ Absent SINGULAR always
Examples — "Only one of the" → Singular verb always
✓
Rina is the only one of the girls who has ever been to India.
"Only one of the" + who → but verb is SINGULAR "has" (not "have"). [Book example] ✓
✓
This is the only one of the novels which is interesting.
"Only one of the" + which → but verb is SINGULAR "is" (not "are"). [Book example] ✓
✗
Rina is the only one of the girls who have ever been to India.
WRONG — With "Only one of the", verb must always be singular "has", never plural "have". ✗
💡
Memory trick: The word "only" = "one single thing" → always singular.
Think: "Only one = just one = singular". The "only" cancels out any plural effect.
4 All 4 Concepts Together — Complete Summary
# Structure Noun Who/Which/That? Verb
1 One of the + Noun + who/which/that Always Plural ✅ YES PLURAL (V1)
2 One of the + Noun (alone) Always Plural ❌ NO SINGULAR (V1+s/es)
3 Only one of the + Noun + who/which/that Always Plural ✅ YES SINGULAR always ⚠
4 Only one of the + Noun (alone) Always Plural ❌ NO SINGULAR always ⚠
★ The ONE thing that never changes (across all 4 concepts):
The noun after "One of the" / "Each of the" / "Only one of the" is ALWAYS PLURAL — in every single case, no matter what else changes.

📋 Rule 5 — Quick Recap

  • After "One of the" / "Each of the" → noun is ALWAYS PLURAL (One of the students / Each of the books)
  • Concept 1: One of the + Plural Noun + who/which/that → PLURAL verb
  • Concept 2: One of the + Plural Noun (no who/which/that) → SINGULAR verb
  • Exception: Only one of the + Plural Noun → verb is ALWAYS SINGULAR — whether who/which/that is present or not, doesn't matter
  • The word "only" overrides everything — whenever "only" appears, ignore Concepts 1 & 2 and use singular verb

Rule 5 — One of the / Each of the 4 Key Concepts
Structure Noun Form Who/Which/That? Verb Form
One of the Always Plural ✅ YES PLURAL
One of the Always Plural ❌ NO SINGULAR
Only one of the Always Plural ✅ YES or ❌ NO SINGULAR always ⚠
Each of the Always Plural - SINGULAR always
Key Trick 1:The noun following "One of the", "Each of the", or "Only one of the" is ALWAYS PLURAL. (e.g. "One of the students", NOT "student").
Key Trick 2:If relative pronoun (who/which/that) is present, verb becomes plural because it refers back to the plural noun.
The "Only" Exception:"Only one of the" makes the context point to a single subject → verb is always singular.
Summary: Plural Noun is a constant. Rel Pronoun? → Plural Verb. No Rel Pronoun? → Singular Verb. "Only" added? → Singular Verb always.
