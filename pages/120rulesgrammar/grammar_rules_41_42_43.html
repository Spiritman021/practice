Objective Case of Pronoun after Preposition & Verb

Preposition + Objective Pronoun · Verb + Objective Pronoun · NEVER Subjective after preposition

→ Subjective vs Objective Case — The Full Table
★ From the book (Rule 41): Objective Case of the pronoun comes after the Preposition.
Subjective Case (before Verb)Objective Case (after Preposition / Verb)
I→me
We→us
You→you
They→them
He→him
She→her
It→it
Preposition + Objective Case (always)  |  Verb + Objective Case (always)
Subjective Case NEVER comes after a preposition
→ "but" and "except" as Prepositions → Objective Case
★ From the book: *NOTE: "But" plays the role of both "Conjunction" and "Preposition".
When "but" means लेकिन → Conjunction. When "but" means सिवाय / अलावा → Preposition → Objective Case follows.
✗
None but he is going to the party. [Incorrect]
"but" here means "except" = preposition → must use objective "him". ✗
✓
None but him is going to the party. [Correct]
"but" = preposition (except) → objective case "him". ✓
✗
All except he liked the performance. [Incorrect]
"except" = preposition → must use objective "him". ✗
✓
All except him liked the performance. [Correct]
"except" = preposition → objective case "him". ✓
→ Verb also takes Objective Case ("Let" examples)
★ From the book: A Verb is also followed by the pronoun of Objective case.
✓
Let him do that work. [Correct]
"Let" is a verb → followed by objective case "him". ✓
✓
They agreed to let us live there rent-free. [Correct]
"Let" is a verb → followed by objective case "us". ✓
→ "as well as" vs "between" — Important Distinction
✓
Ram as well as he is going to the party. [Correct]
"as well as" = Conjunction (NOT a preposition) → subjective case "he" is correct here. ✓
✓
There is a good friendship between you and him. [Correct]
"between" = preposition → both pronouns in objective case (you, him). ✓
💡
Quick check:
→ Is the word a preposition? → Objective Case follows (me, us, him, her, them).
→ Is the word a conjunction like "as well as"? → Subjective Case (I, he, she, we, they).
→ After a verb? → Objective Case (let him, let us).

📋 Rule 41 — Quick Recap

  • Preposition + Objective Case always: between you and him ✓ | except him ✓ | but him ✓ (when "but" = except)
  • Verb + Objective Case always: Let him do it ✓ | Let us go ✓
  • "as well as" = conjunction → Subjective: "Ram as well as he" ✓
  • "None but him" ✓ | "All except him" ✓ | "between you and him" ✓
  • "but" = conjunction (लेकिन) → no case rule | "but" = preposition (सिवाय) → Objective Case

Order of Pronouns — 2-3-1 (Positive) & 1-2-3 (Negative / Mistakes)

Positive sense → You, He, I · Negative / Mistake / Blame → I, You, He · All plural → 1-2-3 always

→ Person Reference Table
★ From the book (Rule 42): In case of pronouns as the Subject, when all the three persons are used as the Subject of the sentence and are connected by "and", the sequence of 231 of the Pronouns is followed if they give a Positive Sense to the sentence and a plural verb is used.
NumberPersonPronoun
1Speaker — First PersonI / We
2Listener — Second PersonYou
3Non-Active — Third PersonHe / She / They
→ The Two Orders — Visual
✓ POSITIVE Sense → 2-3-1
2 → 3 → 1
You → He → I
Good things, achievements, positive qualities → give credit to others first, yourself last.
✗ NEGATIVE Sense → 1-2-3
1 → 2 → 3
I → You → He
Mistakes, guilt, crimes, blame → take responsibility yourself first, then others.
💡
APJ Abdul Kalam Rule (from class):
When the team does something good → "My team did it" (give credit → 2-3-1).
When a mistake is made → "I made the mistake" (take blame → 1-2-3).
English is ethical — always take blame yourself first!
★ All Examples from Book — Case-by-Case
Case 1 — All 3 pronouns · Positive sense → 2-3-1 · Plural verb
✗
I, you and he are going to the party. [Incorrect — positive sense]
Positive → order must be 2-3-1 (You, He, I). ✗
✓
You, he and I are good friends. [Correct]
Positive sense → 2-3-1 order → "You, he, I" → plural verb "are". ✓
Case 2 — Two pronouns · Positive sense → pairs: (2,3) · (2,1) · (3,1)
✓
You and she are good friends. [Correct — sequence (2,3)]
2nd person first, 3rd person second. Positive → plural verb "are". ✓
✓
You and I are playing chess. [Correct — sequence (2,1)]
2nd person first, 1st person second. ✓
✓
He and I were watching T.V. [Correct — sequence (3,1)]
3rd person first, 1st person second. Positive → plural verb. ✓
Case 3 — All 3 pronouns · Negative sense (mistake/guilt) → 1-2-3 · Plural verb
✓
I, you and he are guilty. [Correct — sequence 1-2-3]
Negative (guilty) → 1-2-3 order. Plural verb "are". ✓
✓
I, you and he are robbers. [Correct — sequence 1-2-3]
Negative (crime) → 1-2-3 order. ✓
Case 4 — Two pronouns · Negative sense → pairs: (1,2) · (2,3) · (1,3)
✓
I and you have committed a crime. [Correct — sequence (1,2)]
Negative (crime) → 1st person first. ✓
✓
You and he are sinners. [Correct — sequence (2,3)]
Negative with 2nd and 3rd person only → (2,3). ✓
✓
I and he were guilty. [Correct — sequence (1,3)]
Negative → 1st person first (1,3). ✓
Case 5 — All pronouns plural → always 1-2-3 (We, You, They)
✓
We, you and they are going to watch a movie. [Correct — sequence 1-2-3]
All plural pronouns → always use 1-2-3 order regardless of positive/negative. ✓

📋 Rule 42 — Quick Recap

  • Positive sense → 2-3-1: "You, he and I are good friends." ✓
  • Negative/mistake/blame → 1-2-3: "I, you and he are guilty." ✓
  • All plural pronouns (We, You, They) → always 1-2-3 in all cases
  • All verbs must be plural when multiple pronouns are subjects
  • Positive two-person pairs: (2,3) (2,1) (3,1) · Negative two-person pairs: (1,2) (2,3) (1,3)

Superfluous Errors — Extra Words that Make Sentences Wrong

Superfluous = more than required in the sentence · It means EXTRA · Remove the redundant word

→ What is Superfluous?
★ From the book (Rule 43): Some Superfluousness Errors.
Superfluous means that more than required is given in the sentence. It means extra.
When a word's meaning is already contained in another word of the phrase, using both together creates a superfluous error.
★ All Numbered Examples from Book (I–XII)
I — previous records
✗
India broke all previous records in today's match. [Incorrect]
"records" always specifies "previous" → "previous" is superfluous. ✗
✓
India broke all records in today's match. [Correct]
Remove "previous" — it's already implied in "records". ✓
II — wet water
✗
I want wet water. [Incorrect]
Water is always wet — "wet" is superfluous. ✗
✓
I want water. [Correct]
Remove "wet" — water is always wet. ✓
III — suppose if
✗
Suppose if you won the Oscar, what would you do? [Incorrect]
"suppose" and "if" both indicate condition → using both is superfluous. ✗
✓
Suppose you won the Oscar, what would you do? [Correct]
Use only "suppose" or only "if" — never both. ✓
*NOTE: "Supposing if" is also wrong. Use only 'supposing' (without "if").
IV — retreated back
✗
The army retreated back from the conflict zone. [Incorrect]
"Retreat" itself means to withdraw/go back → "back" is superfluous. ✗
✓
The army retreated from the conflict zone. [Correct]
Remove "back" — retreat already means come back. ✓
V — reborn again
✗
The sage was reborn again in Vijayanagar. [Incorrect]
"re-" prefix already means "again" → "again" is superfluous. ✗
✓
The sage was reborn in Vijayanagar. [Correct]
Remove "again" — "re" already means "again". ✓
*NOTE: Never use "again" after — Rebirth / Reborn / Rebuild / Regain / Reiterate / Recast etc. The prefix "re-" = "again".
VI — equally as good as
✗
Manu is equally as good as Sharma. [Incorrect]
"equally" already means "as…as" → using both is superfluous. ✗
✓
Manu and Sharma are equally good. / Manu is as good as Sharma. [Correct]
Use EITHER "equally" OR "as…as" — never both together. ✓
VII — mutual agreement
✗
They decided on a mutual agreement. [Incorrect]
"Agreement" is always mutual → "mutual" is superfluous. ✗
✓
They decided on an agreement. [Correct]
Remove "mutual". ✓ Note: say "common friend" not "mutual friend" | "common interest" not "mutual interest".
VIII — forbade not
✗
She forbade me not to leave her alone. [Incorrect]
"Forbade/forbid" already has negative meaning → "not" is superfluous. ✗
✓
She forbade me to leave her alone. [Correct]
"Forbade" = prevented/denied → no "not" needed. ✓
*NOTE: "Not" is NEVER used after — until / unless / forbid / prohibit / prevent / dissuade / deny / refuse / lest — all these already contain the negation.
IX — coward man
✗
He's a coward man/person. [Incorrect]
"Coward" itself means "a person who lacks courage" → "man/person" is superfluous. ✗
✓
He is a coward. [Correct]
Remove "man/person". ✓
X — with bag and baggage
✗
She left the house with bag and baggage. [Incorrect]
"Bag and baggage" is an idiom meaning "with all belongings" → "with" is superfluous. ✗
✓
She left the house, bag and baggage. [Correct]
Idiom already means "with all belongings" → remove "with". ✓
XI — passing marks / linking language / dressing sense
✗
Passing marks are thirty-three. [Incorrect]
Wrong phrase. ✗
✓
Pass marks are thirty-three. [Correct]
Correct: pass marks / link language / link network / dress sense. ✓
Wrong: passing marks · linking language · linking network · dressing sense
Correct: pass marks · link language · link network · dress sense
XII — suddenly came across
✗
I suddenly came across him on the train. [Incorrect]
"Come across" already means to meet suddenly → "suddenly" is superfluous. ✗
✓
I came across him on the train. [Correct]
Remove "suddenly" — "come across" already means to meet suddenly. ✓

📋 Rule 43 — Quick Recap

  • Superfluous = extra words whose meaning is already contained in another word in the phrase
  • Negation words (forbid/prohibit/prevent/deny/refuse/unless/until/lest) → NEVER add "not"
  • "re-" prefix words (reborn/rebuild/regain/reiterate/recast) → NEVER add "again"
  • Either "suppose" OR "if" — never "suppose if" together
  • Either "equally" OR "as good as" — never "equally as good as"
  • "come across" already means suddenly → no need to add "suddenly"
  • Wrong: mutual friend, passing marks, dressing sense · Correct: common friend, pass marks, dress sense

Rule 41 — Objective Case after Preposition / Verb Pronouns
Core Rule:Preposition → always Objective Case (me/us/him/her/them). Verb → also Objective Case. Never Subjective after preposition.
Pronoun Table:I→me · We→us · He→him · She→her · They→them · You→you · It→it
Wrong:"None but he" ✗ · "All except he liked…" ✗ · "between you and he" ✗
Correct:"None but him" ✓ · "except him" ✓ · "between you and him" ✓ · "Let him do it" ✓ · "Let us go" ✓
Exception:"as well as" = conjunction (not preposition) → Subjective: "Ram as well as he is going…" ✓

Rule 42 — Order of Pronouns 2-3-1 / 1-2-3 Pronoun Order
Positive sense:2-3-1 → "You, he and I are good friends." ✓ | "(2,3): You and she" · "(2,1): You and I" · "(3,1): He and I"
Negative/mistake:1-2-3 → "I, you and he are guilty." ✓ | "(1,2): I and you" · "(2,3): You and he" · "(1,3): I and he"
All plural:Always 1-2-3 → "We, you and they are going…" ✓
APJ Kalam Rule:Good things → credit others (2-3-1) | Mistakes → blame yourself first (1-2-3)
