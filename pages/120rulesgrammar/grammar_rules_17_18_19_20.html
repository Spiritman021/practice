17

Verb Agrees with the First Subject

When subjects are connected by quasi-coordinators, verb always follows the first subject

→ The Core Rule

Whenever two subjects are connected by any of the following words/phrases, the verb will always and always agree with the first subject only. The second subject (after the connector) is treated as additional information and has no effect on the verb.

★ From the book (Rule 17): Whenever the two subjects are connected by "as well as, together with, along with, in addition to, not, unlike, like, with, rather than, except, no less than, nothing but, more than one", the verb agrees with the first Subject.
First Subject + [Connector] + Second Subject → Verb agrees with FIRST Subject only
→ All Connectors (Quasi-Coordinators)

Any of the following words/phrases triggers this rule:

as well as together with along with in addition to not unlike like with rather than except no less than nothing but more than one governed by accompanied by but besides led by run by and not
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
as well as — Verb follows First Subject
✓
John as well as Mary wants to drop the course.
First subject = John (singular) → singular verb "wants". Mary has no effect on verb. ✓
✓
Ram as well as his friends keeps asking me many questions.
First subject = Ram (singular) → singular verb "keeps". ✓
✓
The publisher, as well as the writers, has come.
First subject = publisher (singular) → singular verb "has". ✓
along with — Verb follows First Subject
✓
California, along with Florida, is among the most popular US tourist destinations.
First subject = California (singular) → singular verb "is". ✓
✓
The teachers, along with the Principal, are entering the class.
First subject = teachers (plural) → plural verb "are". [Subject is plural in this case] ✓
✗
The club members, along with his owner, was arrested.
WRONG — First subject = club members (plural) → plural verb needed. ✗
✓
The club members, along with his owner, were arrested.
CORRECT — plural verb "were" for plural first subject "club members". ✓
and not / not — Verb follows First Subject
✗
He and not his friends, are found guilty.
WRONG — First subject = He (singular) → singular verb needed. ✗
✓
He and not his friends, is found guilty.
CORRECT — "He" is first subject (singular) → "is". ✓
Club members — along with owner — plural verb
✓
The club members, along with the owner, were arrested.
Club members = plural first subject → "were". Along with / accompanied by / led by / run by = same rule. ✓
His parents not — Verb follows His parents
✓
His parents, not the child, are responsible.
First subject = His parents (plural) → plural verb "are". ✓
💡
Quick Trick: Find the first subject (before any connector). Check if it is singular or plural. Verb matches that — completely ignore the second part.

Singular first subject → Singular verb (V1 + s/es)
Plural first subject → Plural verb (V1 without s/es)

📋 Rule 17 — Quick Recap

  • Connectors: as well as, together with, along with, in addition to, not, and not, unlike, like, with, besides, accompanied by, governed by, led by, run by, but, except, nothing but, no less than, more than one, rather than
  • Verb agrees always and always with the First Subject only.
  • "John as well as Mary wants" ✓ (John = singular first subject)
  • "Teachers along with the Principal are" ✓ (teachers = plural first subject)
  • "He and not his friends is found guilty" ✓ (He = singular first subject)

✏️ Practice — Singular or Plural Verb?

✓
Ram, as well as his friends, keeps asking questions.
First subject = Ram (singular) → "keeps" ✓
✓
All men, unlike Ram, go to college every day.
First subject = All men (plural) → "go" ✓
✗
Ram, together with his girlfriends, are going to Goa.
WRONG — First subject = Ram (singular) → "is" needed. ✗
✓
Ram, together with his girlfriends, is going to Goa.
First subject = Ram (singular) → "is" ✓
18

Noun vs Verb Word Pairs

Advice/Advise · Practice/Practise · Belief/Believe — different spelling, different function

→ The Core Rule

Certain words exist in two forms — one as a Noun and one as a Verb. They have similar spellings but different pronunciation and different grammatical roles.

Noun → answers "What?" or "Who?" (name of a thing/person)
Verb → answers "Do What?" (action performed)
→ The Word Pairs
Pair 1 — Advice (Noun) vs Advise (Verb)
ADVICE — Noun (–ce)
  • Meaning: सलाह (Salah)
  • Answers: "What?" (व्हाट का आंसर)
  • Pronunciation: ad-VICE (s sound)
  • e.g. "He gave me a piece of advice."
ADVISE — Verb (–se)
  • Meaning: सलाह देना (action)
  • Answers: "Do What?" (क्रिया)
  • Pronunciation: ad-VIZE (z sound)
  • e.g. "I advise you to practise a lot."
Pair 2 — Practice (Noun) vs Practise (Verb)
PRACTICE — Noun (–ce)
  • Meaning: अभ्यास
  • Answers: "What?" (name/thing)
  • e.g. "Practice makes a man perfect."
PRACTISE — Verb (–se)
  • Meaning: अभ्यास करना (action)
  • Answers: "Do What?"
  • e.g. "I practise daily."
Pair 3 — Belief (Noun) vs Believe (Verb)
BELIEF — Noun
  • Meaning: विश्वास
  • Answers: "What?" (noun)
  • e.g. "It is my belief that my students are very talented."
BELIEVE — Verb
  • Meaning: विश्वास करना (action)
  • Answers: "Do What?"
  • e.g. "I believe my students." / "I believe in God."
★ Memory Rule: Words ending in –ce (advice, practice, belief) = Noun. Words ending in –se / –ve (advise, practise, believe) = Verb.
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Advice vs Advise
✓
I advise you to practise a lot since practice makes a man perfect.
"advise" = Verb (action of giving advice). "practise" = Verb after "to" (infinitive). "practice" = Noun (subject — answers "what makes a man perfect?"). ✓
✓
He gave me a piece of advice.
"advice" = Noun (what did he give? → advice) ✓
✗
I am quite good at tennis but I need to practice my serve.
WRONG — "to" is followed by a Verb form. Use Verb "practise", not Noun "practice". ✗
✓
I am quite good at tennis but I need to practise my serve.
CORRECT — "to + practise" (infinitive = verb form). ✓
Belief vs Believe
✓
It is my belief that my students are very talented.
"belief" = Noun (my what? → belief). ✓
✓
I believe my students.
"believe" = Verb (I do what? → believe). ✓
✓
I have a belief that God helps me.
"belief" = Noun. ✓

📋 Rule 18 — Quick Recap

  • Noun → answers "What?" or "Who?" → e.g. advice, practice, belief
  • Verb → answers "Do What?" (action) → e.g. advise, practise, believe
  • "Practice makes a man perfect" ✓ (Noun as subject); "I practise daily" ✓ (Verb as action)
  • "He gave me a piece of advice" ✓ (Noun); "I advise you" ✓ (Verb)
  • "to + practise" ✓ (after "to", always use Verb); "to + practice" ✗ (practice is Noun)

✏️ Practice — Noun or Verb form?

✓

Noun — "what did I give?" ✓
✓

Noun — subject of sentence ✓
✗

WRONG — action = Verb form needed: "advise" ✗
✓

CORRECT — Verb form ✓

Pronoun Cases & Comparison Rules

Subjective vs Objective cases — compare like with like

→ Pronoun Cases — The Full Table
Person Subjective Pronouns Objective Pronouns Possessive Adjectives Possessive Pronouns
1st Person I, We Me, Us My, Our Mine, Ours
2nd Person You You Your Yours
3rd Person She, He, It, They Her, Him, It, Them Her, His, Its, Their Hers, His, —, Theirs
Key: Verb comes before the Subjective case. Verb comes after the Objective case.
"I (Subject) love him (Object)" — the verb separates subject from object.
Rule Comparisons Must Be Between Similar Cases

Comparisons are always done between similar things. A chocolate cannot be compared with a person. Similarly in grammar:

Subjective case → compared with Subjective case only
Objective case → compared with Objective case only
★ NOTE: To locate the nouns in the sentence, we generally try to find the answer of 'what', 'who', or 'whom'. So whenever we compare, we compare Subjective case and Objective case to Objective case.
Simple — No object in sentence → Only Subjective possible
✗
I am as tall as him.
WRONG — "I" is Subjective. "him" is Objective. Cannot compare Subjective with Objective. ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
I am as tall as he. (= I am as tall as he is.)
CORRECT — "I" (Subjective) compared with "he" (Subjective). ✓
✗
She is taller than me.
WRONG — "She" is Subjective. Cannot compare with "me" (Objective). ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
She is taller than I. (= She is taller than I am.)
CORRECT — "She" (Subjective) compared with "I" (Subjective). ✓
Both cases present — Both answers are valid but mean different things
✓
My parents love me more than he. (= …than he loves me.)
Comparing "my parents" (Subjective) with "he" (Subjective). Meaning: My parents love me more than HE loves me. Both my parents and he love me, but my parents love me more. ✓
✓
My parents love me more than him. (= …than they love him.)
Comparing "me" (Objective) with "him" (Objective). Meaning: My parents love me and him, but they love me more. ✓ Both sentences are grammatically correct but mean different things.
⚠️
Cannot compare incompatible things:
"She likes chocolates more than me." ✗ — Cannot compare chocolates (thing) with "me" (person).
"She likes chocolates more than I." ✓ — Both she and I are persons being compared as subjects.
💡
Rule 19 Trick: If there is no object in the sentence, only Subjective case is possible. If both Subject & Object exist, check which case the comparison belongs to. When in doubt, expand the sentence: add the missing verb mentally — "than he [loves me]" vs "than [they love] him".
★ More Examples from Book
✓
My parents scold me more than he.
Correct — he (Subjective) compared with my parents (Subjective). Meaning: He and my parents both scold me, my parents more. ✓
✓
My parents scold me more than him.
Correct — him (Objective) compared with me (Objective). Meaning: parents scold both me and him, but scold me more. ✓
✗
She is taller than me.
WRONG — "She" is Subjective. Compare only with Subjective "I". ✗ [Incorrect]
✓
He is as tall as I.
Correct — He (Subjective) compared with I (Subjective). ✓
✓
He speaks more frequently than she.
Correct — No object in sentence. He (Subjective) compared with she (Subjective). ✓
✗
She likes chocolates more than me.
WRONG — Cannot compare chocolates (thing) with "me" (person). Comparing objects needs same type. ✗
✓
She likes chocolates more than I.
CORRECT — She (person) compared with I (person). Both are persons liking chocolates. ✓

📋 Rule 19 — Quick Recap

  • Subjective: I, We, You, She, He, It, They | Objective: Me, Us, You, Her, Him, It, Them
  • Comparison rule: Subjective ↔ Subjective only. Objective ↔ Objective only.
  • "I am as tall as he" ✓ | "I am as tall as him" ✗
  • "My parents love me more than he" (he loves me less) vs "more than him" (they love him less) — both correct, different meanings.
  • No object in sentence → only Subjective case can be used in comparison.

WRONG — "I" is Subjective; compare with "he" ✗
✓

Who vs. Whom

"Who" = Subjective pronoun  |  "Whom" = Objective pronoun

→ The Core Rule

"Who" is used as a subjective pronoun — it is always working as the subject of a verb.
"Whom" is used as an objective pronoun — it is always working as an object in a sentence.

★ From the book (Rule 20): "Who" is used as a subjective pronoun, and "Whom" is used as an objective pronoun. That simply means that "who" is always as a subject to a verb, and "whom" is always working as an object in a sentence.
Substitute he / she → correct → use "who"
Substitute him / her → correct → use "whom"
→ How to Decide — The Substitution Trick

To clear this doubt, simply substitute the personal pronoun "he/him" or "she/her" for "who/whom".

If "he" or "she" fits → use WHO
  • Subject of the clause
  • Used before a verb
  • Test: Replace with he/she
If "him" or "her" fits → use WHOM
  • Object of the clause
  • Used after a verb or preposition
  • Test: Replace with him/her
★ All Examples — from Book & Class
Whom — Object examples (she met him → him → whom)
✓
Mrs. Dixit consulted an astrologer whom she met in Sonpur.
Test: "She met him in Sonpur" → him → use "whom" ✓
✓
Jack is the man whom I went fishing with.
Test: "I went fishing with him" → him → use "whom" ✓
✓
Whom can we turn to in a time of crisis?
Test: "Can we turn to her?" → her → use "whom" ✓
✓
He is the man whom everyone admires.
Test: "Everyone admires him" → him → use "whom" ✓
✓
They hired the man whom we interviewed last week.
Test: "We interviewed him last week" → him → use "whom" ✓
Who — Subject examples (she got the job → she → who)
✓
Jyoti is the girl who got the job.
Test: "She got the job" → she → use "who" ✓
✓
The delegates differed as to who they thought might win.
Test: "They thought he might win" → he → use "who" ✓ [Note: not "whom" here]
✓
She is one of the professors who I think is a mathematician.
Test: "She is a mathematician" → she → use "who" ✓
✓
She met the people who they say are helping all aspirants.
Test: "They are helping all aspirants" → they → use "who" ✓
💡
Quick Tip — The Golden Trick:
Replace who/whom with he/him:
→ If "he" sounds correct → write WHO
→ If "him" sounds correct → write WHOM

"She met him in Sonpur" → him → whom
"She got the job" → she → who

📋 Rule 20 — Quick Recap

  • Who = Subjective pronoun (subject of a verb). Substitute with he/she to verify.
  • Whom = Objective pronoun (object in a sentence). Substitute with him/her to verify.
  • "Jyoti is the girl who got the job" ✓ (she got the job → she → who)
  • "She met the astrologer whom she met in Sonpur" ✓ (she met him → him → whom)
  • "The delegates differed as to who they thought might win" ✓ (they thought he might win → he → who)

Rule 17 — Verb Agrees with First Subject Quasi-Coordinators
The Rule:When subjects are joined by quasi-coordinators, verb always agrees with the first subject only, regardless of the second subject.
Key Connectors:as well as, together with, along with, in addition to, not, and not, unlike, like, with, besides, accompanied by, governed by, led by, run by, but, except, nothing but, more than one, rather than.
Examples:"John as well as Mary wants" (John = singular → wants). "Teachers along with Principal are" (teachers = plural → are). "He and not his friends is guilty" (He = singular → is).
18
Rule 18 — Noun vs Verb Word Pairs Word Pairs
Noun (–ce / –f):Answers "What?" — advice (सलाह), practice (अभ्यास), belief (विश्वास). Used as subject/object of sentence.
Verb (–se / –ve):Answers "Do What?" — advise (सलाह देना), practise (अभ्यास करना), believe (विश्वास करना). Used as action word.
Examples:"Practice makes a man perfect" (Noun-subject). "I practise daily" (Verb-action). "I advise you to practise" (both Verbs). "He gave me a piece of advice" (Noun-object).
19
Rule 19 — Pronoun Cases & Comparison Pronoun Cases
Subjective:I, We, You, She, He, It, They — used before the verb (subject of sentence).
Objective:Me, Us, You, Her, Him, It, Them — used after the verb (object of sentence).
Comparison Rule:Compare Subjective with Subjective only, Objective with Objective only. "I am as tall as he" ✓. "She is taller than I" ✓. No object in sentence → only Subjective possible.
Both correct:"My parents love me more than he" (= than he loves me) vs "…more than him" (= than they love him). Different meanings but both grammatically correct.
20
Rule 20 — Who vs. Whom Who / Whom
Who:Subjective pronoun — subject of a verb. Test: substitute with he/she. If correct → use "who".
Whom:Objective pronoun — object in a sentence. Test: substitute with him/her. If correct → use "whom".
Examples:"Jyoti is the girl who got the job" (she got → she → who). "She met the astrologer whom she met in Sonpur" (she met him → him → whom). "He is the man whom everyone admires" (admires him → whom).
