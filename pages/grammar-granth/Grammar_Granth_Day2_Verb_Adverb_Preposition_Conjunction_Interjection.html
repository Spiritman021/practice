1. Quick Recap of Previous Class

In the last class, we learned to identify 3 Parts of Speech:

  • Noun – naming word
  • Pronoun – word used in place of a noun
  • Adjective – word that describes a Noun or Pronoun

Today we move to the next, very important part of speech: VERB, followed by Adverb, Preposition, Conjunction, and Interjection — completing all 8 Parts of Speech.

2. VERB – Definition & Identification

Verb (क्रिया) shows THREE things:

What Verb ShowsMeaningExample
Action क्रिया / काम करना — someone is doing some work "He dances." → क्या करता है? Dances
State कोई इंसान कुछ है (a person's state of being) "I am a teacher." → "am" shows my state — मैं एक टीचर हूं
Possession हक / किसी चीज का अधिकार — someone has a right/ownership over something "I have a car." → "have" shows possession — मेरे पास एक गाड़ी है

Question Test for Verb

For Noun, we asked: "What is...?" (क्या है?)

For Verb, we ask: "Do what?" / "What does he/she do?" (क्या करता है?)

"He dances." → He = Pronoun. "Do what?" → Dances → Dances = Verb

3. The "Auto-Verb" List (Always Verbs)

Certain words are always verbs — no logic needed, just recognize them by sight (this is a list to be memorized):

Be-forms / Have-formsModal Verbs
is, am, are, was, werecan, could
has, have, hadmay, might
be, been, beingwill, would
shall, should
ought
must

These will be studied in depth later under "Modal Verbs", "Verbs", and "Advanced Verbs" chapters. The HARDEST part of verb identification is finding the verb that shows an "action" (क्या करता है) — that's where logic is needed.

Worked Examples — Identifying the Action Verb

  • "You have done a great job." → "Have" is already known as a verb. Next verb: "Done" — this is the 3rd form of "Do" (Do, Did, Done). → "What did you do? → You have done a great job." → Done = Verb
  • "The teacher explained the lesson." → "What did the teacher do? → Explained the lesson." → Explained = Verb
  • "She reads books." → "What does she do? → Reads books." → Reads = Verb
  • "He runs." → "What does he do? → Runs." → Runs = Verb
  • "My friend bought a car." → "What did my friend do? → Bought a car." → Bought = Verb
  • "The team played well." → "What did the team do? → Played well." → Played = Verb
  • "She is honest and hardworking." → "Is" is already a known verb — no other part of speech possible here.
  • "He placed the bag." → "What did he do? → Placed the bag." → Placed = Verb
  • "The weather is pleasant." → "Is" = known verb.
  • "He ran and fell." → "What did he do? → Ran" AND "What happened? → Fell" — TWO verbs in this sentence (Ran, Fell). He both ran and fell.

The Three Forms of Verb (V1, V2, V3)

Only Verbs have 3 forms — no other part of speech has this property. This is another way to identify/confirm a verb.

V1 (First Form)V2 (Second Form)V3 (Third Form)
DoDidDone
"You have done a great job." → "Done" is the V3 form of "Do" → confirms it's a Verb.

4. Verb = "Soul of the Sentence"

Verb is the SOUL of the sentence (वर्ब इज द सोल ऑफ द सेंटेंस).

A sentence can be formed without any other part of speech, but it cannot be formed without a Verb. If there's no verb in a group of words, it's not a sentence at all.

Always remember: A sentence can exist without Noun, Pronoun, Adjective, Adverb, etc. — but NEVER without a Verb. The Verb MUST be present.

5. ADVERB – "Pados ki Aunty" (The Neighbour's Aunty)

The teacher gives Adverb a funny nickname: "पड़ोस की आंटी" (the neighbour's aunty) — because just like a nosy neighbour-aunty who knows everything about everyone, the Adverb tells us about almost EVERYTHING:

Adverb tells us about:
  1. Verb — how an action is done
  2. Adjective — describes/intensifies an adjective
  3. Another Adverb — describes another adverb
  4. Complete Sentence — can comment on the whole sentence
  5. Preposition
  6. Conjunction

Golden Rule: If you're confused about which part of speech a word is, and it doesn't fit anywhere else — it's most likely an Adverb. Adverb has this much "power" (it covers almost everything).

6. Worked Examples: Adverb vs Adjective Confusion

Example Set 1: "He is good." → "He is very good."

He       is       good
Pronoun   Verb    Adjective

He       is       very      good
Pronoun   Verb    Adverb   Adjective
  • "He is good." → He = Pronoun, Is = Verb. "How is he? → He is good" → Good describes the Pronoun "He" → Good = Adjective
  • "He is very good." → "How good is he? → Very good." The word describing an Adjective ("good") is an Adverb → Very = Adverb

So: Adjective tells us about Noun/Pronoun. The word that tells us about an Adjective = Adverb.

Example Set 2: "He walks fast." → "He walks very fast."

He       walks    fast
Pronoun   Verb     Adverb

He       walks    very    fast
Pronoun   Verb     Adverb   Adverb
  • "He walks fast." → He = Pronoun, Walks = Verb. "How does he walk? → Fast" → describes the VERB → Fast = Adverb
  • "He walks very fast." → "How fast does he walk? → Very fast" → "Very" describes ANOTHER ADVERB ("fast") → Very = Adverb (an adverb describing another adverb is still an adverb)

Example Set 3: "He is a very fast learner."

He       is    a     very fast    learner
Pronoun   Verb   Article   (Adj)     Noun

He = Pronoun, Is = Verb, A = Article (Article always describes a Noun) → Learner = Noun.

"What kind of learner? → Fast learner" → the word describing the NOUN "learner" is "fast" → here Fast = Adjective (because it's describing a noun, not a verb).

"How fast? → Very fast" → "Very" describes the adjective "fast" → Very = Adverb.

KEY COMPARISON:

  • "He walks very fast" → here both "Very" and "Fast" are Adverbs (describing the verb "walks" — how he walks)
  • "He is a very fast learner" → here "Fast" is an Adjective (describing the noun "learner") and "Very" is an Adverb (describing the adjective "fast")

The SAME WORDS ("very", "fast") can be different parts of speech depending on usage. Don't memorize blindly — check what the word is describing: if it describes a Noun/Pronoun → Adjective; if it describes anything else (Verb, Adjective, another Adverb, etc.) → Adverb.

Example Set 4: "She is the very girl who came first in the class."

She    is    the    very    girl    who came first in the class
Pronoun   Verb   Article   (Adj)    Noun

She = Pronoun, Is = Verb, The = Article → describes the Noun "Girl" → Girl = Noun.

"Which girl? → The VERY girl (who came first in class)" → "Very" here describes the NOUN "girl" → Very = Adjective (NOT an adverb here!).

Compare: In "He walks very fast" → Very = Adverb. In "She is the very girl..." → Very = Adjective. Same word, different roles based on what it describes.

7. Special Case: "However" (Adverb describing the WHOLE SENTENCE)

Sentence: "However, he failed."

However,    he    failed
(Adverb)     Pro    Verb

"He" = Pronoun, "Failed" = Verb. "However" — हालांकि वो फेल हो गया (However, he failed) — this word is commenting on/describing the ENTIRE SENTENCE, not just one word.

Important clarification on Conjunction vs Adverb:

  • Conjunction joins TWO sentences — it needs sentences on BOTH sides within the same overall sentence.
  • In "However, he failed." — there is only ONE sentence (a full stop comes before "However", so nothing is being joined).
  • Since "However" is not joining two sentences here, it is NOT a Conjunction.
  • Since it describes the entire sentence (the complete idea), it is an Adverb.

→ "However" = Adverb (in this context — describing the complete sentence)

Similarly: "Thus, he completed the work." → "Thus" also describes the entire sentence/idea → Thus = Adverb.

Teacher notes: "Starter" and "Connector" concepts (to be covered later) will revisit this idea of words like "However" and "Thus" in more depth.

8. PREPOSITION – Definition & Identification

Preposition = RELATES two Nouns or two Pronouns (शब्दों के बीच का रिश्ता/संबंध बताता है).

Preposition shows the relationship between two nouns/pronouns. This relationship can be of different types:

Type of RelationExamplePreposition
Position"Book is on the table."On
Time"I will come at 4 pm."At
Direction"I went to Agra."To

Other common Prepositions: in, from, by, during, etc.

How to identify Prepositions: Unlike Noun/Pronoun/Verb/Adjective/Adverb (which need LOGIC to identify), Prepositions are identified simply by recognizing them on sight — there's a known, fixed set of preposition words (in, on, at, to, from, by, during, etc.). No logical reasoning is required.

9. CONJUNCTION – Definition & Identification

Conjunction = JOINS (दो सेंटेंसेस/फ्रेज/वर्ड्स को जोड़ता है).

Conjunction can join:

  • Two sentences
  • Two phrases
  • Two words
  • "He is poor but he has a big house." → "But" joins TWO different sentences ("He is poor" + "He has a big house") into one → But = Conjunction
  • "I and Ram committed this blunder." → Originally two separate ideas ("I committed this blunder" + "Ram committed this blunder") joined by "And" → And = Conjunction

Comparison: Preposition vs Conjunction

  • Preposition's job = create a RELATION (between nouns/pronouns)
  • Conjunction's job = JOIN (two sentences/phrases/words within the SAME overall sentence)

Both Preposition and Conjunction cannot be identified using logic — they must be recognized by repeated exposure/memorization. The more you encounter words like "however", "but", "and", "or", "yet" in context, the more naturally you'll recognize whether they're acting as a conjunction or not.

Common Conjunctions to recognize:

ConjunctionUsage Pattern
However___ However ___ (joining two sentence ideas)
But___ but ___
Or___ or ___
Yet___ yet ___

Test for Conjunction: Is it in ONE sentence? Is it joining two different "sides"/ideas of that sentence together? If YES to both → it's a Conjunction. If "However" appears at the start with only ONE idea following (no joining happening) → it's an Adverb, not a Conjunction (see "However" example above).

10. INTERJECTION – Definition & Identification

Interjection = a word used to express EMOTIONS (इमोशंस को शब्दों के द्वारा दिखाना).

These words, by themselves, don't carry independent grammatical "meaning" in the sentence — they simply express a feeling.

EmotionInterjection Word
Happiness/Excitement (मैं बहुत खुश हूं)Hurray!
Sadness/Grief (मैं बहुत दुखी हूं)Alas!
Praise/Appreciation (शाबाशी)Bravo! / Well done!

How to identify Interjections: Very easy — look for the exclamation mark (!). That's usually the giveaway for an Interjection.

11. Master Summary: Logic vs Recognition

The teacher draws a key distinction for ALL 8 Parts of Speech:

GroupParts of SpeechIdentification Method
Group 1 — Needs LOGIC Noun, Adjective, Adverb, Verb Apply reasoning: "What does it answer? What does it describe?" — These FOUR are the most important and most frequently used in identification exercises. Master these four.
Group 2 — Recognized on SIGHT Pronoun, Preposition, Conjunction, Interjection Just look at them — limited fixed lists, recognized instantly without logical reasoning.

Priority for mastery: Identification of Noun is the MOST important. Then Adjective, then Adverb, then Verb — these four require the most "logic" and are used most often.

12. Practice: Full Sentence-by-Sentence Parsing (10 Sentences)

The teacher walked through 10 full sentences, identifying the part of speech for EVERY word. Full breakdown:

1. "Hurray, you have done a great job."

Hurray,   you    have   done   a      great   job
(Interj)   Pronoun   Verb   Verb(V3 of Do)   Article   Adjective   Noun

"Hurray" = Interjection (exclamation/emotion). "You" = Pronoun. "Have" = Verb (already known). "Done" = Verb — V3 form of Do (Do→Did→Done) — "What have you done? → A great job". "A" = Article (describes Noun). "Great" = Adjective (describes "job" — what kind of job? great job). "Job" = Noun.

2. "The teacher explained the lesson clearly."

The    teacher   explained   the   lesson   clearly
Article   Noun     Verb        Article   Noun    Adverb

"The" = Article → "Teacher" = Noun. "Explained" = Verb ("What did the teacher do? → Explained" — also क्या किया? पूछ रहा है). "The" = Article → "Lesson" = Noun. "Clearly" = Adverb (describes the verb "explained" — how did she explain? → clearly).

3. "She reads books in the library."

She    reads   books   in      the    library
Pronoun   Verb     Noun    Prep   Article   Noun

"She" = Pronoun. "Reads" = Verb ("What does she do? → Reads books"). "Books" = Noun (object — what does she read? → books). "In" = Preposition (recognized on sight). "The" = Article. "Library" = Noun (object of preposition "in").

4. "He runs very fast."

He    runs   very    fast
Pronoun   Verb   Adverb   Adverb

"He" = Pronoun. "Runs" = Verb. "Fast" = Adverb (How does he run? → Fast — describes the verb). "Very" = Adverb (How fast? → Very — describes another adverb "fast").

5. "My friend bought a new car."

My     friend   bought   a      new     car
Adjective   Noun    Verb     Article   Adjective   Noun

"My" = Adjective (whose friend? → My — describes the noun "friend"). "Friend" = Noun. "Bought" = Verb ("What did my friend do? → Bought a new car"). "A" = Article → "Car" = Noun. "New" = Adjective (what kind of car? → New car — describes the noun "car").

6. "Bravo, the team played well."

Bravo,    the    team   played   well
(Interj)   Article   Noun   Verb     Adverb

"Bravo" = Interjection (used for praise — also studied in interjection section). "The" = Article → "Team" = Noun. "Played" = Verb (1 verb here — "What did the team do? → Played well"). "Well" = Adverb (how did they play? → well — describes the verb "played").

7. "She is honest and hardworking."

She    is     honest    and     hardworking
Pronoun   Verb   Adjective   Conj   Adjective

"She" = Pronoun. "Is" = Verb (always-verb list). "Honest" = Adjective (how is she? → honest — describes the pronoun "she"). "And" = Conjunction (joins two descriptions/ideas about "she" — honest AND hardworking). "Hardworking" = Adjective (also describes the pronoun "she").

8. "He placed the bag on the table."

He    placed   the    bag    on     the    table
Pronoun   Verb     Article   Noun   Prep   Article   Noun

"He" = Pronoun. "Placed" = Verb ("What did he do? → Placed the bag"). "The" = Article → "Bag" = Noun. "On" = Preposition (recognized on sight — shows position). "The" = Article → "Table" = Noun (object of preposition "on").

9. "The weather is very pleasant."

The    weather   is     very    pleasant
Article   Noun      Verb   Adverb   Adjective

"The" = Article → "Weather" = Noun. "Is" = Verb (always-verb list). "Pleasant" = Adjective (how is the weather? → pleasant — describes the noun "weather"). "Very" = Adverb (how pleasant? → very — describes the adjective "pleasant").

10. "He ran fast but fell suddenly."

He    ran    fast    but    fell    suddenly
Pronoun   Verb   Adverb   Conj   Verb   Adverb

"He" = Pronoun. "Ran" = Verb #1 ("What did he do? → Ran fast"). "Fast" = Adverb (how did he run? → fast — describes the verb "ran"). "But" = Conjunction (joins the two ideas: "he ran fast" + "he fell suddenly" — within ONE sentence). "Fell" = Verb #2 (a second action — "what else happened? → fell"). "Suddenly" = Adverb (how did he fall? → suddenly — describes the verb "fell").

Reminder: Verb = Soul of the Sentence. A sentence can exist without other parts of speech but never without a Verb.
