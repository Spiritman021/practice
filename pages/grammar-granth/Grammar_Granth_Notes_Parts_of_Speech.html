1. Introduction to Grammar & Parts of Speech

Grammar = a combination of different Parts of Speech.

Grammar is essentially about how you use all the parts of speech together — their combination forms grammar.

The 8 Parts of Speech

#Part of Speech
1Noun
2Pronoun
3Adjective
4Verb
5Adverb
6Preposition
7Conjunction
8Interjection

Bonus (9th, sort of): Article – not officially one of the 8 parts of speech, but is treated as a part of Adjective.

Today's class covers: Introduction to all parts of speech + how to identify them in sentences. Today specifically: Noun, Pronoun, and Adjective. (Verb, Adverb, Preposition, Conjunction, Interjection to be covered in the extra Saturday class.)

2. NOUN – Definition & Identification

Noun = A Naming Word.

The textbook definition ("name of a place, person, animal, thing") is incomplete. The simpler, more powerful definition: whenever you give ANYTHING a name, that name is a Noun.

Examples:
• "I named you Nimisha" → Nimisha = Noun
• "I named you Anu" → Anu = Noun
• "I called you a boy" → boy = Noun
• "I called this thing a table" → table = Noun

So: Noun = naming word. Whenever something is given a name → it's a Noun.

3. Five Methods to Identify a Noun

Method 1: The Question Test

Ask these questions to the verb (kriya): What? Who? Whom? Which thing? (क्या, कौन, किसको, किस चीज)
Whichever word answers these questions = Noun (or Pronoun).
Example: "I have a home." → "What do I have?" → Home → answers "what" → Home = Noun
Example: "Love is in the air." → "What is in the air?" → Love → Love = Noun

Note: Noun and Pronoun are identified using the same questions — the difference is that there are only a limited, fixed set of pronouns, so you recognize them by sight (covered in the Pronoun section).

Method 2: Subject–Object Rule (using the Verb)

  • The word that comes before the verb = Subject
  • The word that comes after the verb = Object
  • The word that comes after a Preposition = also Object

Golden Rule: Subject and Object can ONLY ever be a Noun or a Pronoun — nothing else.

Example: "He loves them." → loves = verb. Before verb = "He" (Subject = Pronoun). After verb = "them" (Object = Pronoun).

Example (Preposition + Object): "In Agra", "in the air", "in Rameshwaram", "at my desk" → Agra, air, Rameshwaram, Desk = all Nouns (objects of preposition "in/at").

Summary of Concepts So Far (as given by teacher):

  1. A Noun gives something a name.
  2. A Noun answers certain questions: Who? What? Whom?
  3. Subject comes before the verb, Object comes after the verb (and also after prepositions). Subject and Object are always Noun or Pronoun — no other part of speech.

Method 3: Comparing Similar-looking Sentences (Question Test in detail)

Sentence A: "I have a home." → "What do I have?" → Home → Answers "what" → Home = Noun

Sentence B: "I went home." → Try the question: "What did I go?" — doesn't work. "Where did I go?" → Home answers "Where" — and there's no preposition before it.
→ Home = NOT a Noun here (it answers "where", which is an Adverb question — to be studied later).

"I" in both sentences = Pronoun (answers "Who went?" / "Who has a home?").

Method 4: Gerund (Verb + ing acting as a Noun)

Sentence: "I am addicted to teaching." → "What am I addicted to?" → Teaching → answers "what"/"which thing" → Teaching = Noun.

When V1 + ing is used as a Noun (i.e., it answers noun-type questions), it is called a Gerund.
Compare: "I want to teach." → "What do I want?" → To teach (the entire phrase) is answering "what" → the whole phrase "to teach" = Noun (not just "teach" alone).

When "to + V1" plays the role of a Noun, it is called an Infinitive.
Two ways a Verb can become/act as a Noun:
  1. V1 + ing (Gerund) — single word noun
  2. To + V1 (Infinitive) — full phrase acts as a single noun

Method 5: Article + Noun "Relationship" (Husband-Wife Analogy)

Rule: Wherever there is an Article (a / an / the), there is always a Noun following it — no matter how many words come in between.

The teacher's analogy: it's like a committed relationship ("जहाँ-जहाँ मैं चलूंगा, जहाँ-जहाँ तू चलेगा, मेरा साया साथ होगा") — wherever the Article goes, the Noun follows.

• "a man" → Noun = man
• "an honest man" → Noun = man (adjective "honest" comes in between)
• "a very honest man" → Noun = man (more words in between, still works)
• "a very honest and reliable man" → Noun = man (even more distance, relationship still holds)

The "Exception" — When Article + Adjective has NO following Noun

Story told by teacher: Article and Noun have a strong relationship (commitment), but one exception exists — caused by the Adjective.

Rule: "The" + Adjective (with NO noun after) = refers to a whole class/category of people.

• "The poor" = poor people
• "The rich" = rich people
• "The brave" = brave people

This is the only case where Article is not followed by a Noun. In every other case, Article → Noun is guaranteed.

Important Clarification: "Student Life Lessons" / "Self Discovery"

Some students wondered why "Student" was not counted as a separate Noun. Explanation:

"Student Life" (as in "Student Life Lessons") describes which lessons — i.e., it tells us about the noun "Lessons". A word/phrase that describes a noun is an Adjective. So "Student Life" here plays the role of an Adjective, modifying "Lessons" — it is NOT counted separately as a Noun.

Similarly "Self" in "Self-discovery" can be seen as an Adjective describing "Discovery" (Noun) — "whose discovery? → self's discovery".

6. PRONOUN – Definition & Types

Pronoun = a word used IN PLACE OF a noun, to avoid repetition.

"Pronoun is a word which is used in place of noun to avoid repetition."

Example: "Ram is a good boy. Ram comes daily. Ram scores well. Ram is very consistent."
Instead of repeating "Ram" again and again → use a Pronoun ("He").

Identification of Pronoun = SAME as Noun (answers Who/What/Whom etc.) — but pronouns are easy to spot because there is only a limited, fixed list of them.

Types of Pronouns

(A) Personal Pronouns

CasePronounsUsed as
SubjectiveHe, She, It, They, I, We, YouBefore the verb (Subject)
ObjectiveHim, Her, It, Them, Me, Us, YouAfter the verb / preposition (Object)

Gender/usage notes mentioned by teacher (to be covered in detail later):

  • He – used for males
  • She – used for females
  • It – used when gender is unknown, or for non-living things
  • They – used for plural

(B) Possessive Pronouns

Show possession/ownership — "this belongs to me/you/etc."
PronounMeaning
Minebelongs to me
Yoursbelongs to you
Oursbelongs to us
Hisbelongs to him
Hersbelongs to her
Theirsbelongs to them

Special note: "It" has NO possessive pronoun form. "Its" is NOT used as a possessive pronoun in this category.

• "This book is mine." – यह किताब मेरी है
• "This book is yours." – यह किताब तुम्हारी है
• "This book is ours." – यह किताब हमारी है
• "This book is his." – यह किताब उसकी है
• "This book is theirs."
• "This book is hers."

(C) Indefinite Pronouns (when the name of the person/thing is NOT known)

Core logic: If you don't know someone's/something's name → use a Pronoun. If you know the name → it becomes a Noun.

Indefinite Pronouns

Example: "One should do one's duties." → One = Pronoun.
"Someone" = Pronoun (name not known).

7. ADJECTIVE – Definition & Identification

Adjective = a word that tells us something about a Noun OR a Pronoun.

"Adjective will tell us about a noun and a pronoun."

Key Examples Establishing the Definition

• "Our book" – "किसकी किताब? हमारी किताब" → Book = Noun, "Our" describes the noun → Our = Adjective
• "My book" → Book = Noun, "My" describes it → My = Adjective
• "This book" (कौन सी किताब? ये वाली) → Book = Noun, "This" describes which book → This = Adjective

8. CRITICAL CONFUSION: "This/That" — Pronoun vs Adjective

The teacher gives TWO sentences with the SAME meaning but DIFFERENT parts of speech:

SentenceMeaning (Hindi)Analysis
"This is my book." यह किताब मेरी है Book = Noun. "My" describes Book → My = Adjective. "This" points out which one (कौन सी का जवाब) → This = Pronoun.
"This book is mine." ये किताब मेरी है Book = Noun. "This" comes directly before the noun and describes WHICH book → This = Adjective. "Mine" answers "whose? → mine" → Mine = Pronoun.

Key takeaway: Same word ("this"), same overall meaning, but DIFFERENT part of speech depending on its position/role in the sentence. Always analyze each sentence individually.

Two Ways to Identify an Adjective

Method 1: Adjective comes BEFORE its Noun.
• "Red bag" → Bag = Noun (कौन सा बैग? Red बैग) → Red = Adjective
• "My bag" → My = Adjective
• "Such books" → Such = Adjective
• "Student Life Lessons" → "Student Life" = Adjective (describes "Lessons")
Method 2: Adjective comes AFTER the Noun/Pronoun, connected via a Linking Verb.

Linking Verb = a verb that LINKS the Noun/Pronoun to the Adjective.

"She is intelligent." → She = Pronoun, "is" = linking verb, Intelligent = Adjective (describes "She" — कैसी है? इंटेलिजेंट है)

Words ending in these suffixes are commonly (90–95%) Adjectives (not always true, but high probability):

Practice: 7 Practice Sentences for Adjectives

Worked examples discussed by teacher:

  • "Good teacher" → Teacher = Noun; "कैसी टीचर है? अच्छी टीचर है" → Good = Adjective
  • "He answered the question wisely." → No adjective here. "Wisely" describes the VERB ("how did he answer? → wisely") → it is an Adverb, NOT an adjective. Common student mistake: calling "-ly" words adjectives — wrong, they describe the verb → Adverb.
  • "His brother..." → "His" = Adjective (whose brother? → his)
  • "Old man" → Man = Noun, कौन सा मैन? Old → Old = Adjective
  • "They are studying English today." → No adjective (describing "English" — कैसी इंग्लिश? — none given)
  • "He spoke politely to the officer." → "Politely" describes the VERB ("how did he speak? → politely") → Adverb, not adjective. Also: "The" (article) before "Officer" plays an adjective-like role (articles are technically a sub-part of adjective, but identified separately).
  • "The child is intelligent." → कैसा बच्चा? Intelligent → Intelligent = Adjective
  • "Our books" → "किसकी किताबें? हमारी किताबें" → Our = Adjective
  • "He completed the work nicely." → "Nicely" describes how the work was completed → describes the VERB → Adverb, not Adjective. (कैसा काम नहीं? कैसे किया? — describes "how", not "which work")
  • "He sat quietly." → "Quietly" describes HOW he sat (verb) → Adverb, not Adjective.

Golden Rule repeated: A word describing the NOUN/PRONOUN = Adjective. A word describing the VERB (how an action was done) = Adverb.

9. Note on Adverb (Preview/Things Mistaken as Adjectives)

Several words were initially mistaken by students as Adjectives but are actually Adverbs (to be studied in detail in the next class):

  • Wisely, Deeply, Politely, Nicely, Quietly → all describe the VERB (how an action was performed) → Adverbs, not Adjectives.
  • Rule mentioned: Adjective + "-ly" often converts it into an Adverb. Example: "Deep" (a river is deep → Adjective describing river/noun) + "-ly" → "Deeply" (describes HOW something is done → Adverb).
  • "Living" = a Verb (an action — "to live/jeena"). "Living wisely" → "wisely" describes HOW one lives (the verb) → Adverb.

Teacher's reassurance: many doubts (why is X an adjective and not noun, why Y is adverb not adjective, etc.) will become clear once Verb, Adverb, Preposition, Conjunction, and Interjection are also covered — because identification of one part of speech depends on understanding the others. Patience (पेशेंस) is essential while studying Grammar.

10. Overall Summary, Approach & Homework/Schedule

Key Takeaways from Today's Class

  • Grammar = combination of 8 Parts of Speech (+ Article, treated as part of Adjective).
  • Noun = naming word. Identify via: (1) Question test (what/who/whom/which thing), (2) Subject-before-verb / Object-after-verb-or-preposition rule, (3) Gerund (V1+ing as noun), (4) Infinitive (to+V1 as noun phrase), (5) Article-Noun relationship (with "the+adjective" = whole class exception), (6) Suffix patterns.
  • Pronoun = word used in place of a noun to avoid repetition. Types: Personal (Subjective/Objective), Possessive, Indefinite. Identification = same as noun, but limited fixed list makes them recognizable.
  • Adjective = describes a Noun or Pronoun. Identify via: (1) comes before its noun, (2) comes after noun/pronoun via a linking verb, (3) suffix patterns. Finding the Adjective = finding its corresponding Noun.
  • Adverb (preview) = describes the VERB (how an action happens) — often ends in "-ly". Many "-ly" words students mistake for adjectives are actually adverbs.
