1. Why This Class Matters — The "Foundation" Approach

This class works on the FOUNDATION of Grammar — the actual base/structure that all grammar rules sit on top of. Until now, students were taught grammar by directly memorizing rules ("this is the rule, that is the rule") without ever working on this base.

Today's class teaches the structure of sentences — how sentences are actually formed. This is what makes grammar finally "click" instead of being a pile of memorized rules.

2. What is a Sentence? (Subject + Predicate)

A Sentence = a group of words containing a SUBJECT + a VERB + the rest of the portion.

The Verb + everything else (the "rest of the portion") together is called the PREDICATE.

SENTENCE = SUBJECT + PREDICATE
(where PREDICATE = VERB + ... rest of the sentence)

3. 5 Types of Sentences — Overview

Before going deep into Subject and Predicate, we must first understand the TYPES OF SENTENCES. There are 5 main types:

#TypeSub-types
1AssertiveAffirmative (Positive), Negative
2Interrogative(Questions) — Positive, Negative
3ImperativeRequest, Order, Suggestion/Advice, Proposal/Offer
4OptativeBlessing, Curse
5Exclamatory(Emotions)

4. ASSERTIVE Sentences (Affirmative & Negative)

Assertive = Statements (असर्टिव सेंटेंस). Two types:

  • Affirmative (Positive): Sentences WITHOUT "not", "no", etc.
  • Negative: Sentences WITH "not", "no", etc.

Structure

SUBJECT comes first → then VERB
If Helping Verb (H.V.) is present: Subject + H.V. + Main Verb (M.V.)
If no Helping Verb: Subject + Main Verb directly
  • "I am a teacher." → I = Subject, Am = Verb (this is the AFFIRMATIVE form)
  • "I am not a doctor." → I = Subject, Am = Verb, "not" comes after the verb → this is the NEGATIVE form

Key feature: In Assertive sentences, the SUBJECT always comes FIRST, and the VERB comes AFTER (whether Affirmative or Negative).

5. INTERROGATIVE Sentences (Questions)

Interrogative = Question sentences (इंटेरोगेटिव सेंटेंसेस).

The special feature: the Helping Verb (H.V.) — which helps the Main Verb — ALWAYS comes BEFORE the Subject. Then comes the Main Verb (the actual action).

Structure

H.V. + SUBJECT + MAIN VERB

Helping Verbs vs Main Verbs — quick reminder:

  • Helping Verbs: is, am, are, was, were (and modals like can, could, may, might, shall, should)
  • Main Verb: the verb that shows the actual action

"Have you done it?" (क्या तुमने इस काम को किया है?)

Have    you    done    it ?
H.V.     Subject   M.V.

Notice: "Have" (Helping Verb) comes FIRST, "you" (Subject) comes AFTER.

Compare with the Assertive (statement) version: "You have done it." → here Subject ("You") comes first, then Helping Verb ("have").

The ONLY real difference between Assertive and Interrogative (besides "?" vs "."): the PLACEMENT of Subject and Helping Verb swaps. Everything else stays the same.

TypeOrder
Assertive (Statement)Subject → Helping Verb → Main Verb
Interrogative (Question)Helping Verb → Subject → Main Verb

Error-Spotting Examples from Class

"Why you have not completed it?" → ERROR! Since this is a Question (ends with "?"), the Helping Verb MUST come before the Subject. The Subject ("you") cannot come before the Helping Verb ("have") in a question.

Correct: "Why have you not completed it?"

"I asked him what was his name." → ERROR! This sentence ends with a full stop, meaning it's a STATEMENT (Assertive), not a question. In Assertive sentences, the Subject must come BEFORE the Helping Verb.

Correct: "I asked him what his name was." (Subject "his name" comes before Verb "was")

This type of error is extremely common in exams (Error Detection questions).

Affirmative vs Negative Interrogative

TypeStructure
Positive (Affirmative) Interrogative(Wh-word) + H.V. + Subject + M.V. + ...?
Negative Interrogative(Wh-word) + H.V. + Subject + not + M.V. + ...?

Wh-word = What, When, Why, How, etc. — these are OPTIONAL (not compulsory) in a question.

Sub + H.V. + M.V. → I can dance.
Sub + M.V. → I dance.

H.V. + Sub + M.V. → Can I dance?
Why + H.V. + Sub + M.V. → Why can I dance?

6. Exception: Inversion of Sentence

Inversion of Sentence = a SPECIAL EXCEPTION where, even in an Assertive (statement) sentence, the Helping Verb comes BEFORE the Subject — i.e., the order gets "inverted/reversed".

"English is full of exceptions" — this concept will be studied in much more depth later, in the Adverb chapter. Today it's just introduced so students aren't confused when they encounter such sentences.

"Hardly had I entered the class when it began to rain." (मुश्किल से मैंने क्लास में एंट्री ही की थी कि बारिश होने लग गई।)

Hardly   had    I     entered the class
         H.V.    Subject

This is a STATEMENT (full stop, not a question) — but the Helping Verb "had" comes BEFORE the Subject "I". This is INVERSION.

"Here comes the king." (यहां पर राजा आता है।)

Here   comes   the king
      M.V.     Subject

Normal order would be "The king comes here" (Subject first, Verb after). But here the Main Verb "comes" comes BEFORE the Subject "the king" — this is also INVERSION.

These are very particular, rare cases — exceptions, not the general rule. Don't get confused if you see such inverted sentences; just recognize it as "Inversion".

7. IMPERATIVE Sentences (4 Types)

Imperative Sentences "HIDE" the Subject — they DIRECTLY start with the VERB, with NO Subject.

Recap: Every sentence normally needs (1) a Subject — who/what we're talking about, and (2) a Verb — the action. Example: "I teach." → "I" = Subject, "Teach" = Verb (action).

But Imperative sentences say "hide the subject — no subject needed". They start DIRECTLY with the Verb.

IMPERATIVE = MAIN VERB + (rest of sentence)    [NO SUBJECT]

The 4 Types of Imperative Sentences

TypeDescriptionExamples
1. Request Your TONE shows you're requesting someone, politely "Give me your book." / "Please give me your book." / "Kindly give me your book."
2. Order (Command) A direct command/order to someone "Shut up your mouth." / "Obey your elders."
3. Suggestion / Advice Giving advice to someone "Obey your elders." (अपने बड़ों की बात मानो)
4. Proposal / Offer Proposing/offering something to do together "Let us go for a walk." (चलो वॉक पे चलते हैं)

Key Analysis — "Please" / "Kindly"

"Please give me your book." → "Give" is the verb. Before "Give" there is NO Noun/Pronoun (no Subject). "Please" is an EXTRA word here, playing the role of an ADVERB — because it describes the VERB ("Give"). Same for "Kindly" — also an Adverb.

"Shut up your mouth." → "Shut" is used DIRECTLY (no subject before it) — this is an ORDER.

"Obey your elders." → "Obey" used directly, no subject — this is ADVICE.

"Let us go for a walk." → "Let" is a verb, no Noun/Pronoun before it — this is a PROPOSAL/OFFER.

These 4 types of Imperative sentences will be revisited when studying Active-Passive Voice and Direct-Indirect Speech — to learn how to convert Imperative sentences between these forms.

8. Imperative vs Interrogative — "Will You Marry Me?"

A student asked a "very cute" question: "Will you marry me?" — Is this an Imperative sentence (since it sounds like a proposal)?

Answer: NO! This is NOT an Imperative sentence — it's an INTERROGATIVE (Question) sentence.

Will    you    marry    me ?
H.V.     Subject   M.V.

"Will" = Helping Verb, "you" = Subject, "marry" = Main Verb. H.V. + Subject + M.V. = the structure of an Interrogative sentence.

What WOULD the Imperative version look like?

  • "Please marry me." → Request (you're requesting). Directly starts with verb "Marry" — Imperative.
  • "Marry me." → Order (you're commanding). Imperative.
  • "Let's get married." → Proposal/Offer. Imperative.

Summary: "Will you marry me?" = Interrogative (Question). "Shut up and marry me" / "Marry me" = Imperative (Order/Request).

9. OPTATIVE Sentences (Blessing / Curse)

Optative Sentences = used to give BLESSINGS or CURSES.

  • Blessing: "भगवान तुम्हारा भला करे" / "तुम्हें जिंदगी में सारी खुशियां मिले" (May you get all happiness in life)
  • Curse: "इसके सारे दांत टूट जाए" / "कीड़े पड़ जाए दांत में" (May all your teeth break / rot — like a famous movie dialogue!)

Special Feature — Similar to Imperative, but with a Twist

Like Imperative sentences, Optative sentences also START WITH A VERB (often "May", which is itself a Helping Verb).

BUT — unlike Imperative — Optative sentences DO HAVE A SUBJECT. The Subject comes AFTER the verb (after "May" or after the main verb).

SentenceBreakdown
"May God bless you." May (Verb/H.V.) → God (Subject) → bless you (rest)
"Long live the king." Long (Adverb) → live (Verb) → the king (Subject)
"May you live a thousand years." May (Verb) → you (Subject) → live a thousand years (rest)

"Long live the king" — "Long" here = Adverb (लंबा जी है — "long" describes how he lives, i.e. describes the verb "live").

Two Structures for Optative Sentences

Structure 1: MAY + SUBJECT + VERB + ...
Structure 2: VERB + SUBJECT + ...

"May" is itself a Helping Verb — so even Structure 1 technically "starts with the verb", since "May" IS a (helping) verb.

10. Optative vs Imperative — "Bless Me"

A student asked: in "Bless me" — is this Optative or Imperative?

Bless    me
Verb     Object
     (Imperative — No Subject)

Answer: IMPERATIVE, not Optative.

"Bless me" = मुझे ब्लेस करो — you're REQUESTING the other person to bless YOU. It starts directly with the verb "Bless", and there's NO subject (the "me" here is an OBJECT, not a Subject — because "me" isn't doing any action; the action is being done TO "me").

Key Distinction

Sentence TypeWho is giving the blessing?Subject present?
Imperative ("Bless me")You're asking the OTHER person to bless YOU (the speaker requests a blessing)NO Subject (the verb-receiver, "me", is an Object)
Optative ("May God bless you" / "Long live the king" / "May you live a thousand years")YOU are giving a blessing/curse TO the other personYES — there IS a Subject, but it comes AFTER the verb ("May")

Imperative = NO Subject at all. Optative = Subject IS present, but positioned AFTER the verb/"May".

11. EXCLAMATORY Sentences

Exclamatory Sentences = used to show EMOTIONS through words, when we don't know exactly how to express that emotion otherwise.

This connects to the previous class's topic: Interjection — exclamatory sentences USE interjections to show emotions through words.

SentenceEmotion Shown
"Yay! I did it."Excitement/achievement
"Wow! It is so beautiful."Amazement
"What a pleasant day it is!"Joy/pleasure
"Well done! You deserved it."Praise (Subject = "You", Verb = "deserved")
"Hurray!"Happiness (खुशी)
"Alas!"Sadness (दुख)
"Bravo!"Appreciation/praise (शाबाशी)

Identification of Exclamatory Sentences

  1. Always uses the Exclamation Mark (!)
  2. Always uses WORDS to express emotions (Interjections)

Sentence Structure of Exclamatory Sentences

! + SUBJECT + VERB + ...

Even in Exclamatory sentences, the underlying structure is: Subject comes first, Verb comes after — same as in normal Assertive sentences.

"Yay! I did it." → Subject "I" first, Verb "did" after.

"Wow! It is so beautiful." → Subject "It" first, Verb "is" after.

"What a pleasant day it is!" → Subject "it" first, Verb "is" after.

13. Subject, Verb & Complement — The Core Idea

CORE IDEA (VERY IMPORTANT): A sentence is NOT just Subject + Verb. At the advanced level, we analyze WHAT COMPLETES THE MEANING of the verb. That completion is called a COMPLEMENT.

"I am a teacher."

I    am    a teacher
Subject   Verb   Complement

"I" = the one we're talking about → Subject. "am" = Verb. Now — WHO is completing this verb? "I am WHAT?" → the answer "a teacher" COMPLETES the verb "am". This completing word/phrase is called the COMPLEMENT.

⚠️ Spelling note from class: It's "Complement" (complEment = something that COMPLETES), NOT "Compliment" (complIment = praise/तारीफ). Different words, different meanings!

Definition: A complement is a word or phrase that completes the meaning of a VERB or a SUBJECT.

14. SUBJECT COMPLEMENT (Linking Verbs)

Subject Complement: comes after a LINKING VERB and describes or renames the SUBJECT.

What is a Linking Verb?

A Linking Verb LINKS the Subject to the Subject Complement.

List of Linking Verbs: be, is, am, are, was, were, seem, appear, become, feel, look, sound, smell, taste, remain

"I am a teacher." → "a teacher" tells us WHO "I" is — it gives information ABOUT the subject "I". So it COMPLETES the subject → it's the Subject Complement. And "am" (the verb connecting "I" to "a teacher") = the Linking Verb.

"He became a teacher." → "a teacher" = Subject Complement (Noun), "became" = Linking Verb.

"It tastes delicious." → "delicious" = Subject Complement (Adjective), "tastes" = Linking Verb.

"He is smart." → "smart" = Subject Complement (Adjective), "is" = Linking Verb.

15. Predicate Nominative vs Predicate Adjective

A Linking Verb can link the Subject to TWO different kinds of Subject Complements:

Predicate Nominative (Renames Subject — links to a NOUN)

Subject + Linking Verb + NOUN
  • ✅ "She is a teacher" → "teacher" = Noun → this is Predicate Nominative
  • ✅ "Ramesh became a leader" → "leader" = Noun → Predicate Nominative
  • ❌ "She is teacher" (article missing) → INCORRECT — needs "a teacher"

Predicate Adjective (Describes Subject — links to an ADJECTIVE)

Subject + Linking Verb + ADJECTIVE
  • ✅ "The food smells delicious" → "delicious" = Adjective → Predicate Adjective
  • ✅ "He seems confident" → "confident" = Adjective → Predicate Adjective

⚠️ Important: No Object here. Why? Because Object/Subject can ONLY ever be a Noun or Pronoun — never an Adjective. Since "delicious" and "confident" are Adjectives, there's no Object in these sentences.

Naming Logic — Where Did These Names Come From?

Recall: the Subject + everything after the Verb = the PREDICATE. So:

  • If the Predicate part (the complement) is a Noun → it's called Predicate Nominative
  • If the Predicate part (the complement) is an Adjective → it's called Predicate Adjective

"The name isn't something new — it's just describing what TYPE of word the Predicate/Complement is."

  • "The sky turned blue." → "Sky" = Subject, "turned" = Linking Verb, "blue" = Subject Complement → since "blue" = Adjective → Predicate Adjective
  • "She remained calm during the crisis." → "She" = Subject, "remained" = Linking Verb, "calm" = Subject Complement → "calm" = Adjective → Predicate Adjective. "during the crisis" = Prepositional Phrase (starts with the preposition "during"), and "crisis" = Prepositional Object (the noun/pronoun after a preposition).

16. Practice: Identify the Subject Complement

  1. "The sky turned blue."
    The sky    turned    blue
    Subject     Verb      Subject Complement (Predicate Adjective)
  2. "She remained calm during the crisis."
    She    remained    calm     (during the crisis)
    Sub     Verb        Subject Complement   Prepositional Phrase
                                        (Predicate Adjective)                "crisis" = Prepositional Object

Bigger Picture Summary (Sub Complement)

The teacher's overall flowchart for analyzing any sentence:

  1. Find the SUBJECT (what we're talking about) and the VERB.
  2. IF the Verb is a Linking Verb → whatever comes after it = Subject Complement.
  3. The Subject Complement can ONLY be one of two things: a Noun (→ Predicate Nominative) or an Adjective (→ Predicate Adjective).

17. OBJECT — Direct & Indirect

Subject = what we TALK OF (जिसके बारे में हम बात कर रहे हो). Object = the one on whom the VERB's ACTION is performed (जिसके ऊपर वर्ब ने अपना एक्शन किया है).

Recall: Object is ALWAYS a Noun or Pronoun — nothing else.

"I painted a picture."

I    painted    a picture
Sub   Verb (Action)   Object

"What did I paint?" → "a picture" → Object.

Two Types of Objects: Direct (DO) and Indirect (IO)

A. Direct Object (DO)

The DIRECT OBJECT answers: "What?" — when you ask the verb "What [did the subject do]?", the answer is the Direct Object.

  • "She wrote a letter." → "What did she write? → A letter" → "a letter" = Direct Object
  • "He bought a car." → "What did he buy? → A car" → "a car" = Direct Object

B. Indirect Object (IO)

The INDIRECT OBJECT is the BENEFICIARY of the action — answers questions like "To whom?" / "For whom?" (किसको?)

"I gave him a book." (मैंने उसको एक किताब दी)

I    gave    him    a book
Sub   Verb    IO      DO

"What did I give? → A book" → DO. "To whom did I give it? → Him" → IO.

  • "She gave me a gift." → "gave" = Verb; "What did she give? → A gift" = DO; "To whom? → me" = IO
  • "He taught us grammar." → "taught" = Verb; "What did he teach? → Grammar" = DO; "To whom? → us" = IO

Two Structures for DO & IO

Structure 1: Verb + IO + DO
Structure 2: Verb + DO + to/for + IO
  • "She gave me a book." → Structure 1: gave + me (IO) + a book (DO)
  • "She gave a book to me." → Structure 2: gave + a book (DO) + to + me (IO)

Special note: In "She gave a book to me", the word "to" is a Preposition, and "me" (after the preposition) can ALSO be called a Prepositional Object (PO) — IN ADDITION to being the Indirect Object. So "me" here = IO AND Prepositional Object, both at once.

    "What did he send? → A message" = DO. "To whom? → her" = IO.

19. OBJECT COMPLEMENT

Just as a Subject can have a Subject Complement (which completes the SUBJECT's meaning), an OBJECT can have an Object Complement — which completes the OBJECT's meaning.

Comparison: Subject Complement vs Object Complement

"She is a teacher." → "a teacher" tells us about "She" (the Subject) → Subject Complement.

"She elected him president."

She    elected    him    president
Sub    Verb      IO      Object Complement (OC)

Who became "president"? NOT "She" — it's "him". So "president" describes/completes "him" (the Object), NOT "She" (the Subject) → it's the Object Complement, not the Subject Complement.

Object Complement: comes after CERTAIN verbs and gives EXTRA INFORMATION about the OBJECT.

Verbs that take Object Complement (memorize this list): make, call, name, elect, consider, declare, find

⚠️ Common misconception clarified: "All Helping Verbs are NOT Linking Verbs." Modals like Can, Could, May, Might, Shall, Should are Helping Verbs but NOT Linking Verbs.

Structure

SUBJECT + VERB + OBJECT + COMPLEMENT
  • "They elected him president." → They=Sub, elected=Verb, him=IO/Object, president=Object Complement
  • "She called the plan foolish." → She=Sub, called=Verb, the plan=Object, foolish=Object Complement
  • "I made him the king." → I=Sub, made=Verb, him=IO, the king=Object Complement

20. Practice: Identify Object Complement

  1. "The judge declared him guilty."
    The judge    declared    him    guilty
    Subject (S)     (verb)       IO      Object Complement (OC)

    "guilty" describes/completes "him" (who is guilty? he is) → Object Complement.

  2. "We found the movie boring."
    We    found    the movie    boring
    S     V        O         OC

    "boring" describes the movie (Object) — how is the movie? Boring → Object Complement. (Note: "Boring" and "Foolish" ARE Adjectives — confirmed by teacher when a student asked.)

Golden Rule: If a word describes/gives info about the SUBJECT → Subject Complement. If it describes/gives info about the OBJECT → Object Complement. Only a FEW specific verbs (make, call, name, elect, consider, declare, find) take an Object Complement — not all verbs.

22. Why Are We Learning This? (Error Reduction)

A common student question: "Why are we learning all this? Where is it used?"

The answer: Error Reduction / Error Detection questions — a HUGE chunk of exam questions (Bank, SSC exams especially) come from this area. Students often CANNOT identify:

  • Which verb belongs to which subject
  • Whether a helping verb has been incorrectly placed before/after the object

For SHORT sentences (e.g., "He is a talented." — where the article "a" shouldn't be there before "talented" since it's an adjective, not a noun), spotting errors is easy.

But as sentences get LONGER, and the Subject and Verb get separated by many words (or the Object is far away), it becomes very difficult to spot errors — UNLESS you understand the sentence STRUCTURE deeply.

If your grammar foundation is strong and you understand sentence structure well, you'll automatically RECOGNIZE every sentence pattern and instantly spot what's wrong.

Real Example — The "as" Rule

Old-style learning: memorize "120 rules of grammar" like — "Elect doesn't take 'as' after it", "Consider doesn't take 'as' after it", "Call doesn't take 'as' after it" — just memorized as a list of exceptions.

The REAL logic (now revealed): Verbs like elect, consider, call, make DIRECTLY take an Object Complement right after their object. Since the Object Complement ALREADY completes/describes the object, there's NO NEED for "as" — that's WHY "as" isn't used after these verbs!

This logic only becomes clear once your foundation/basics are strong — which is exactly the point of this redesigned Grammar Granth course.

Clarifications Given

"Boring" IS an Adjective. "Foolish" IS an Adjective. Different words in a sentence can have different Parts of Speech (Pronoun, Verb, Article, Noun, Adjective) — but TODAY's focus was on SENTENCE STRUCTURE (Subject/Verb/Object/Complement), not on Parts of Speech identification (that was the previous classes' topic).

"Object" vs "Predicate" — Predicate = the ENTIRE portion of the sentence after the Subject (includes the Verb AND everything else, including any Object). Object = specifically a Noun/Pronoun that the verb's action falls upon. These concepts overlap — Object can exist WITHIN the Predicate, and Subject Complement can contain an Object within it (in certain structures). The point isn't to draw rigid boundaries between these terms, but to understand WHAT each part of a sentence IS and HOW it functions.
