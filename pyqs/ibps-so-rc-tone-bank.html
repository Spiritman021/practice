✅ Master Tone Combos From Your RCs

Pragmatic & Conciliatory

Meaning: practical + conflict-reducing. Author rejects extreme/military answers and prefers realistic compromise/dialogue.

Trap beaten: Circumspect & Prescriptive. If the passage is more about practical compromise than deep caution, pragmatic wins.

Expository & Hopeful

Meaning: explains facts clearly + positive future outlook.

Look for meeting timelines, project scope, sector capabilities, and constructive follow-up signals.

Analytical & Concerned

Meaning: data diagnosis + measured worry.

CPI/WPI, currency, resource bottlenecks, unorganized labor risk. Not catastrophic because author stays evidence-based.

Critical & Analytical

Meaning: policy/system flaws + numbers-backed breakdown.

Most common pattern in economy editorials: flaws are criticized, but logic stays sober.

Analytical & Encouraging

Meaning: data-backed explanation + urging action/opportunity.

Seen in trade/FTA style passages where author highlights opportunity despite known limitations.

Critical & Cautionary

Meaning: points out flaws + warns of future danger.

Choose when the author says current policy friction can damage future competitiveness.

Critical & Prescriptive

Meaning: diagnoses flaws + gives clear remedies.

Look for “need to”, “should”, “must”, “requires”, “hybrid model”, “guidelines”.

Pragmatic & Critical / Critical & Pragmatic

Meaning: criticizes policy while staying practical and field-reality based.

Choose over dogmatic/defeatist when the passage gives a constructive phased solution.

🎯 Near-Miss Rules You Need

Critical ≠ Alarmist

If author uses data and logic to show flaws, pick critical. Pick alarmist only when there is exaggerated panic.

Concerned ≠ Catastrophic

Concerned means measured worry. Catastrophic means total disaster framing. IBPS options love this trap.

Prescriptive ≠ Pragmatic

Prescriptive gives solutions. Pragmatic is broader: practical constraints, compromises, ground reality.

Skeptical ≠ Critical

Skeptical doubts claims. Critical points out faults. If passage diagnoses actual flaws, critical is stronger.

Laudatory ≠ Encouraging

Laudatory is heavy praise. Encouraging can be forward-looking without worshipping the policy.

Dogmatic ≠ Assertive

Dogmatic refuses evidence. A firm, evidence-backed stance is not dogmatic.
